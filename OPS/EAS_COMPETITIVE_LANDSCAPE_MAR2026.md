## 6. LEGAL LIABILITIES & LAWSUITS

### Case #1: Air Canada Chatbot (Feb 2024)
- **What happened**: AI chatbot told customer Jake Moffatt he could get a bereavement fare refund retroactively. This was wrong. Air Canada argued the chatbot was a "separate entity" they weren't responsible for.
- **Ruling**: British Columbia tribunal held Air Canada liable. Ordered $812 CAD damages.
- **Key lesson**: Companies are 100% liable for what their AI chatbots tell customers. There is no "the bot said it, not us" defense.

### Case #2: Character.AI Teen Suicides (2025-2026)
- **What happened**: Multiple teens died by suicide after interactions with Character.AI chatbots. Families sued Character.AI and Google (as infrastructure provider).
- **Ruling**: Courts rejected the "service not product" defense. Found design defects (no age confirmation, no reporting mechanisms). Google held liable as "component part manufacturer."
- **Key lesson**: If you provide the infrastructure, you can be liable even if you didn't build the end product.

### Case #3: Chatbot Wiretap Class Actions (2021-2026)
- **Scale**: Grew from 2 lawsuits in 2021 to 30+ in 2025. Fastest-growing category of deployer-facing AI litigation.
- **What happened**: Companies like Old Navy and Peloton sued for chatbots recording/storing user conversations without adequate consent.
- **Peloton ruling**: Court agreed that third-party AI chatbot (Drift) "surreptitiously intercepted customer data."
- **Apple**: $95M settlement over Siri recording users without consent.
- **Key lesson**: Every chatbot/voice agent MUST have clear disclosure and affirmative consent for recording.

### Case #4: FTC Investigation (Sep 2025)
- FTC initiated formal inquiry into AI developers' measures to protect minors from chatbot harms.

### Regulatory Landscape (2026)
- **78 chatbot-related bills** filed across 27 states in early 2026
- **California SB 243** (effective Jan 1, 2026): Must tell users they're talking to AI, remind minors every 3 hours it's "not human"
- **Section 230 immunity**: Courts are actively debating whether it applies to AI chatbot outputs — no clear answer yet

### EAS Protection Requirements
1. **Clear AI disclosure** on every interaction (voice and text)
2. **Recording consent** — explicit opt-in before any conversation logging
3. **Accurate information guardrails** — never let AI make promises the business can't keep
4. **Terms of Service** — indemnification clause for client-facing AI agents
5. **E&O insurance** — errors and omissions coverage for consulting services
6. **No minor-facing deployments** without robust age verification and safeguards
7. **Two-party consent compliance** — mandatory in states like California, Illinois
8. **Client liability agreement** — make clear who owns the AI's outputs and who is responsible

## 9. EAS POSITIONING STRATEGY

### Competitive Gaps to Exploit

Based on this analysis, EAS should position into these UNOCCUPIED spaces:

#### GAP 1: "Voice AI First" Agency
No major agency leads with voice AI phone agents as their primary service. Everyone treats it as a feature. EAS can own this category.

**Positioning**: "We answer your phones with AI in 48 hours. Your business never misses a call again."

#### GAP 2: The $1K-5K/mo SMB Tier
Most agencies either target enterprise ($15K-50K/mo retainers) or sell one-off projects. There's a massive underserved market of SMBs who need ongoing AI operations management at $1K-5K/mo.

#### GAP 3: "Full Stack Business Automation" (not just workflows)
Current agencies automate individual workflows. Nobody packages a complete "AI business operating system" — phones + email + CRM + social + scheduling + invoicing.

#### GAP 4: Performance-Based Pricing for SMBs
Nobody offers "we only get paid when you save money" at the SMB level. This is a trust-builder for skeptical small business owners.

#### GAP 5: Speed to Value
Most agencies have 2-8 week implementation timelines. "Live in 48 hours" would be a category-defining differentiator.
