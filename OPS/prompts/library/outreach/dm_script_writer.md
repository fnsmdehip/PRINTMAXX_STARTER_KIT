# DM Script Writer

Write direct message scripts for social platforms. Follows PRINTMAXX copy-style rules.

---

## System prompt

```
You are a social media outreach specialist. You write DMs that start conversations without being spammy or salesy.

Approach:
- Platform-native tone (Twitter DMs are different from LinkedIn)
- Lead with genuine engagement, not pitch
- Short messages that invite response
- No copy-paste templates that feel robotic
- Build relationship before asking

Rules:
- Under 50 words for opener
- No immediate pitch in first message
- No AI vocabulary
- No fake compliments
- No "I have an opportunity for you"
- Be a human, not a salesperson
```

---

## User prompt template

```
Write DM scripts for [PLATFORM] to [PURPOSE].

About me:
- Who I am: [WHO_I_AM]
- What I want: [GOAL]
- What I offer: [VALUE - if relevant]

About recipients:
- Who they are: [RECIPIENTS]
- Why I'm reaching out: [REASON]
- What we have in common: [COMMON_GROUND]

Conversation flow:
- First message goal: [FIRST_GOAL - start conversation, not sell]
- Follow-up goal: [FOLLOW_UP_GOAL]
- End goal: [END_GOAL]

Tone: [TONE - casual, professional, friendly, peer-to-peer]
```

## Example output

**Input:**
```
Write DM scripts for Twitter to connect with potential podcast guests.

About me:
- Who I am: Host of a podcast for indie hackers
- What I want: Book podcast guests
- What I offer: Exposure to engaged audience of 5,000 listeners

About recipients:
- Who they are: Indie hackers with interesting products or stories
- Why I'm reaching out: They have a story worth sharing
- What we have in common: Building in public, indie hacker community

Conversation flow:
- First message goal: Start a genuine conversation
- Follow-up goal: Bring up the podcast naturally
- End goal: Book them as a guest

Tone: Casual, peer-to-peer
```

**Output:**

```
## Twitter DM script: Podcast guest outreach

---

### Message 1: Opener (conversation starter)

**Option A: Comment on their work**
```
Hey [Name], been following your journey with [Product]. The way you handled [specific thing] was smart. Quick question: what made you decide to [specific decision they made]?
```

**Option B: Relate to shared experience**
```
Just saw your post about [topic]. Going through something similar with my project right now. How did [specific question about their experience]?
```

**Option C: Genuine compliment + question**
```
Your thread on [topic] was really useful. The part about [specific point] made me rethink my approach. Have you found [related question]?
```

**Note:** Don't mention the podcast yet. Start a real conversation.

---

### Message 2: Continue conversation (after they reply)

```
That makes sense. I've been thinking about [related topic] a lot lately. Actually running a podcast for indie hackers where we dig into these kinds of decisions. Would love to hear more about your story sometime if you're interested.
```

Or if conversation is going well:

```
This is exactly the kind of stuff I love hearing about. Would you ever be open to chatting about this on a podcast? I think your approach to [thing] would resonate with a lot of builders.
```

---

### Message 3: Make the ask (if they're interested)

```
Awesome! The podcast is [Name], about [X] listeners who are building their own things. Usually 30-45 mins, pretty casual conversation.

If you're up for it, [link to schedule/calendar] or let me know what works for you.
```

---

### If they haven't replied (after 3-5 days)

**Soft follow-up:**
```
No worries if you're busy! Just wanted to bump this in case it got buried. Either way, keep shipping [Product].
```

**Note:** One follow-up max. Don't be annoying.

---

### If they say "not right now"

```
Totally get it. If things change, happy to chat anytime. Good luck with [their project]!
```

## Platform-specific tips

**Twitter:**
- Very casual tone
- Can reference their tweets
- Keep it short (character limit + attention span)
- Mutual followers help credibility

**LinkedIn:**
- Slightly more professional
- Reference mutual connections
- Can be slightly longer
- Avoid the "I noticed your profile" opener

**Instagram:**
- Very casual
- Reference their posts/stories
- Visual content creators expect DMs
- Keep it super short
```

## Variations

### Partnership DM
```
Write DMs to propose [PARTNERSHIP TYPE].

Structure:
1. Opener: Reference their work genuinely
2. Bridge: Common ground or mutual benefit
3. Ask: Soft proposal, easy to say yes or no

Keep first message under 30 words. Save details for after they respond.
```

### Collaboration DM
```
Write DMs to propose collaboration on [PROJECT].

Structure:
1. Why them specifically (not generic flattery)
2. What you're working on (brief)
3. What collaboration could look like
4. Low-commitment ask to discuss

Lead with what they get out of it.
```

### Networking DM
```
Write DMs to build relationship with [TYPE OF PERSON].

Structure:
1. Genuine connection point
2. Question that invites conversation
3. No ask in first message

Goal is relationship, not transaction.
```

### Feedback request DM
```
Write DMs to ask for feedback on [THING].

Structure:
1. Brief context on what you're building
2. Why their feedback specifically matters
3. Specific, bounded ask (not "what do you think?")
4. Make it easy to say no

Be specific about what feedback you want.
```

## DM conversation flow

**Stage 1: Opener (1 message)**
- Start with genuine engagement
- Ask a question they'd want to answer
- No pitch, no ask

**Stage 2: Conversation (2-4 messages)**
- Build rapport naturally
- Share relevant things about yourself
- Listen more than talk

**Stage 3: Bridge (1 message)**
- Natural transition to your purpose
- Make it relevant to the conversation
- Still don't pitch hard

**Stage 4: Ask (1 message)**
- Clear, specific request
- Easy to say yes or no
- Low commitment first step

**Stage 5: Handle response**
- Yes: Move to next step gracefully
- No: Accept gracefully, maintain relationship
- No response: One follow-up, then stop

---

## What to avoid

**Opener mistakes:**
- "Hey, I love your content!" (too generic)
- "I have an exciting opportunity" (salesy)
- "Can I pick your brain?" (low value for them)
- Long paragraph about yourself (they don't care yet)

**Follow-up mistakes:**
- Pitching immediately after opener
- Multiple messages without response
- "Just following up!" guilt trip
- Changing tone when they don't respond

**General mistakes:**
- Copy-paste templates obvious
- Fake personalization
- Immediate sales pitch
- Not respecting their time
