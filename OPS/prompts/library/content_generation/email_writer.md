# Email Writer

Write email sequences and individual emails. Follows PRINTMAXX copy-style rules.

---

## System prompt

```
You are an email copywriter for solopreneur newsletters. You write emails that get opened, read, and acted on.

Voice: Conversational. Like emailing a friend who asked for advice.

Rules:
- Subject line is specific, not clever
- First line earns the second line
- No "I hope this email finds you well"
- No em dashes
- No AI vocabulary
- One CTA per email
- PS line only if it adds value
- Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences)
- Write like you talk
```

---

## User prompt template

```
Write a [TYPE] email about [TOPIC].

Type: [TYPE - welcome, nurture, sales, announcement, story, tips]

Context:
- Sender: [SENDER - name/brand]
- Audience: [AUDIENCE]
- Goal: [GOAL - engagement, click, reply, purchase]

Email details:
- Subject line approach: [APPROACH - curiosity, benefit, urgency, personal]
- CTA: [CTA - what action should they take]
- Length: [LENGTH - short <200 words, medium 200-400, long 400+]

Key points:
[POINTS - what must be covered]
```

## Example output

**Input:**
```
Write a nurture email about time management.

Type: tips

Context:
- Sender: Alex, solopreneur coach
- Audience: Solo founders juggling too many tasks
- Goal: engagement and authority building

Email details:
- Subject line approach: curiosity
- CTA: reply with their biggest time sink
- Length: medium

Key points:
- Most time management advice doesn't work for solopreneurs
- The real problem is context switching
- One technique that helped: time blocking with buffer zones
```

**Output:**

```
Subject: The time management advice nobody gives solopreneurs

Hey,

Most time management advice assumes you have a boss.

"Prioritize your tasks." "Set boundaries." "Learn to say no."

All true. All useless when you're the CEO, developer, marketer, and support team of a one-person company.

The real problem isn't prioritization. It's context switching.

I tracked my time for a week and found something brutal:

I switched tasks 47 times per day.
Each switch cost about 15 minutes to get back into flow.
That's nearly 12 hours of lost productivity. Per week.

The fix that actually worked for me: time blocking with buffer zones.

Here's what I mean:

Instead of scheduling tasks back-to-back like this:
- 9am: Write newsletter
- 10am: Code feature
- 11am: Customer calls

I started adding 30-minute buffers:
- 9am: Write newsletter
- 10:30am: Code feature
- 12pm: Customer calls

Those buffer zones absorb the overrun and let my brain actually switch gears.

My context switches dropped from 47 to 12 per day.

What's your biggest time sink right now?

Hit reply and tell me. I read every response.

Alex

PS - If you haven't tried time tracking yet, I use Toggl. Free tier is enough. Even tracking for 3 days will show you where your time actually goes.
```

## Variations

### Welcome email
```
Write a welcome email for new subscribers.

Structure:
1. Thank them (1 sentence, not gushing)
2. What they'll get from being on the list
3. Quick intro of who you are (2-3 sentences)
4. Deliver the promised lead magnet (if applicable)
5. One question to get a reply

Keep it short. They just signed up, don't overwhelm them.
```

### Sales email
```
Write a sales email for [PRODUCT].

Structure:
1. Hook with a pain point or result
2. Agitate the problem briefly
3. Introduce the solution
4. Specific benefits (not features)
5. Social proof if available
6. Clear CTA with urgency if real
7. Risk reversal (guarantee, trial, etc.)

No fake urgency. No manipulative tactics. Just clear value proposition.
```

### Story email
```
Write a story-driven email about [TOPIC].

Structure:
1. Hook that pulls them into the story
2. Set the scene (quick, specific details)
3. The conflict or challenge
4. What you learned or did
5. The lesson that applies to them
6. CTA connecting story to action

Make it personal but relatable. Specific details make it real.
```

### Announcement email
```
Write an announcement email for [NEWS].

Structure:
1. Lead with the news
2. Why it matters to them
3. Key details (what, when, how)
4. What they need to do (if anything)
5. Link to more info

Don't bury the announcement in backstory. News first.
```

### Sequence structure
```
Write a [N]-email sequence for [GOAL].

For each email provide:
- Send timing (Day 1, Day 3, etc.)
- Subject line
- Main message
- CTA

Sequence types:
- Welcome: 3-5 emails introducing you and delivering value
- Nurture: 5-7 emails building trust and demonstrating expertise
- Launch: 4-6 emails building urgency toward a deadline
- Onboarding: 3-5 emails getting them to use a product
```

## Subject line formulas

**Curiosity:**
- "The [thing] nobody talks about"
- "Why I stopped [common practice]"
- "What happened when I [action]"

**Benefit:**
- "How to [result] in [timeframe]"
- "The fastest way to [achieve goal]"
- "[Number] [things] that [benefit]"

**Personal:**
- "A question for you"
- "Quick favor"
- "Can I ask you something?"

**Urgency (only if real):**
- "Last day for [thing]"
- "Closing [thing] tomorrow"
- "[Time] left to [action]"

## Quality checklist

- [ ] Subject line is specific, not clickbait
- [ ] First line earns attention
- [ ] No "I hope this email finds you well"
- [ ] No em dashes
- [ ] No banned AI vocabulary
- [ ] One clear CTA
- [ ] Short paragraphs (1-3 sentences)
- [ ] Sounds like a person, not a brand
- [ ] Would you open this email?
- [ ] Would you read it to the end?
