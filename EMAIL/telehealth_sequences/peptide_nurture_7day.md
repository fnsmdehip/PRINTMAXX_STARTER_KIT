## Day 3: How Peptides Actually Work (No Broscience)

**Subject A:** How peptides work at a molecular level (2-minute version)

**Preview text:** Short chains of amino acids that signal your body to do specific things. Here's the actual mechanism.

**Body:**

{{FIRST_NAME}},

A lot of peptide content online is either PhD-level biochemistry or Instagram broscience. Here's the middle ground.

**What is a peptide?**
A chain of amino acids. Shorter than a protein. Your body makes thousands of them naturally. They act as signaling molecules, telling your cells to do specific things.

**Why inject synthetic versions?**
Because your body's natural peptide production declines with age. At 50, you're producing significantly less growth hormone-releasing hormone (GHRH), less body protection compound, less NAD+. Adding them back is like restoring a signal that got weaker.

**How each one works (simplified):**

**BPC-157** binds to receptors that promote angiogenesis (new blood vessel formation). More blood flow to damaged tissue = faster repair. It also modulates nitric oxide and growth factor signaling. This is why it works for such a wide range of injuries. It's not targeting one tissue type. It's improving the repair process itself.

**Sermorelin** mimics your body's natural GHRH (growth hormone-releasing hormone). It tells your pituitary gland to release growth hormone. The key: your body still controls the feedback loop. It won't produce more GH than your pituitary can handle. This is fundamentally different from injecting exogenous HGH, which bypasses the feedback loop entirely.

**NAD+** is a coenzyme in every cell of your body. It's essential for converting food to energy (mitochondrial function), repairing DNA damage, activating sirtuins (longevity-associated enzymes), and regulating circadian rhythms. NAD+ levels drop about 50% between ages 40 and 60. Supplementing restores the substrate your cells need to function.

**CJC-1295/Ipamorelin** work together. CJC-1295 extends the half-life of growth hormone release. Ipamorelin triggers GH pulses that mimic natural patterns. Together, they increase GH output while maintaining the body's normal pulsatile secretion pattern. This matters because continuous GH elevation (like with exogenous HGH) has more side effects than pulsatile elevation.

**Why subcutaneous injection?**
Peptides are proteins. If you swallow them, stomach acid destroys them. IV works but requires a clinic visit. Subcutaneous injection (tiny insulin needle into belly fat) delivers the peptide into tissue where it absorbs over 30-60 minutes. Most peptides have 90%+ bioavailability this way.

**What about oral peptides?**
Companies sell oral BPC-157, oral NAD+ precursors (NR, NMN), etc. Oral bioavailability is significantly lower. Oral BPC-157 may help the gut directly (since it contacts the GI lining), but systemic effects (joints, tendons) are better achieved through injection. NAD+ precursors (NR, NMN) are the oral alternative to NAD+ IV/injection, with decent but not equivalent efficacy.

## Day 4: 503A vs 503B Pharmacies - Why Sourcing Matters

**Subject A:** Your peptide comes from one of two pharmacy types. One is safer.
**Subject B:** The pharmacy question nobody asks (and everyone should)

**Preview text:** 503A vs 503B. The letters matter more than the brand name.

**Body:**

{{FIRST_NAME}},

This is the most important email in this sequence. Not the flashiest, but the one that could save you from a bad experience.

Every compounded peptide comes from one of two pharmacy types. The difference matters.

**503A Pharmacies (state-regulated)**
- Licensed and inspected by the state board of pharmacy
- Can compound medications with a valid prescription for an individual patient
- Quality standards vary by state (some states are rigorous, others are not)
- Not required to follow FDA's current Good Manufacturing Practices (cGMP)
- No FDA inspection requirement
- Can be a single pharmacist in a small facility

**503B Pharmacies (FDA-registered outsourcing facilities)**
- Registered with the FDA as outsourcing facilities
- Subject to FDA inspection on the same schedule as drug manufacturers
- Must follow cGMP standards
- Can compound larger batches (not just individual prescriptions)
- Required to report adverse events to the FDA
- Required to do sterility and potency testing on each batch
- Typically larger, more sophisticated facilities

**Why this matters for peptides specifically:**

Peptides are injected. Anything you inject needs to be sterile. Period.

A 503B facility tests every batch for sterility, potency (is the stated dose actually in the vial?), endotoxins (bacterial contamination byproducts), and particulate matter. A 503A pharmacy may or may not do this depending on state requirements and their own protocols.

This isn't hypothetical risk. The FDA has issued warning letters to compounding pharmacies for peptides with incorrect potency (you think you're injecting 5mg but it's actually 2mg or 8mg), contamination, and sterility failures.

**How to check:**
1. Ask your clinic which pharmacy compounds their peptides
2. Search the pharmacy name on the FDA's 503B outsourcing facility list
3. Check for any FDA warning letters or inspection findings
4. If they won't tell you the pharmacy name, that's your answer

**The cost difference:**
503B peptides cost 10-30% more than 503A. That's the cost of FDA-level quality control. Worth it for something you're injecting into your body.

**Step 1: Sign up and fill out the intake form (10-15 minutes)**
Medical history, current medications, health goals, previous treatments. Standard stuff. Some clinics also ask about sleep quality, energy levels, recovery time, and cognitive function to establish a baseline.

**Step 2: Blood work (optional but recommended)**
Some clinics require it. Others recommend it. What they're looking at:

- Hormone panel (testosterone, estradiol for men; full female panel for women)
- Inflammatory markers (CRP, ESR)

At-home blood draw kits are the norm. A phlebotomist comes to your house, or you use a finger-prick kit that ships to the lab. Results in 3-5 business days.

**Step 3: Provider consultation (15-30 minutes, usually video call)**
A physician or PA reviews your history and labs, discusses your goals, and recommends a protocol. This is where you ask every question you have.

Questions to ask:
- Which pharmacy compounds your peptides? (503A or 503B?)
- What's the reconstitution and storage protocol?
- What side effects should I watch for?
- How often will we adjust the protocol?
- What does blood work monitoring look like?
- What happens if I want to stop?

**Step 4: Prescription and shipping (2-5 business days)**
Your protocol gets sent to the compounding pharmacy. They mix your peptides, test the batch (if 503B), and ship directly to you. Peptides arrive lyophilized (freeze-dried powder) with bacteriostatic water for reconstitution.

**Step 5: Your first injection**
Most programs include injection training (video or live walkthrough). Subcutaneous injections use a tiny insulin needle. Pinch belly fat, inject, done. 30 seconds.

Most people are nervous the first time and completely comfortable by the third injection.

**Step 6: Follow-up and adjustment (4-8 weeks in)**
Provider checks in. You report on symptoms, energy, recovery, sleep. Adjust dose or add/remove peptides based on response. Blood work again at 8-12 weeks.
