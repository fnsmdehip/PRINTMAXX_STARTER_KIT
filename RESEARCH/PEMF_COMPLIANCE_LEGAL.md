# PEMF Compliance & Legal Research

**Date:** 2026-02-07
**Status:** COMPLETE
**Purpose:** Full regulatory, compliance, and legal landscape for selling PEMF devices in the US (and internationally)

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

There are two viable paths to selling PEMF devices in the US:

1. **Wellness device path (cheapest, fastest):** Market as "general wellness device" with no disease claims. No 510(k) required. Use disclaimers. This is what Pulse PEMF, FluxHealth, OmniPEMF, and most consumer brands do. Estimated startup legal cost: $2,000-$8,000.

2. **FDA 510(k) clearance path (expensive, credible):** Get Class II medical device clearance. Takes 6-12 months, costs $50,000-$150,000+. This is what BEMER did. Allows medical claims and prescription use.

**Bottom line:** Start with Path 1 (wellness device). Only pursue Path 2 if revenue justifies the investment ($500K+/year).

## 1. FDA CLASSIFICATION

### What FDA Class Are PEMF Devices?

PEMF devices fall into multiple FDA classifications depending on intended use:

| Classification | Product Code | Description | 510(k) Required? |
|---------------|-------------|-------------|-------------------|
| **Class II** | MBQ | Diathermy, shortwave | Yes |
| **Class II** | NGX | Stimulator, muscle, powered (for muscle conditioning) | Yes |
| **Class II** | OQP | Bone growth stimulator | Yes |
| **Class I** | (various) | General wellness device (if no medical claims) | No |
| **Class III** | (specific) | Brain stimulator, depression treatment | Yes (PMA) |

**Key insight:** The classification depends entirely on INTENDED USE and MARKETING CLAIMS, not the device itself. The exact same PEMF coil can be Class I wellness or Class II medical depending on what you SAY it does.

### Is 510(k) Clearance Required?

**Only if you make medical claims.** If you market the device for treating, curing, diagnosing, or preventing any disease or medical condition, you need 510(k) clearance as a Class II device.

If you market it as a general wellness product with no disease claims, you can sell without 510(k) under FDA's enforcement discretion policy.

### Medical Device vs. Wellness Device

| Factor | Medical Device | Wellness Device |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|
| Claims | "Treats pain," "heals fractures," "reduces inflammation" | "Supports wellness," "promotes relaxation," "cellular exercise" |
| FDA clearance | Required (510(k) or PMA) | Not required (enforcement discretion) |
| Cost to market | $50,000-$150,000+ | $2,000-$8,000 |
| Time to market | 6-12 months | Immediate |
| Marketing restrictions | Can make cleared medical claims | Cannot reference any disease or condition |
| Prescription required? | Sometimes (depends on clearance) | No |
| Insurance reimbursement | Possible | No |

### FDA General Wellness Guidance (Revised January 2026)

On January 6, 2026, FDA issued revised guidance on "General Wellness: Policy for Low Risk Devices." Two criteria must be met:

1. **Intended only for general wellness use** - maintaining or encouraging a healthy lifestyle unrelated to diagnosis, cure, mitigation, prevention, or treatment of a disease or condition
2. **Presents low risk to user safety** - non-invasive, no significant harm potential

**What this means for PEMF:** FDA does NOT specifically address PEMF in the 2026 wellness guidance (it focuses on sensor-based wearables). However, the general wellness framework applies: if your PEMF device makes only wellness claims and is low-risk, FDA exercises enforcement discretion and does not regulate it as a medical device.

**Critical limitation:** The January 2026 guidance specifically expanded scope for sensor-based technologies (blood pressure, glucose, HRV). It did NOT expand scope for electromagnetic therapy devices. PEMF sellers still rely on the original general wellness framework.

- **1979:** FDA first approved PEMF as safe and effective for healing nonunion bone fractures
- **1998:** FDA cleared PEMF for urinary incontinence and muscle stimulation

- **2023:** BEMER received 510(k) clearance (K231368)

## 2. FCC REQUIREMENTS

### Do PEMF Devices Need FCC Certification?

**Yes.** PEMF devices that emit electromagnetic energy fall under FCC regulation. They are classified as **Industrial, Scientific, and Medical (ISM) equipment** under **FCC Part 18** (not Part 15).

### Part 15 vs. Part 18

| Regulation | Applies To | PEMF Relevant? |
|-----------|-----------|----------------|
| **Part 15** | Unintentional radiators, low-power intentional radiators (WiFi, Bluetooth) | Only for incidental emissions from control electronics |
| **Part 18** | ISM equipment that intentionally uses RF energy for non-communication purposes | **YES - primary regulation for PEMF devices** |

PEMF devices use RF energy intentionally for therapeutic/wellness purposes (not communication), which puts them squarely in Part 18 ISM territory.

### FCC Part 18 Requirements for Consumer PEMF Devices

1. **FCC Certification required** for consumer ISM equipment before marketing in the US
2. **EMC/EMI testing** at an accredited lab to verify emissions comply with 47 CFR 18.307
3. **Conducted emission limits** must meet Class B CISPR 32 limits (same as consumer electronics)
4. **Radiated emission limits** per 47 CFR 18.305
5. **Equipment must be labeled** with FCC ID
6. **Equipment Authorization** through a Telecommunications Certification Body (TCB)

### Testing Process

1. Submit device to accredited EMC testing lab
2. Lab tests for conducted and radiated emissions per Part 18 standards
3. If compliant, submit application with technical report to TCB
4. TCB reviews and issues FCC Grant of Equipment Authorization + FCC ID
5. Device can then be legally marketed

### Estimated FCC Compliance Cost

| Item | Cost Range |
|------|-----------|
| EMC pre-compliance testing | $2,000-$5,000 |
| Full EMC compliance testing (Part 18) | $5,000-$15,000 |
| TCB certification application | $1,000-$3,000 |
| **Total FCC compliance** | **$8,000-$23,000** |

### How Existing Companies Handle FCC

Most consumer PEMF companies either:
- Get full FCC Part 18 certification (larger companies like BEMER)
- Operate at power levels low enough to potentially fall under Part 15 unintentional radiator rules
- Source from OEMs/manufacturers who already have FCC certification (white-label approach)

**White-label shortcut:** If you source devices from an existing manufacturer with FCC certification, you may not need separate FCC testing. The OEM's certification covers the device. This is the cheapest path.

## 3. MARKETING CLAIMS

### What You CAN Legally Say (Without FDA Clearance)

**Structure/function claims (SAFE):**
- "Supports cellular wellness"
- "Promotes relaxation"
- "Cellular exercise"
- "Supports the body's natural recovery processes"
- "Enhances general well-being"
- "Supports healthy circulation"
- "Promotes a sense of calm and balance"
- "Optimizes the body's natural self-healing functions"

**General wellness claims (SAFE):**
- "For wellness purposes"
- "Promotes healthy lifestyle"
- "Supports overall wellness"
- "Helps you feel your best"

### What You CANNOT Say (Without FDA Clearance)

**Disease claims (ILLEGAL without 510(k)):**
- "Treats pain" / "Reduces pain"
- "Heals fractures" / "Promotes bone healing"
- "Reduces inflammation"
- "Treats arthritis" / "Helps with osteoporosis"
- "Cures depression" / "Treats anxiety"
- "Improves blood circulation" (if referencing a medical condition)
- "Treats insomnia"
- Any reference to a specific disease, condition, or diagnosis

**Trigger words that make claims medical:**
- Treat, cure, prevent, diagnose, mitigate
- Any specific disease name (cancer, diabetes, arthritis, etc.)
- Medical symptoms (inflammation, edema, chronic pain)

### FTC Substantiation Requirements

The FTC applies the SAME standard to ALL health claims regardless of whether FDA considers the product a medical device or wellness device:

- **Competent and reliable scientific evidence** required for ANY health claim
- This means **randomized, controlled human clinical trials** (not anecdotes, not in-vitro studies)
- Testimonials do NOT constitute substantiation
- "Results may vary" does NOT protect unsubstantiated claims
- FTC makes NO distinction between structure/function claims and disease claims for substantiation purposes

**This is critical:** Even "safe" wellness claims like "supports cellular health" technically need scientific backing under FTC rules. In practice, FTC enforcement focuses on specific disease claims, but the legal standard applies to all health claims.

### How Major Brands Word Their Marketing

**Pulse PEMF:**
> "Pulse PEMF products are wellness devices, not medical devices. Rather than addressing or diagnosing medical issues, PEMF stimulates and exercises the cells, optimizing wellness at the cellular level."
> "Products featured on this website have not been evaluated by the FDA and are not intended for use as medical devices. Neither PEMF systems nor PEMF accessories diagnose, treat or cure any diseases, illnesses, medical conditions, or injuries."

**FlexPulse:**
> "FlexPulse products have not been evaluated by the FDA. The FlexPulse is intended for the beneficial effect as set forth in the directions and instruction literature. These products do not claim to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any medical condition. Always consult your medical doctor regarding any health concerns."

**OmniPEMF (NeoRhythm):**
> "NeoRhythm has not been evaluated by the FDA. These products do not claim to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent any medical conditions. Always consult your medical doctor regarding any health concerns."
> "NeoRhythm and NeoRhythm Pad are not a substitute for professional health care or medical consultation. Use of NeoRhythm devices is done at your own risk and volition."

**BEMER (has 510(k) clearance):**
> Markets as "FDA Class II cleared consumer medical device" that "sends a low-intensity pulsed electromagnetic field to the body to safely stimulate healthy muscles which temporarily enhances local blood flow."
> Includes specific contraindications and safety information as required by FDA clearance.

### FTC Enforcement Example: PEMF Wellness Technology

In June 2020, the FTC sent warning letters to multiple PEMF companies (including PEMF Wellness Technology/pemf8000.com and BioBalance PEMF) for making unsubstantiated claims that PEMF devices could treat or prevent COVID-19. The FTC required corrective action within 48 hours.

**Lesson:** FTC actively monitors and enforces against PEMF companies making unsupported disease claims. Stick to wellness language.

## 4. LIABILITY PROTECTION

### Business Entity Structure

**LLC is the standard** for PEMF device sellers. Benefits:
- Personal liability protection (company debts/lawsuits don't reach personal assets)
- Pass-through taxation (no double taxation)
- Flexible management structure
- Relatively cheap to set up ($50-$500 depending on state)

**Recommended structure:**
- Form an LLC in a business-friendly state (Wyoming, Delaware, or your home state)
- Maintain corporate formalities (separate bank account, operating agreement, meeting minutes)
- Consider a holding company LLC that owns an operating LLC for additional protection

**Corp (C-Corp or S-Corp)** is overkill for initial launch but may make sense if:
- You plan to raise investor capital
- You want to issue stock options
- Revenue exceeds $80K+ (S-Corp for tax savings on self-employment tax)

### Product Liability Insurance

| Coverage Type | Estimated Annual Cost | Notes |
|--------------|----------------------|-------|
| General liability + product liability | $2,200-$5,000/year | Standard for small consumer product companies |
| Medical device-specific coverage | $3,000-$10,000/year | Higher due to health product risk category |
| Professional liability (if offering services) | $1,000-$3,000/year | If you also provide PEMF therapy sessions |
| Umbrella policy | $1,000-$2,000/year | Additional coverage above base policies |

**Key challenges for PEMF insurance:**
- Many insurers categorize PEMF as wellness (lower premiums) rather than medical (higher premiums)
- UL Safety Certification and ISO 13485 can significantly reduce premiums
- One PEMF company (Pulse Centers) claims to be the only US company with UL Safety Certification, suggesting it is a differentiator for insurance purposes
- If white-labeling from an established manufacturer, their product liability coverage may extend to your products (verify with manufacturer)

**Estimated total insurance cost:** $3,000-$8,000/year for a small PEMF startup

### Required Warnings and Terms of Service

**Mandatory disclaimer elements (based on industry standard):**

```
DISCLAIMER: [Your Brand] products have not been evaluated by the FDA.
These products are not intended to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent
any disease or medical condition. [Your Brand] devices are general
wellness products. Always consult your licensed healthcare provider
before using any wellness device, especially if you have a medical
condition or are taking medication.
```

**Mandatory contraindication warnings:**

```
CONTRAINDICATIONS - DO NOT USE IF YOU HAVE:
- Active implanted electrical devices (pacemakers, defibrillators,
  cochlear implants, brain stimulators)
- Implanted medication pumps (insulin pumps, intrathecal pumps)
  [ABSOLUTE CONTRAINDICATION]
- Magnetizable metal implants or prostheses

USE WITH CAUTION / CONSULT PHYSICIAN:
- Pregnancy (safety not established)
- Epilepsy or seizure disorders
- Active bleeding
- Organ transplant recipients on immune suppression
- Graves' disease
- Breast implants (avoid high-intensity over implants)
```

**Terms of Service must include:**
- Assumption of risk clause
- Limitation of liability
- Indemnification clause
- No medical advice disclaimer
- Testimonial disclaimer ("individual experiences, not guaranteed results")
- Governing law and dispute resolution
- Return/refund policy

### What If Someone Misuses the Device?

1. **Clear instructions for use** included with every device
2. **Contraindication warnings** prominently displayed on packaging, manual, and website
3. **Terms of service** include assumption of risk and limitation of liability
4. **Product liability insurance** covers legal defense costs
5. **LLC structure** protects personal assets
6. **Document everything** - keep records of warnings provided, customer communications, complaint handling

**BEMER recall precedent:** BEMER had an FDA Class 2 recall specifically because they initially failed to list contraindications (particularly regarding medication pumps and pacemakers). They updated their safety labeling. This shows the FDA takes missing contraindication warnings seriously even for cleared devices.

## 6. BIG BRAND COMPLIANCE ANALYSIS

### BEMER - The Gold Standard (510(k) Path)

**What BEMER did:**
- Filed 510(k) premarket notification (K151834, first cleared 2015)
- Classified as **product code NGX** ("Stimulator, Muscle, Powered, For Muscle Conditioning")
- Used existing cleared "powered muscle stimulator" devices as predicates
- Demonstrated substantial equivalence to legally marketed predicate devices
- Got cleared for OTC (over-the-counter) use without prescription
- Filed additional 510(k)s for product line extensions (K210174 in 2021, K231368 in 2023)

**What BEMER can claim (because of 510(k)):**
- "FDA Class II cleared consumer medical device"
- "Sends a low-intensity pulsed electromagnetic field to safely stimulate healthy muscles"
- "Temporarily enhances local blood flow in healthy leg muscles during and after use"
- "Temporarily enhances local blood flow in healthy calf muscles during use"

**What BEMER had to do for compliance:**
- Full 510(k) submission with testing data
- Quality Management System (likely ISO 13485)
- Post-market surveillance
- Adverse event reporting (MAUDE database)
- Labeling with contraindications (learned the hard way via recall)
- FDA establishment registration and device listing
- Annual registration fees

**BEMER recall lesson:** In 2019, BEMER had a Class 2 FDA recall because they failed to include contraindication warnings about medication pumps and pacemakers. They had to notify all affected customers and update safety labeling. This shows even cleared companies face enforcement for inadequate warnings.

**Estimated cost for BEMER's regulatory path:** $100,000-$300,000+ including testing, consulting, legal, and ongoing compliance.

### Pulse PEMF - The Wellness Path

**What Pulse PEMF did:**
- Deliberately chose NOT to seek FDA clearance
- Markets exclusively as "non-medical general wellness device"
- Uses "cellular exercise" language (never "treatment" or "therapy" in medical context)
- Clear disclaimer on every page
- Does not reference any disease or medical condition
- Sells through direct sales, distributors, and practitioners

**Pulse PEMF's stated position:**
> "Pulse PEMF products are wellness devices, not medical devices. Rather than addressing or diagnosing medical issues, PEMF stimulates and exercises the cells, optimizing wellness at the cellular level. For this reason, Pulse has not sought any evaluations or clearances from the FDA."

**Risk level:** Moderate. If FTC or FDA decides their marketing implies medical claims (even indirectly), they could face enforcement. But this model has been operating for years without action.

### FluxHealth/Micro-Pulse - The Research/OEM Path

- Positions as technology company and OEM
- Sells to consumers under multiple brand names
- Emphasizes research applications
- Uses "ICES" (Inductively Coupled Electrical Stimulation) branding to differentiate from generic PEMF

- No FDA clearance sought

### OmniPEMF (NeoRhythm) - The Consumer Tech Path

- Markets as wearable wellness technology

## 7. INTERNATIONAL CONSIDERATIONS

### EU Sales (CE Marking / EU MDR)

**Requirements:**
- CE marking mandatory for medical devices sold in the EU
- Under EU MDR 2017/745, PEMF devices likely classified as Class IIa
- Need a Notified Body assessment and certification
- Must appoint an Authorized Representative in the EU
- Register in EUDAMED (European Database on Medical Devices)
- Quality Management System per ISO 13485

**Cost estimate for EU MDR compliance:**
| Item | Cost |
|------|------|
| ISO 13485 QMS implementation | $15,000-$40,000 |
| CE Technical Documentation | $10,000-$30,000 |
| Notified Body assessment | $10,000-$25,000 |
| EU Authorized Representative | $3,000-$10,000/yr |
| **Total** | **$38,000-$105,000** |

**If selling as wellness device in EU:** Requirements may be less stringent, but the EU is generally stricter than the US on health product claims. Consult EU regulatory specialist.

### Canada (Health Canada)

- Health Canada classifies medical devices into 4 classes
- PEMF devices are typically Class 2 in Canada (e.g., SEQEX HC/FAM/MED are Health Canada Class 2 licensed)
- Class 2 devices require a Medical Device License from Health Canada
- Manufacturer must have a Quality Management System (ISO 13485)
- If selling as wellness device with no medical claims, requirements may differ

**BEMER's approach:** Holds both FDA Class II clearance AND Health Canada registration.

### Can You Sell Internationally from the US?

**Yes, but:**
- Each country has its own medical device regulations
- EU requires CE marking
- Canada requires Health Canada license for medical devices
- UK requires UKCA marking post-Brexit
- Australia requires TGA registration
- You are responsible for compliance in each market you sell into
- Using distributors in each country can shift some compliance burden

**Cheapest international approach:**
1. Start US-only (wellness device path)
2. Use international distributors who handle local regulatory compliance
3. Pursue CE marking / Health Canada only when revenue justifies the investment

## RISK ASSESSMENT

### Legal Risk Matrix

| Risk | Likelihood | Impact | Mitigation |
|------|-----------|--------|------------|
| FTC enforcement for health claims | MEDIUM | HIGH | Strict wellness-only language, attorney review |
| FDA warning letter | LOW-MEDIUM | MEDIUM | No disease claims, proper disclaimers |
| Product liability lawsuit | LOW | HIGH | Insurance, LLC, contraindication warnings |
| FCC non-compliance | LOW | MEDIUM | Use FCC-certified OEM or get Part 18 testing |
| State AG consumer protection action | LOW | MEDIUM | FTC compliance covers most state requirements |
| Customer injury from misuse | LOW | HIGH | Clear warnings, contraindications, ToS |

### Critical Dos and Don'ts

**DO:**
- Use an LLC
- Get product liability insurance
- Have an attorney review all marketing claims and disclaimers
- Include contraindication warnings everywhere
- Source from FCC-certified manufacturers
- Keep documentation of all safety testing and warnings
- Respond promptly to any adverse event reports

**DON'T:**
- Make ANY disease claims without 510(k) clearance
- Use words like "treat," "cure," "prevent," "diagnose" in relation to any medical condition
- Ignore FCC requirements (even if competitors seem to)
- Skip product liability insurance
- Sell without contraindication warnings
- Make COVID-19, cancer, or other serious disease claims (FTC actively monitors these)
- Use customer testimonials that include disease claims without disclosure
- Ignore FDA warning letters or FTC inquiries
