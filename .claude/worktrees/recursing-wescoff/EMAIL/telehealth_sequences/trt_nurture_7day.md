## Day 1: Testosterone by Age - Where You Probably Fall

**Subject A:** Here's where your testosterone should be (and probably isn't)
**Subject B:** The testosterone chart your doctor doesn't show you

**Preview text:** "Normal" is a 300-point range. That's the problem.

**Body:**

{{FIRST_NAME}},

When you get bloodwork done, the lab report says your testosterone is "normal" if it falls between roughly 264-916 ng/dL. That's a 652-point range. It's almost useless as a diagnostic tool.

Here's what actually matters: where do you fall relative to your age?

**Average total testosterone by age (approximate):**
- 20-29: 600-900 ng/dL
- 30-39: 500-750 ng/dL
- 40-49: 450-650 ng/dL
- 50-59: 400-550 ng/dL
- 60-69: 350-500 ng/dL
- 70+: 300-450 ng/dL

These are averages. Individual variation is huge. But here's the problem: if you're 35 with a level of 320 ng/dL, your doctor will say "normal." Because technically, it is. It's within the reference range.

But it's the testosterone level of a 75-year-old man. That distinction matters.

**What you should actually look at:**
1. Total testosterone (the big number)
2. Free testosterone (the usable amount, usually 2-3% of total)
3. SHBG (sex hormone binding globulin, affects how much T your body can actually use)
4. Estradiol (estrogen levels, critical for balance)

Most primary care doctors test total T only. That gives you maybe 40% of the picture. You need all four.

The good online TRT clinics test all of these before prescribing anything. The bad ones ask you to fill out a symptom questionnaire and send a prescription.

## Day 3: Injection vs Gel vs Pellets - Which Is Right for You

**Subject A:** Injections, gels, or pellets? Here's the honest breakdown.
**Subject B:** The TRT delivery method nobody talks about (and why)

**Preview text:** Each has tradeoffs your clinic might not mention. Here's the full picture.

**Body:**

{{FIRST_NAME}},

Three main ways to get testosterone into your body. Each has real tradeoffs.

**Injections (testosterone cypionate or enanthate)**
- Frequency: Once or twice per week (self-administered, subcutaneous or intramuscular)
- Cost: Cheapest option. $30-80/month for the medication itself.
- Pros: Most stable levels if injecting 2x/week. Precise dosing. Decades of clinical data.
- Cons: You're injecting yourself. Some guys never get comfortable with it. Slight peaks and valleys.
- Who it's for: Men who want maximum control and the best-studied method.

**Topical gel/cream (AndroGel, compounded creams)**
- Frequency: Daily application
- Cost: $200-500/month for brand name. $50-150 for compounded.
- Pros: No needles. Simple daily routine.
- Cons: Transfer risk (skin contact with partner or kids before it dries). Inconsistent absorption. Some men are "poor absorbers" and never reach target levels no matter the dose.
- Who it's for: Men who absolutely won't inject and accept the absorption variability.

**Pellets (Testopel)**
- Frequency: Every 3-4 months (inserted under skin by a provider)
- Cost: $500-1,000 per insertion
- Pros: Set it and forget it. Most consistent levels over time.
- Cons: Requires an in-office procedure. Can't adjust dose easily. If you don't respond well, you're stuck for 3-4 months. Infection risk at insertion site.
- Who it's for: Men who want zero daily/weekly maintenance and have a provider who does insertions.

**What most online clinics prescribe:** Injections. There's a reason. Cypionate injections are the gold standard for cost, control, and evidence. Most protocols start at 100-200mg/week, split into two doses.

**The thing nobody mentions:** Your protocol isn't just testosterone. A good clinic also manages:
- HCG (to maintain fertility and testicular size, if desired)
- Anastrozole (to manage estrogen conversion, if needed based on labs)
- DHEA (depending on your labs)

If a clinic hands you a vial of testosterone and nothing else, they're not managing your endocrine system. They're dispensing a drug.

## Day 4: The Bloodwork You NEED Before Starting TRT

**Subject A:** Don't start TRT without these 7 blood tests
**Subject B:** The lab panel that separates real TRT clinics from prescription mills

**Preview text:** If your clinic only checks total testosterone, walk away.

**Body:**

{{FIRST_NAME}},

The bloodwork you get BEFORE starting TRT is more important than the prescription itself. It establishes your baseline and tells you whether TRT is actually the right move.

Here's what a proper pre-TRT panel includes:

**The essentials (non-negotiable):**
1. **Total testosterone** - The headline number. Draw between 7-10 AM when levels peak.
2. **Free testosterone** - What your body can actually use. More diagnostic than total T alone.
3. **SHBG** - Determines how much of your total T is bioavailable.
4. **Estradiol (sensitive assay)** - Baseline estrogen. Critical for managing aromatization on TRT.
5. **Complete blood count (CBC)** - Testosterone increases red blood cell production. Need a baseline to monitor.
6. **Comprehensive metabolic panel (CMP)** - Liver and kidney function. Baseline health markers.
7. **Lipid panel** - Testosterone affects cholesterol. Know where you start.

**The ones good clinics also include:**
- LH and FSH (pituitary hormones, tells you WHY your T is low, not just that it is)
- Thyroid panel (TSH, free T3, free T4 - thyroid issues mimic low T symptoms)
- PSA (prostate baseline, especially if you're 40+)
- Prolactin (rules out pituitary tumor, rare but important)
- DHEA-S (adrenal function)
- Insulin/glucose (metabolic health affects testosterone production)

**Red flags in a TRT clinic's process:**
- They only test total testosterone
- They don't require blood work at all (symptom questionnaire only)
- They don't retest after 6-8 weeks on therapy
- They don't test estradiol (missing this leads to the worst TRT side effects)
- Blood draws at random times of day (levels vary by 30%+ throughout the day)

The point of all this: you should know EXACTLY what your hormonal baseline looks like before adding external testosterone. Otherwise you're flying blind, and so is your provider.

{{FIRST_NAME}},

After reviewing 9 online TRT programs, I found that the medication is basically the same everywhere. Testosterone cypionate. 200mg/mL. 10mL vial. Comes from a licensed pharmacy.

The difference is everything around the medication. Here are 3 things that separate a real clinic from a prescription mill:

**1. Ongoing blood work included in the price (not "recommended" or "available for purchase")**

You need blood work at 6-8 weeks, then every 90 days for the first year, then every 6 months. This monitors your hematocrit (red blood cells, the main safety concern), estradiol, free T, and PSA.

Some clinics include this. Others charge $200-400 per panel on top of your subscription. Ask before you sign up.

**2. Protocol adjustments based on labs, not a static prescription**

Your starting dose is a guess. An educated guess, but still a guess. It takes 2-3 blood draws to dial in the right dose for your body. Some men need 100mg/week. Others need 200mg. Same weight, same age, different metabolism.

Clinics that write you 200mg/week and never adjust aren't managing your health. They're running a subscription drug business.

Look for: documented protocol reviews after every blood draw, dose changes when labs indicate, and a provider who explains why they're adjusting (or not).

**3. Ancillary medications managed, not ignored**

TRT isn't just testosterone. Depending on your labs:
- HCG may be needed to maintain fertility and testicular function
- An aromatase inhibitor (anastrozole) may be needed if estradiol gets too high
- DHEA supplementation may help if your adrenals are underperforming

Clinics that prescribe testosterone alone and nothing else are leaving 40% of the protocol on the table. Your body converts some testosterone to estrogen (aromatization). If nobody's watching that ratio, you can end up with high estrogen symptoms: water retention, mood swings, gynecomastia. These are avoidable.

**Bottom line:** The best clinics treat your endocrine system. The worst ones sell you a vial.
