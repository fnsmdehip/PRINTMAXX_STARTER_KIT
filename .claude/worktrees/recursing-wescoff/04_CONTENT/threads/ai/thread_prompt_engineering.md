# Thread: Prompt Engineering Tricks Nobody Talks About

## Tweet 1
I've written 10,000+ AI prompts in the last 18 months.

Most prompt guides are wrong about what actually works.

Here are 6 tricks that made my outputs 3x better:

## Tweet 2
Trick 1: Show, don't tell

Bad: "Write in a casual tone"
Good: Paste 3 examples of your actual writing style

AI pattern-matches better than it interprets abstract instructions.

I keep a swipe file of 50 tweet examples I paste into prompts.

## Tweet 3
Trick 2: Constrain harder

Bad: "Write a blog post"
Good: "Write 800 words. Use 6 H2 headings. Max 3 sentences per paragraph."

Vague prompts get vague outputs. Specific constraints force quality.

I add word counts, sentence limits, and structure requirements to every prompt.

## Tweet 4
Trick 3: Multi-step beats one-shot

Bad: "Analyze this data and write a report"
Good:
Step 1: "List 10 insights from this data"
Step 2: "Rank them by importance"
Step 3: "Write 200 words on the top 3"

Breaking complex tasks into steps gets better results 80% of the time.

## Tweet 5
Trick 4: Negative examples work

Don't just show what you want. Show what you DON'T want.

"Don't use these words: leverage, synergy, robust"
"Don't start with questions"
"Don't use em dashes"

AI learns faster from "avoid this" than from "be like this."

## Tweet 6
Trick 5: Ask for self-critique

After the AI generates something, ask:
"What are 3 weaknesses in this output?"

Then: "Rewrite fixing those weaknesses."

Gets you to version 3 quality without manually editing. I do this for every blog post draft.

## Tweet 7
Trick 6: Version control your prompts

I keep a Notion doc with 30 prompts I use weekly.

Every time I improve one, I save it as v2, v3, etc.

My "write a tweet" prompt is on v14 now. It's 10x better than v1.

Treat prompts like code. Iterate and save the good versions.
