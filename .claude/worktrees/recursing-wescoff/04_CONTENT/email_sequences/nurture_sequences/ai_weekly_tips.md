## Email 1: The prompt template

**Send timing:** Tuesday, 10am
**Week:** 1 of nurture cycle

### Subject line
The prompt structure that works every time

**A/B variants:**
- A: Copy this prompt format
- B: Why your prompts get bad results

### Preview text
Role + Context + Task + Format

### Body

Most people write prompts like this:

"Write me a blog post about AI tools."

That's too vague. You'll get generic output.

Here's the structure that works:

**Role:** You are a [specific expert type]
**Context:** I'm working on [specific situation]
**Task:** Create [specific deliverable]
**Format:** Structure it as [specific format]

Example:

"You are a technical writer who specializes in explaining complex software to non-technical business owners.

I'm creating a comparison guide for my newsletter about automation tools.

Write a 200-word explanation of why n8n is better than Zapier for solopreneurs.

Format: 3 paragraphs. First paragraph: the main difference. Second: specific use case. Third: cost comparison."

Same topic, much better output.

Try it today: Take a prompt you've used recently. Rewrite it with all four components.

## Email 2: The automation audit

**Send timing:** Tuesday, 10am
**Week:** 2 of nurture cycle

### Subject line
5 questions to audit your AI setup

**A/B variants:**
- A: Is your AI stack working?
- B: Quick audit: 5 minutes

### Preview text
Most people fail question 3

### Body

Answer these 5 questions honestly:

1. How many AI tools do you pay for monthly?
2. Which ones did you use in the last 7 days?
3. Can you describe the specific workflow each tool serves?
4. How much time does your AI stack save you per week?
5. When was the last time you cancelled an AI subscription?

If you struggled with questions 3 or 4, you have tools without workflows.

Tools without workflows cost money without producing output.

Here's a quick fix:

Open your subscriptions list. For each AI tool, write one sentence describing exactly when and how you use it.

If you can't write that sentence, you probably don't need that tool.

I cut 8 tools doing this exercise. Saved $200/month.

## Email 3: Batch vs real-time

**Send timing:** Tuesday, 10am
**Week:** 3 of nurture cycle

### Subject line
Stop using AI in real-time

**A/B variants:**
- A: The batch processing shift
- B: Why context-switching kills AI productivity

### Preview text
This doubled my output

### Body

Most people use AI tools throughout the day. A prompt here, a prompt there.

This feels efficient but isn't.

Every time you open an AI tool:
- You context-switch from your main work
- You craft a prompt from scratch
- You wait for the response
- You edit the output
- You context-switch back

Each interruption costs 10-15 minutes of focus.

Here's what works better: batch processing.

Once per week (I do Mondays):
1. List everything I need AI help with
2. Prepare all prompts in advance
3. Run them all in one session
4. Process all outputs together

Time spent: 1-2 hours
Interruptions during the week: zero

The same work, consolidated into one focused session.

Try it next Monday. Write down every AI task you'd normally do throughout the week. Do them all at once.

**Week:** 4 of nurture cycle

Measure your AI ROI

**A/B variants:**
- A: Is AI actually saving you time?
- B: The numbers most people avoid

Simple calculation inside

### Body

Let's do some math.

**What you're paying:**
Add up your monthly AI subscriptions. Include ChatGPT, Claude, any writing tools, automation tools, etc.

My total: $45/month

**What you're getting:**
Estimate hours saved per week from AI tools. Be honest.

My estimate: 5 hours/week (20 hours/month)

**The ROI:**
$45 / 20 hours = $2.25 per hour saved

If your hourly value is above $2.25, the tools are worth it.

**Most people's problem:**

They pay $200/month but only save 5 hours.
$200 / 5 = $40 per hour saved.

Unless you value your time at $40+/hour, you're overpaying.

**The fix:**
Either reduce tools (lower the cost) or improve workflows (increase the output).
