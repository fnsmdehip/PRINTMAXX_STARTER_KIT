# Emergency Fund & Cash Reserve Guidelines

## Philosophy

- Cash is oxygen for a business
- Reserves enable boldness, not timidity
- Plan for bad months, not just good ones
- Separate business and personal reserves

---

## Reserve Targets by Phase

## Reserve Building Strategy

### Priority Order

1. **Survival reserve** - 1 month expenses (first)
2. **Stability reserve** - 3 months expenses (second)
3. **Opportunity reserve** - 6 months expenses (third)
4. **Growth capital** - Beyond reserves (fourth)

### Funding the Reserve

| Revenue Level | % to Reserve | Monthly Contribution |
|---------------|--------------|---------------------|
| $0-1K | 20% | $0-200 |
| $1-3K | 15% | $150-450 |
| $3-5K | 12% | $360-600 |
| $5-10K | 10% | $500-1,000 |
| $10K+ | 8% | $800+ |

### Where to Keep It

| Option | Pros | Cons | Best For |
|--------|------|------|----------|
| High-yield savings | 4-5% APY, liquid | Lower returns | Primary reserve |
| Money market | 4-5% APY, liquid | Minimum balance | Larger reserves |
| Short-term CDs | Higher rates | Less liquid | Portion of reserve |
| Treasury bills | Safe, competitive | Complexity | Advanced |

**Recommendation:** High-yield savings (Mercury, Bluevine, Novo) for simplicity

## What Qualifies as Emergency

### Use Reserve For

**Technology failures:**
- Server crash requiring emergency hosting
- Critical tool subscription lapse
- Data recovery needs

**Revenue disruptions:**
- Platform bans or algorithm changes
- Payment processor issues
- Major client loss

**Unexpected expenses:**
- Legal/compliance issues
- Tax surprises
- Health issues affecting work

**Business protection:**
- Trademark defense
- Contract disputes
- Refund obligations

### Do NOT Use Reserve For

- Speculative investments
- "Nice to have" tools
- Scaling before profitable
- Hiring without clear ROI
- Marketing experiments
- Course/training purchases
- Conference attendance
- Equipment upgrades (unless broken)

## Reserve Depletion Protocol

### Level 1: Minor Dip (10-25% of reserve)

**Trigger:** Unexpected expense or short revenue dip
**Action:**
- Identify cause
- Create plan to rebuild
- Increase reserve contribution temporarily
- No major changes needed

### Level 2: Moderate Depletion (25-50% of reserve)

**Trigger:** Significant unexpected expense or revenue problem
**Action:**
- Freeze non-essential spending
- Review all subscriptions
- Reduce contractor hours if possible
- Pause marketing experiments
- Focus on revenue-generating activities
- Weekly cash flow review

### Level 3: Severe Depletion (50-75% of reserve)

**Trigger:** Major crisis or sustained revenue loss
**Action:**
- Cut all non-essential expenses immediately
- Reduce contractor team to minimum
- Pause all paid marketing
- Consider temporary revenue sources (services, consulting)
- Daily cash monitoring
- Create 30-60-90 day recovery plan

### Level 4: Critical (>75% depleted)

**Trigger:** Business survival threat
**Action:**
- Cut to absolute essentials only
- Preserve 1 month runway at all costs
- Consider emergency funding options
- Evaluate business viability
- Have honest conversation about pivot/pause

---

## Rebuilding After Emergency

### 30-60-90 Day Plan

**Days 1-30:**
- Stabilize cash flow
- Cut to minimum viable expenses
- Focus on quick revenue wins
- No new commitments

**Days 31-60:**
- Slowly restore essential functions
- Rebuild to 1 month reserve
- Analyze what caused depletion
- Implement preventive measures

**Days 61-90:**
- Restore normal operations
- Increase reserve contribution to 20%
- Build back to target reserve
- Document lessons learned

## Cash Flow Management

### Invoicing Best Practices

- Invoice immediately upon delivery
- Require 50% upfront for projects
- Offer discount for annual prepayment
- Net 15 terms, not Net 30
- Auto-charge for subscriptions

### Payment Terms

| Relationship | Terms |
|--------------|-------|
| New clients | 100% upfront or 50/50 |
| Established clients | Net 15 |
| Enterprise | Net 30 (if necessary) |
| Products | Immediate payment |

### Managing Receivables

- Follow up on Day 1 after due
- Second notice Day 7
- Phone call Day 14
- Payment plan or escalation Day 21
- Collection consideration Day 30+

## Seasonal Planning

### Anticipate Revenue Fluctuations

| Period | Typical Pattern | Action |
|--------|-----------------|--------|
| Jan-Feb | Slow (post-holiday) | Extra reserve |
| Mar-Apr | Recovery | Normal spending |
| May-Aug | Variable (summer) | Monitor closely |
| Sep-Oct | Strong (back to business) | Build reserve |
| Nov-Dec | Strong then slow | Hold reserve |

### Build for Slow Periods

- Identify your slow months
- Add 1 extra month reserve for each slow period
- Launch promotions before slow periods
- Create passive revenue for coverage

## Key Principles

1. **Reserve is not optional** - It's survival
2. **Build before you need it** - Emergencies don't wait
3. **Separate accounts** - Out of sight, out of mind
4. **Automate contributions** - Remove decision fatigue
5. **Know your numbers** - Expenses, runway, break-even
6. **Plan for worst case** - Hope for best, prepare for worst
7. **Review regularly** - Needs change, adjust accordingly
8. **Don't touch for opportunities** - That's growth capital, not reserve
