# Signal Map Delivery Playbook

**How to deliver the $1,500 Signal Map package in 5 days**

---

## Overview

The Signal Map is a discovery-and-analysis engagement. You spend 2 days learning the client's business, 2 days analyzing what you found, and 1 day packaging and presenting recommendations. The deliverable is an Executive Readout document (5-10 pages) that maps the client's manual workflows, scores them for automation potential, and recommends a pilot.

**Package price:** $1,500
**Your payout:** $1,050 total (30/40/30 milestone split)
**Timeline:** 5 business days from kickoff call
**Effort estimate:** 15-20 hours of actual work

## Day-by-Day Execution

### DAY 1-2: DISCOVERY (Estimated: 6-8 hours)

#### Step 1: Kickoff Call (60 minutes)

Join the 60-minute kickoff call with the client. For your first engagement as an EAS sub, the EAS owner may join to observe. After your first successful delivery, you run these solo.

**Call structure:**

- **0-5 min:** Introductions. Restate scope: "We're going to spend the next 5 days mapping your operations, finding where automation can save you the most time and money, and delivering a concrete plan."
- **5-45 min:** Discovery questions (below)
- **45-55 min:** Request access to tools, schedule observation session
- **55-60 min:** Confirm next steps, set expectations for the week

**Discovery Question Template:**

Use these in order. Let the conversation flow naturally but make sure you cover every question. Take notes in real time (use Otter.ai or similar for transcription if needed).

**Process & Time:**
1. "Walk me through a typical day. Where do you spend time that feels repetitive?"
2. "What tasks do you personally wish you could stop doing?"
3. "How many hours per week does your team spend on [specific process they mention]?"

**Communication & Calls:**
4. "How many phone calls does your team handle daily? How many get missed?"
5. "What happens when a call gets missed? Is there a follow-up process?"
6. "How do meeting outcomes get tracked? Who follows up on action items?"

**Tools & Systems:**
7. "What tools do you use? Walk me through them." (CRM, project management, accounting, scheduling, email marketing, phone system, etc.)
8. "Do any of these tools talk to each other, or is data moved manually between them?"
9. "Are there any tools you've tried and abandoned? Why?"

**Pain Points & Errors:**
10. "What's the most frustrating manual process in your business right now?"
11. "Where do things fall through the cracks? What gets forgotten?"
12. "How often do errors happen in [process X]? What do they cost you?"

**Team & Cost:**
13. "How many people are involved in [process X]? What's their hourly rate (roughly)?"
14. "If you could free up 10 hours per week for your team, where would you redeploy that time?"

**Prior Automation:**
15. "Have you tried automating anything before? What happened?"
16. "What's your comfort level with new technology? How about your team's?"

**Follow-up probes for any answer:** "Can you give me a specific example?" / "How often does that happen?" / "What does that cost you when it goes wrong?"

#### Step 2: Request Observation Access (15 minutes)

After the kickoff call, send a follow-up email requesting:

- 1-day screen share observation (shadow a team member for 2-3 hours), OR
- Access to call logs for the past 30 days (call volume, duration, missed calls), OR
- Access to email inbox metrics (volume, response time), OR
- Access to their CRM for read-only review

Use whichever method the client is most comfortable with. Screen share is best. Call logs are second best. The goal is to validate what they told you on the call with real data.

### DAY 3-4: ANALYSIS (Estimated: 6-8 hours)

#### Step 4: Map Workflows Visually (3-4 hours)

Take each process from your Process Inventory and create a visual workflow map.

**Tools (use any — free tiers are fine):**
- draw.io (diagrams.net) — recommended, exports clean PNGs/PDFs
- Miro — good for collaborative boards
- Lucidchart — polished output, free tier limited
- Whimsical — clean flowcharts

**For each workflow, map:**
- Trigger: What starts this process? (Phone call, form submission, email, time-based)
- Steps: Every action taken, in order. Include who does it and what tool they use.
- Decision points: Where does the process branch? (If X, then Y)
- Handoffs: Where does work pass from one person/tool to another?
- End state: What's the outcome? (Invoice sent, appointment booked, lead contacted)
- Failure points: Where does it break? Mark these in red.

**Mapping tips:**
- Use swimlanes (one lane per person/role) to show handoffs clearly
- Color-code: Green = automated, Yellow = semi-manual, Red = fully manual
- Keep it to one page per workflow — if it's more complex, break into sub-processes

#### Step 5: Score Each Workflow (1-2 hours)

Score every process from your Process Inventory on three dimensions, each 1-10:

**Automation Potential (1-10):**
- 9-10: Entirely rules-based, APIs available for all tools, no judgment required
- 7-8: Mostly rules-based, some edge cases need human review
- 5-6: Mix of rules and judgment, partial automation possible
- 3-4: Significant judgment required, automation assists but doesn't replace
- 1-2: Creative, strategic, or highly variable — not a good automation target

**ROI Impact (1-10):**
- 9-10: Saves 20+ hours/week, involves expensive staff, errors cost $1,000+/month
- 7-8: Saves 10-20 hours/week, moderate error costs
- 5-6: Saves 5-10 hours/week, minor error costs
- 3-4: Saves 2-5 hours/week, negligible error costs
- 1-2: Saves <2 hours/week, no measurable cost impact

**Implementation Complexity (1-10, lower is better for priority):**
- 1-2: Simple webhook + API call, off-the-shelf connectors exist
- 3-4: Multi-step workflow, some custom logic, well-documented APIs
- 5-6: Several integrations, some custom code needed, moderate testing
- 7-8: Complex logic, poorly documented APIs, data transformation required
- 9-10: Enterprise integration, legacy systems, custom development needed

**Priority Score Formula:**

```
Priority = (Automation Potential x ROI Impact) / Complexity
```

Higher score = better candidate for automation pilot.

**Scoring Matrix Template:**

| Process | Automation (1-10) | ROI (1-10) | Complexity (1-10) | Priority Score | Rank |
|---------|-------------------|------------|-------------------|----------------|------|
| Lead follow-up | 9 | 8 | 3 | 24.0 | 1 |
| Invoice processing | 8 | 6 | 4 | 12.0 | 2 |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |

#### Step 6: Build ROI Model (1-2 hours)

For the top 3 candidates, build a concrete ROI model.

**Inputs (from discovery):**
- `team_size`: Number of people involved in this process
- `hourly_rate`: Average hourly cost per person (salary + benefits / 2080, or ask client)
- `hours_per_week`: Hours spent on this process per week
- `error_cost_monthly`: Estimated monthly cost of errors (missed leads, rework, penalties)
- `tool_cost_monthly`: Cost of automation tools needed

**Calculations:**

```
Current monthly cost = (team_size x hourly_rate x hours_per_week x 4.33) + error_cost_monthly
Automated monthly cost = tool_cost_monthly + (reduced_hours x hourly_rate x 4.33)
Monthly savings = Current monthly cost - Automated monthly cost
Annual savings = Monthly savings x 12
Payback period (days) = Pilot price / (Monthly savings / 30)
ROI percentage = (Annual savings / Pilot price) x 100
```

**Present this as a simple table:**

| Metric | Current State | Automated State | Delta |
|--------|---------------|-----------------|-------|
| Hours/week on process | 15 hrs | 2 hrs | -13 hrs |
| Monthly labor cost | $3,900 | $520 | -$3,380 |
| Monthly error cost | $800 | $100 | -$700 |
| Tool cost/month | $0 | $150 | +$150 |
| **Net monthly savings** | — | — | **$3,930** |
| Annual savings | — | — | $47,160 |
| Pilot investment | — | — | $3,500-$4,500 |
| Payback period | — | — | 27-34 days |

### DAY 5: DELIVERABLES (Estimated: 4-6 hours)

#### Step 7: Compile Executive Readout (3-4 hours)

Use the EAS Executive Readout template. The document should be 5-10 pages, professionally formatted, with the client's logo if available.

**Page-by-page structure:**

**Page 1: Executive Summary**
- 1 paragraph (4-6 sentences) summarizing:
  - What you found
  - The biggest opportunity
  - Expected ROI of recommended pilot
  - Recommended next step
- This is the only page most executives will read. Make it count.

**Pages 2-3: Current State Map**
- Workflow diagrams (from Step 4)
- Pain points highlighted in red
- Brief annotations explaining each bottleneck
- Data points: hours wasted, error rates, costs

**Pages 4-5: Automation Opportunities**
- Top 3 candidates, ranked by priority score
- For each:
  - What it is (1 sentence)
  - Why it's a good candidate (2-3 sentences referencing score)
  - Expected ROI (monthly savings, payback period)
  - What the automated version looks like (brief description)

**Page 6: Recommended Pilot Scope**
- Which automation to build first and why
- What's in scope and what's explicitly out of scope
- Expected outcomes (specific, measurable: "Reduce missed calls from 30% to under 5%")
- Success criteria (how we'll know it worked)

**Page 7: Architecture Diagram**
- Visual diagram showing how the pilot would be built
- Tools involved (n8n, Bland AI, CRM, etc.)
- Data flow arrows
- Integration points
- Keep it understandable by a non-technical stakeholder

**Page 8: Tool Recommendations**
- Vendor-neutral comparison of tools considered
- Cost analysis (monthly/annual for each tool)
- Why you're recommending specific tools for this client
- Note: recommend n8n (self-hosted) for clients who want control, Make/Zapier for clients who want simplicity

**Page 9: Timeline & Investment**
- 10-day pilot timeline (reference the Phone Pilot or Ops Pilot playbook structure)
- Pricing for the recommended pilot package
- What's included, what's not
- Payment terms (milestone-based)

**Page 10: Appendix**
- Raw discovery notes
- Full scoring matrix
- Complete ROI calculations
- Tool inventory
- Any additional data collected

#### Step 8: Pre-Delivery Prep (30 minutes)

- Proofread the entire document. Check for:
  - Client name spelled correctly throughout
  - Numbers are consistent (don't say 15 hours in one place and 12 in another)
  - No EAS internal language visible to the client
  - All diagrams are legible and properly labeled
- Export as PDF
- Send to client 24 hours before the presentation meeting

#### Step 9: Client Presentation (30 minutes)

- Share screen, walk through the readout page by page
- Spend most time on Pages 4-6 (opportunities, pilot scope)
- Ask: "Does this match your experience? Anything we missed?"
- If client wants to proceed to pilot: "Great, I'll connect you with [EAS owner] to get that kicked off."
- Do NOT sell or negotiate pricing. Warm-transfer to EAS owner for next steps.

## Common Pitfalls

| Pitfall | Why It Happens | How to Avoid |
|---------|----------------|--------------|
| Promising specific results before discovery | Client asks "how much will we save?" on kickoff call | Say: "That's exactly what we're here to find out. I'll have real numbers for you by Day 5." |
| Recommending tools without checking existing stack | Enthusiasm for your favorite tool | Always map their current tools first. Build on what they have when possible. |
| Skipping the scoring step | Feels academic, want to jump to recommendations | The scoring matrix is the most valuable deliverable. It shows your work and justifies recommendations. Clients keep this. |
| Presenting more than 3 recommendations | Found 8 good candidates, want to show all of them | Decision paralysis is real. Pick the top 3. Put the rest in the appendix. |
| Over-engineering the readout | Spending 10 hours on formatting | Content matters more than design. Use the template, keep it clean, move on. |
| Not getting real numbers | Client gives vague answers ("a lot of time") | Push for specifics: "Can you estimate? Is it closer to 5 hours or 20?" Even rough numbers are better than none. |
| Talking about EAS pricing during the call | Client asks "how much would the pilot cost?" | Give the range from the readout ($3,500-$4,500) and say "The EAS team will walk you through the details." |

## Escalation

- **Client unresponsive:** If the client doesn't reply within 24 hours or misses a scheduled call, notify EAS owner immediately. Do not chase more than twice.
- **Scope creep:** If the client asks for work beyond the Signal Map (e.g., "can you just build a quick automation while you're in there?"), say: "That's a great idea — let's capture it in the readout recommendations and scope it properly." Do not do free work.
- **Technical blocker:** If you can't access a tool or data source you need, notify EAS within 4 hours. Don't wait until checkpoint day.
- **Client pushback on findings:** Present the data. If they disagree with your scoring, adjust based on their input — they know their business. Note any disagreements in the appendix.
