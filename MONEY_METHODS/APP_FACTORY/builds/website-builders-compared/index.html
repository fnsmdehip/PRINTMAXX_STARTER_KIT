the breakdown

WordPress

powers 43% of all websites. the largest ecosystem of themes, plugins, and developers. if you can imagine a feature, there's a plugin for it. that flexibility is both its strength and its biggest problem for small business owners.

the reality for small businesses: a plumber doesn't need 60,000 plugins. they need a phone number, a service list, and a contact form. WordPress gives you all of that, plus 59,997 things you'll never use. and those plugins need updates, security patches, and occasionally break each other.

when WordPress makes sense: if you plan to blog regularly (weekly), sell products online (WooCommerce), or want full control over your site's code. also makes sense if you already have a tech-savvy employee or contractor who can maintain it.

when it doesn't: if "update your plugins" fills you with dread. if you want something that works without ongoing maintenance. if your site is 5 pages and a contact form.

what's good

  • cheapest hosting ($4/mo on Hostinger)
  • most plugins and themes
  • you own everything
  • best SEO tools (Yoast, RankMath)
  • easy to find developers

what's not

  • requires ongoing maintenance
  • security vulnerabilities (plugins)
  • steeper learning curve
  • slow without optimization
  • hosting quality varies wildly

Squarespace

the best-looking templates in the game. if design matters to your business (restaurants, studios, creative agencies, real estate), Squarespace templates look professional out of the box. no CSS tweaking, no "why doesn't this look like the demo" frustration.

the trade-off is lock-in. your site lives on Squarespace's servers. if you want to move to WordPress later, you're rebuilding from scratch. they know this, which is why they charge $16-49/mo instead of a one-time fee.

real cost analysis: $16/mo Personal plan over 3 years = $576. $27/mo Business plan (for booking + commerce) over 3 years = $972. that's more than a custom build, and you don't own anything at the end.

what's good

  • best design templates
  • zero maintenance
  • SSL, hosting, domain all included
  • good mobile experience
  • built-in scheduling ($27+)

what's not

  • $16-49/mo adds up over years
  • vendor lock-in (can't export easily)
  • limited customization beyond templates
  • no native booking on cheap plan
  • transaction fees on lower plans

Wix

the easiest to use. Wix's drag-and-drop editor is genuinely intuitive. you move things where you want them. no grid constraints, no "this template doesn't support that layout." their AI site builder (ADI) can generate a complete site from a few questions in under 5 minutes.

the page speed problem. Wix sites average 3.1 seconds load time. Google's benchmark is under 2.5 seconds. for local SEO (where Google ranks you in map pack and local search), page speed matters. a slow site means lower rankings, fewer clicks, fewer calls.

Wix makes sense for: businesses that change their content frequently (restaurants with daily menus, event venues with weekly updates) and don't want to call a developer every time. the drag-and-drop means anyone on your team can update the site.

what's good
