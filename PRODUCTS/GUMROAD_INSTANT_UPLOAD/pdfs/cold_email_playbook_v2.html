Part 1: The 6-Question Framework

every cold email that gets replies answers these 6 questions in under 100 words. most cold emails fail because they try to explain everything. this framework forces you to cut the fat.

the 6 questions:

  1. what do you do? (one sentence, no jargon)
  2. who do you do it for? (specific industry + company size)
  3. how do you do it? (the method, not the technology)
  4. what problem do you solve? (their problem, not your solution)
  5. what’s your proof? (one specific result with numbers)
  6. what’s the ROI? (dollars saved or earned, time saved, or risk removed)

example using the framework:

I build cold email systems for B2B SaaS companies with 5-50 employees. I set up the infrastructure, write the sequences, and manage the first 30 days of sending. Last quarter I helped a CRM startup book 47 demos in 60 days, starting from zero outbound. Most clients see positive ROI within the first 2 weeks of sending.

that’s 63 words. answers all 6 questions. no fluff.

how to use it:

write your answers to all 6 questions separately. then combine them into one paragraph. read it out loud. if it sounds like a pitch deck, rewrite it until it sounds like something you’d say to a friend at a bar.

the test: send your 6-question paragraph to someone who doesn’t know what you do. if they can explain your business back to you in one sentence, you nailed it. if they ask “but what do you actually do?” start over.

Part 2: 20 Subject Line Templates

these are tested across 10,000+ sends. open rates listed are averages across multiple campaigns and industries. your results will vary based on your list quality and targeting.

high-performers (55-68% open rate)

  1. quick question about [company name] (68% avg)
  2. [first name], noticed something on [company website] (62% avg)
  3. [mutual connection] suggested I reach out (61% avg - only use when true)
  4. idea for [company name] (59% avg)
  5. [company name] + [your company name] (57% avg)

solid performers (45-55% open rate)

  1. [specific metric] for [company name] (54% avg)
  2. saw your [recent post/article/launch] (52% avg)
  3. question about your [department/process] (51% avg)
  4. [first name] (49% avg - yes, just their name)
  5. thoughts on [industry trend]? (48% avg)
  6. [company name]'s [specific page/product] (47% avg)
  7. can I send you something? (46% avg)
  8. [number] [result] for [similar company] (45% avg)

follow-up subject lines (40-50% open rate)

  1. re: [original subject] (50% avg - simple and effective)
  2. did this land? (47% avg)
  3. one more thing (44% avg)
  4. closing the loop (43% avg)
  5. [first name], last one from me (42% avg - breakup email)
  6. forgot to mention (41% avg)
  7. bumping this (40% avg - direct, no games)

subject line rules

  • keep it under 6 words when possible
  • never use ALL CAPS
  • never use “RE:” to fake a reply chain
  • personalization tokens ([company], [name]) boost opens 15-25%
  • test 2 subject lines per campaign minimum
  • track open rates per subject line, not per campaign

Part 4: Reply Handling Scripts

you sent emails. people replied. now what. here’s exactly how to handle the 7 most common responses.

response 1: “not interested”

totally understand, [first name]. appreciate you letting me know.

quick question before I go: is it a timing thing, or is [your service] not something [company name] needs in general? helps me know whether to check back in a few months or leave you alone.

why this works: 30% of “not interested” replies convert when you dig into the reason. “timing” means follow up in 90 days. “not a fit” means move on.

response 2: “send more info”

absolutely. here’s a quick overview:

what: [one sentence] how it works: [one sentence] results: [one specific case study] cost: [range, not exact number] next step: 15-minute call to see if it fits. here’s my calendar: [link]

let me know if you have questions before booking.

why this works: “send more info” is often a polite way to stall. sending a brief overview + calendar link keeps momentum. do NOT send a 10-page PDF.

response 3: “too expensive”

totally fair. a few options:

  1. we could start with [smaller scope] at [lower price] and expand if you see results
  2. I could do a one-time [specific deliverable] for [price] instead of the full engagement
  3. if budget opens up later, I’m happy to revisit in [Q3/Q4/next year]

which of these works best for you?

why this works: gives them three options instead of a yes/no. option 1 is the one you want. option 3 keeps the door open.

response 4: “we already have someone for this”

got it. not trying to replace what’s working.

just curious: are you happy with the results you’re getting from [service area]? if yes, great, I’ll back off. if there’s a gap, sometimes having a second perspective helps.

why this works: 40% of “we have someone” replies are dissatisfied but haven’t switched. this opens the door without being aggressive.

response 5: “can you call me?”

absolutely. what’s the best number and time?

I’m available [3 specific time slots this week]. if none of those work, here’s my calendar: [link]

why this works: never respond with “sure, when works?” always offer specific times. reduces back-and-forth from 6 emails to 2.

response 6: “how did you get my email?”

your email is listed on [company website / LinkedIn / public directory]. I research companies I think I can help and reach out directly. no purchased lists.

if you’d prefer I don’t contact you again, just say the word and I’ll remove you immediately.

why this works: transparency. most people who ask this are checking if you’re a spammer. showing you did real research usually satisfies the concern.

response 7: ghosted after positive reply

day 3 after ghost: > hey [first name], just wanted to make sure this didn’t get buried. still up for that call?

day 7 after ghost: > [first name], I know things get busy. I’m going to assume the timing isn’t right. if it ever is, my calendar’s always open: [link]

why this works: two follow-ups max after a ghost. more than that and you’re being weird. the second one gives them an easy out while keeping the door open.

Part 5: Domain and DNS Setup Guide

skip this section and nothing else in this playbook matters. your emails will go to spam.

step 1: buy a secondary domain

do NOT send cold emails from your primary domain. if it gets flagged, your entire business email is compromised.

  • buy a domain similar to your main one (e.g., if you’re acme.com, buy acmehq.com or getacme.com)
  • cost: $10-15/year on Namecheap, Google Domains, or Cloudflare
  • use this domain ONLY for cold outbound

step 2: set up email on the new domain

options: - Google Workspace ($6/mo per mailbox) - best deliverability - Microsoft 365 ($6/mo per mailbox) - solid alternative - Zoho Mail (free for 1 user) - budget option

create 2-3 mailboxes per domain. example: - tom@getacme.com - thomas@getacme.com - t.smith@getacme.com

step 3: configure DNS records

SPF record: add a TXT record to your domain’s DNS:

v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com ~all

(replace with your email provider’s SPF include)

DKIM record: your email provider will give you a DKIM key. add it as a TXT record. this proves emails actually came from your domain.

DMARC record: add a TXT record:

v=DMARC1; p=none; rua=mailto:dmarc@getacme.com

start with p=none to monitor. move to p=quarantine after 30 days.

custom tracking domain: if you’re using a cold email tool (Instantly, Smartlead, Lemlist), set up a custom tracking domain instead of using theirs. shared tracking domains get flagged.

step 4: verify everything

  • use mail-tester.com (send a test email, get a score out of 10)
  • target: 9/10 or higher
  • use mxtoolbox.com to verify SPF, DKIM, DMARC are all passing
  • if anything fails, your email provider’s docs will have the fix

Part 6: Email Warmup Checklist (14-Day Plan)

new email accounts have zero reputation. sending 50 cold emails from a new account = instant spam folder. warmup builds reputation gradually.

option A: use a warmup tool (recommended)

free tools: Warmup Inbox (free tier), Instantly (included with paid plan), Mailwarm

the tool sends and receives emails between real accounts automatically. simulates normal email behavior.

option B: manual warmup

days 1-3: send 5 personal emails per day to friends, colleagues, gmail/outlook accounts. have them reply. open and click links.

days 4-7: increase to 10 emails per day. mix of people you know and warmup tool. reply to every email you receive.

days 8-10: increase to 15-20 emails per day. start subscribing to newsletters and replying to them (generates inbound).

days 11-14: increase to 25-30 emails per day. you can now start mixing in cold emails. start with 5 cold + 20 warm per day.

post-warmup sending schedule

  • week 3: 10 cold emails per day per mailbox
  • week 4: 20 cold emails per day per mailbox
  • week 5+: 30-40 cold emails per day per mailbox (max)

never exceed 50 cold emails per day per mailbox. if you need more volume, add more mailboxes. 3 mailboxes x 40 emails = 120 per day.

warmup never stops

even after you start cold sending, keep warmup running. most tools let you run warmup and cold sends simultaneously. if you pause warmup, deliverability drops within 2 weeks.

Part 7: Metrics to Track

the only numbers that matter

metric good average bad fix
open rate 55%+ 35-55% below 35% fix subject lines or deliverability
reply rate 8%+ 3-8% below 3% fix email body or targeting
positive reply rate 3%+ 1-3% below 1% fix offer or ICP
bounce rate below 2% 2-5% above 5% clean your list, verify emails
spam complaint rate below 0.1% 0.1-0.3% above 0.3% stop immediately, fix everything
demo/call booking rate 50%+ of positive replies 30-50% below 30% fix your CTA or calendar link

how to track

free method: Google Sheets with these columns: - date sent - prospect name - company - email - subject line used - sequence number (1-5) - opened (yes/no) - replied (yes/no) - reply type (positive/negative/question) - meeting booked (yes/no) - deal closed (yes/no) - deal value

paid method: Instantly, Smartlead, or Lemlist track all of this automatically.

weekly review process

every Friday, spend 15 minutes reviewing:

  1. which subject lines had the highest open rate? double down on winners, kill losers.
  2. which email in the sequence got the most replies? if email 3 outperforms email 1, your email 1 needs work.
  3. what’s your positive reply rate by ICP? you might find dentists reply 3x more than lawyers. adjust targeting accordingly.
  4. any spam complaints? if yes, check your list quality and email content immediately.
  5. cost per meeting booked. even if cold email is “free,” your time has value. track hours spent and divide by meetings booked.
