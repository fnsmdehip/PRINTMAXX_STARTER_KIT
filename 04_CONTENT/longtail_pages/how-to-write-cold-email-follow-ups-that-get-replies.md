# How to write cold email follow-ups that get replies

## Quick answer

80% of sales require 5+ follow-ups. 44% of salespeople give up after 1. Send 7 follow-ups spaced 3-4 days apart. Each adds new value (case study, insight, resource), never "just checking in." Reply rates double between follow-up 1 and follow-up 5.

## The 7-email sequence

Email 1 (Day 0): The opener. Specific observation about their business + result you achieved for similar company + ask for 15-min call. Under 75 words. No links.

Email 2 (Day 3): Case study. One client story with one specific number. Same thread.

Email 3 (Day 7): Insight. Industry data point they probably don't know. Give value even if they never respond.

Email 4 (Day 11): Social proof. Name a competitor or peer you just closed. Specific improved metric.

Email 5 (Day 15): Resource. First email with a link. PDF, blog post, or video. No pitch attached.

Email 6 (Day 20): Direct ask. "Is [problem] a priority right now? If yes, let's talk. If not, no hard feelings."

Email 7 (Day 25): Breakup. "Closing the loop. I'll reach back in 6 months unless you say otherwise."

The breakup email consistently has the highest reply rate. People respond when they think they are about to lose the option.

## Rules that increase replies

1. Same thread. Every follow-up replies to the original.
2. Shorter each time. Email 1: 75 words. Email 7: 30 words.
3. New value each time. Never "just checking in."
4. Plain text. No HTML, no images.
5. One question per email.

## What not to do

- "Just following up" (zero value)
- "Did you see my last email?" (passive aggressive)
- Following up every day
- HTML templates with images
- Attaching pitch decks

## FAQ

### How many follow-ups before giving up?

7 over 25 days. Then 6-month drip list.

### What if they say not interested?

"Mind if I check back in 6 months?" Set reminder. Situations change.

### What time to send?

Tuesday-Thursday, 8-10 AM in their time zone.

### Is 7 emails too many?

No. 80% of deals need 5+ touches. 7 over 25 days is 1 every 3-4 days. Persistent, not aggressive.
