## How to do AI influencer marketing legally for client onboarding

You've seen the AI influencers. Lil Miquela on Instagram. Virtual avatars selling products. They convert better than real people sometimes.

But here's the problem: the FTC is watching.

If you're using an AI persona to onboard clients or sell anything, you need to know the rules. Because one mistake turns into a $100k fine.

## The core FTC rule

The FTC doesn't ban AI influencers. They ban deception.

If you're using an AI persona and you don't disclose it's AI, you're violating the Guides Against Deceptive Advertising. Full stop.

The rule: consumers have a right to know they're seeing an AI. Not hidden in the terms. Clear. Up front.

## What counts as illegal

An AI persona that looks human, posts like a human, but you don't say it's AI. That's deceptive.

Specific examples from FTC actions:

- "Meet Sarah, your personal wellness coach" (when Sarah is AI-generated).
- AI video testimonials presented as real customer reviews.
- An AI persona with a fake backstory (hometown, age, "past experiences").
- Sponsored posts where the AI personality doesn't disclose the partnership.

The pattern: using AI to impersonate a real person or hide that it's AI.

## What's actually legal

Disclosing upfront. "This is an AI avatar created by [Company] to help onboard clients."

Being obviously AI. If your avatar has a clear synthetic look (stylized animation, digital-only), people understand it's AI already. You still need to say it, but the barrier to deception is lower.

Using AI for efficiency, not impersonation. Example: an AI chatbot that helps clients through onboarding. It's clear it's a bot. You disclose it's AI-powered. That's fine.

Real + AI hybrid. "Sarah is our onboarding coach. Sarah is a real person + AI-assisted tools." That's transparent. That works.

## The gray areas

Some things are unclear and risk-dependent:

**AI voice for a real person.** You use an AI voice narrator for educational videos. You say "narration by AI." The FTC hasn't cracked down on this. But the disclosure needs to be clear.

**AI-generated faces.** Lil Miquela is obviously AI because she's stylized. But if you generate a realistic face and say nothing, that's trouble. The realism makes it deceptive.

**AI copywriting from real personas.** You hire a real influencer but use AI to write their posts. Is that deceptive? The FTC hasn't directly ruled, but best practice is disclosing "AI-assisted content."

## How it applies to client onboarding

If you're using an AI avatar to guide new clients through signup, here's what needs to happen:

**Disclose at the start.** "You're chatting with an AI assistant powered by GPT." Right there on the screen.

**Don't create a false backstory.** Don't say "I'm Jamie, a 12-year customer success expert." You're AI. You don't have experience. You have training.

**Don't hide affiliate connections.** If you make money when the client buys something the AI recommends, disclose it. "I make a commission on this recommendation" or just put an affiliate disclosure on the page.

**Handle payment carefully.** Never have the AI take payment directly. Route to a human or clear checkout process. Adds friction but keeps you legal.

## Real example

You build an AI onboarding bot for a SaaS. Here's what's legal:

"Welcome to [SaaS]. I'm Onyx, an AI assistant. I'll walk you through setup. This is AI-powered. If you need a human, type 'support.'"

Client goes through flow. Bot recommends a paid upgrade. Bot says: "This upgrade is $29/month. Your success team earns a commission if you upgrade. Still want to proceed?"

Transparent. Legal. Works.

Here's what's NOT legal:

"Hi, I'm Jamie, your success coach. I've helped 500+ customers. Here's my recommendation: upgrade to premium."

(Jamie doesn't exist. You don't disclose it's AI. You hide the commission. That's deceptive.)

## FTC enforcement activity

In 2024, the FTC focused on:
- Fake reviews (including AI-generated).
- Hidden endorsements (influencer paid, doesn't disclose).
- Influencer impersonation (real person's face, AI voiceover).

They haven't sued many AI avatar companies directly. But that's because most of them disclose. The ones that don't, watch out.

## The compliance checklist

Before you launch an AI persona for client onboarding:

- Does it clearly say "AI" or "AI-powered"? (visible, not hidden)
- Are there false claims about who the AI is or what it's done?
- Are affiliate relationships disclosed?
- Can a consumer easily identify it's not a real person?
- Is the disclosure before the pitch (not after)?

If all answers are yes, you're safe.
