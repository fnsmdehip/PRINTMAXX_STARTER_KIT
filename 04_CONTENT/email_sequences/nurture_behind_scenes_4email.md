# Behind-the-scenes email templates (4 variations)

Use this for: Sharing authentic looks at your process, failures, and real work. Builds connection and humanizes your brand.

## Overview

Send behind-the-scenes emails 1-2x per month. Show the messy reality behind polished results. Builds trust through transparency. Under 350 words each.

Expected open rate: 45-65%
Expected engagement rate: 10-20% (high reply rate)

---

## Template 1: The failure story

**Subject:** I screwed up [specific thing]
**Subject B:** What went wrong with [project/launch/strategy]
**Preview:** Cost me [consequence]. Here's what happened.
**Use for:** Sharing mistakes and lessons learned

---

{{firstName}},

I messed up.

**What I did:**
[Specific action or decision]

**Why I thought it would work:**
[Your reasoning at the time]

**What actually happened:**
[The failure or negative result]

**The cost:**
[Time, money, opportunity, or credibility lost]

**Where I went wrong:**
[Specific mistake in judgment or execution]

**What I should have done:**
[Better approach in hindsight]

**The lesson:**
[Transferable insight from this failure]

Sharing this so you don't make the same mistake.

[Your name]

P.S. [Related insight or what you're doing differently now]

---

**Example (AI/Productivity niche):**

Subject: I wasted $2,400 on the wrong automation tool

{{firstName}},

I messed up.

**What I did:**
Bought a year subscription to an expensive automation platform ($200/month = $2,400). Didn't test with free trial first.

**Why I thought it would work:**
Everyone on Twitter was raving about it. Saw case studies of people saving 20 hours per week. Figured I needed the "best" tool.

**What actually happened:**
Took 3 weeks to set up. Interface was confusing. Half the features I needed weren't available. Ended up not using it after 2 months.

**The cost:**
$2,400 plus 40 hours trying to make it work. Could have spent that money on tools I actually use.

**Where I went wrong:**
Bought based on hype, not my actual needs. Didn't test first. Picked enterprise solution when I needed simple tool.

**What I should have done:**
Start with free trial. Test for 2 weeks. Make sure it actually solves my problem before paying.

**The lesson:**
Expensive doesn't mean better. The right tool is the one you'll actually use. Always test before committing.

Sharing this so you don't make the same mistake.

[Your name]

P.S. Now I have a rule: Test free/cheap version for 30 days before upgrading. Saved me from 3 other bad purchases since then.

---

**Example (Fitness niche):**

Subject: What my workout week actually looks like

{{firstName}},

Behind the scenes of my typical training week.

**Monday 6:00 AM:**
Alarm goes off. Hit snooze twice. Actually get up at 6:20. Drink coffee while staring at phone for 15 minutes.

**Monday 6:45 AM:**
Start workout. Get through warm-up. Kid wakes up crying. Go deal with that for 10 minutes. Come back. Finish workout. Takes 40 minutes total instead of planned 25.

**Tuesday:**
Planned to work out. Too sore from Monday. Take rest day. Feel guilty about it but body needs recovery.

**Wednesday 7:00 PM:**
Evening workout because morning didn't happen. Gym is crowded. Equipment I need is taken. Adjust workout on the fly. Not ideal but got it done.

**Thursday:**
Sick kid. No workout. Order pizza. Go to bed at 9 PM exhausted.

**Friday 6:15 AM:**
Good workout. Hit all exercises. Feel great. This is the one that makes Instagram.

**What people think I do:**
Perfect morning routine. Flawless workouts 5 days per week. Meal prepped and disciplined.

**What I actually do:**
Miss workouts. Adjust plans constantly. Sometimes eat pizza. Work out when I can, not when I "should."

**Biggest time sink:**
Guilt about missed workouts. Spent years beating myself up. Now I just adjust and move on.

**What actually moves the needle:**
3 workouts per week consistently, even if imperfect. That's it. The rest is nice-to-have.

**The honest truth:**
Perfect routines don't exist. Life happens. Consistency beats perfection every single time.

Not glamorous. But it's real.

[Your name]

P.S. If you miss a workout, don't skip the next one to "make up for it." Just get back on schedule.

**Example (Content creator niche):**

Subject: Real numbers from my first product launch

{{firstName}},

Real numbers from launching my course last month.

**Revenue:**
- $24,380 total
- 167 sales at $147 each
- 4 refunds (2.4%)

**What worked:**
- Email list (79 sales from 2,400 subscribers = 3.3% conversion)
- Twitter thread launch day (42 sales)
- Existing customers buying second product (31 sales)

**What didn't work:**
- LinkedIn posts (12 impressions, 0 sales)
- Instagram stories (dead silence)
- Paid ads ($400 spent, 1 sale)

**Surprises:**
Most sales came in first 48 hours and last 6 hours. Middle 5 days were slow. Next time I'll run a shorter launch.

**The breakdown:**
- 67% from email
- 25% from Twitter
- 8% from word of mouth

**Cost/Investment:**
- 120 hours creating course
- 40 hours marketing
- $400 ads (wasted)
- $200 tools (email software, hosting)
Total: 160 hours + $600

**Net result:**
$23,780 after costs and refunds. That's $148/hour if you count my time. Better than my freelance rate.

**What this actually means:**
Good first launch. Email list is the asset. Social media helps but email converts. Need bigger list to scale this.

Raw data. No spin.

[Your name]

P.S. Cutting paid ads next launch. Putting that money into growing email list instead.

**Example (Solopreneur niche):**

Subject: Why I shut down my $6K/month service

{{firstName}},

People keep asking why I shut down my consulting practice.

Here's the real story.

**The situation:**
Making $6K/month from 8 clients. Working 35 hours per week. Good money. Sustainable workload. But something felt off.

**The options:**
1. Keep going as-is (safe, predictable income)
2. Scale by hiring (more money, more stress)
3. Shut down and build a product (risk, but more upside)

**Factors I considered:**
- Income stability (family has bills)
- Time freedom (wanted to work less, not manage team)
- Scalability (consulting caps at my hours)
- Fulfillment (client work felt repetitive)

**What almost made me choose differently:**
The money. $6K/month covers my expenses with room to spare. Walking away from that felt stupid.

**The deciding factor:**
Did the math on 5-year trajectory. Best case with consulting: $100K/year. Best case with product: $500K+/year. Realized I was trading long-term upside for short-term safety.

**What I'm giving up:**
- Guaranteed income
- Client relationships
- Expertise I've built
- Comfortable routine

**What I'm gaining:**
- Time to build something scalable
- Potential for 10x income
- Control over my schedule
- Forcing myself to grow

**The risk:**
Might fail. Might make $0 for 6 months. Might regret leaving stable income.

**How I'll know if it was right:**
If I hit $3K/month from product in 6 months, it was worth it. If not, I'll get clients again or find a job.

**The honest truth:**
I'm scared. This might be a huge mistake. But staying comfortable felt like slow death.

Time will tell if this was right.

[Your name]

P.S. I'll share monthly updates on how this goes. No sugarcoating.

## When to send behind-the-scenes emails

**Best timing:**
- After failures or setbacks
- During major transitions
- When you have interesting data
- In response to common questions
- When you need to humanize your brand

**Frequency:**
- 1-2 per month in regular nurture
- More during pivots or changes
- Less if you're already very personal

**Use strategically:**
- After a series of wins (stay humble)
- When launching (show the work behind it)
- During slow periods (keep engagement high)

---

## What makes behind-the-scenes content work

**Essential elements:**
- Specificity (numbers, details, names)
- Honesty (don't sanitize everything)
- Vulnerability (admit doubts and failures)
- Lessons (what can they learn from this?)
- Humanity (show you're a real person)

**Avoid:**
- Humble bragging ("I only made $50K this month")
- Oversharing personal drama
- Complaining without insight
- Making it all about you
- Fishing for compliments

---

## Building trust through transparency

**What to share:**
- Actual numbers (revenue, growth, costs)
- Failures and mistakes
- Decision-making process
- Day-to-day reality
- What you're learning

**What not to share:**
- Client confidential info
- Personal family drama
- Illegal or unethical behavior
- Anything you'll regret later
- Information that helps competitors

**The balance:**
- Transparent enough to be relatable
- Careful enough to protect privacy
- Honest enough to build trust
- Professional enough to maintain respect

## Handling responses

**Common replies:**

**"Thanks for being honest"**
→ "Appreciate it. Figured people needed to see the real side."

**"I'm dealing with the same thing"**
→ "Want to share what's working for you? Always curious."

**"How did you decide to [thing]?"**
→ Share more context, maybe turn into future email

**"Can I ask you about [related topic]?"**
→ Answer if you can, or point to resource

**High reply rate = engaged audience.**
Answer most or all responses.

---

## Converting engagement to revenue

**Don't hard sell, but:**
- P.S. mention of relevant product
- Link to resource that goes deeper
- Natural mention of paid offering

**Example soft sells:**

"P.S. The full breakdown of my launch strategy is in my course: [link]"

"P.S. If you want help with [problem], here's how I work with people: [link]"

"P.S. Built a tool to track this stuff: [link]. Free trial."

**The key:**
Behind-the-scenes builds trust.
Trust makes selling easier later.
Don't rush the process.

## Tracking performance

**Key metrics:**
- Open rate: Target 45-65% (curiosity factor)
- Reply rate: Target 5-15% (higher than most)
- Forward rate: People share relatable stuff
- Unsolicited responses: "This resonated"

**What works best:**
- Failure stories (high engagement)
- Real numbers (curiosity + credibility)
- Day-in-life (relatability)
- Decision breakdowns (people love process)

**Test and optimize:**
- Which formats get most replies
- Which lead to sales
- Which get shared most

## Common mistakes

1. **Too polished:** Defeats the purpose. Keep it raw.
2. **No lesson:** Make it useful, not just venting.
3. **Too frequent:** Once per month max unless major event.
4. **Fake vulnerability:** People can tell when you're performing.
5. **Oversharing:** Not every detail needs to be public.
6. **No follow-up:** If you share a journey, update them.
7. **Humble bragging:** "Only $100K this year" alienates people.
