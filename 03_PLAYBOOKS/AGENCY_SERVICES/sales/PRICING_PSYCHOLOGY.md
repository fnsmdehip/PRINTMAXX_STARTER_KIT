# Pricing Psychology

How to present, anchor, and defend your pricing for maximum conversion.

---

## Core principle

Price is a story. The number matters less than the context around it.

$500 feels expensive if you're "buying a service."
$500 feels cheap if you're "saving 15 hours every week forever."

Your job: frame the value before revealing the price.

---

## The anchor-present-justify method

### Step 1: Anchor high

Before revealing your price, anchor to something bigger.

**Time anchor:**
"You said you spend 10 hours/week on this. Over a year, that's 520 hours. At even $50/hour, that's $26,000 worth of your time."

**Competitor anchor:**
"Agencies charge $5,000-10,000 for this kind of setup. They add overhead for project managers, account executives, and fancy offices."

**DIY anchor:**
"If you tried to learn this yourself, you'd spend 40-60 hours. Plus the cost of trial and error. Plus the opportunity cost of not doing revenue-generating work."

---

### Step 2: Present the price

State your price simply and confidently. Then stop talking.

"The investment for this is $1,000."

[Pause. Let them process.]

Don't say:
- "It's only $1,000"
- "$1,000, but I can do a discount"
- "$1,000, I know that sounds like a lot"

Just state it and wait.

---

### Step 3: Justify with value

After presenting, connect back to their specific pain.

"That covers [DELIVERABLES]. You get the system running, documentation, training, and [X] days of support. Based on the 15 hours/week you'll save, the system pays for itself in under a month."

---

## Three-tier pricing strategy

Always present three options. This shifts the question from "should I buy?" to "which one should I buy?"

### Structure

| Tier | Purpose | Pricing |
|------|---------|---------|
| Bronze | Entry point, gets foot in door | Base price |
| Silver | Most common, best value | 1.5-2x Bronze |
| Gold | Premium, maximum value | 2-3x Bronze |

### Presentation order

Present Gold first. Then Silver. Then Bronze.

Why? Gold anchors high. Silver feels reasonable by comparison. Bronze exists for those who truly can't afford more.

---

### Example script

"I have three packages that could work:

**Gold ($2,000)** is the full solution. You get [EVERYTHING]. This is for people who want to go all-in and get maximum results.

**Silver ($1,000)** is what most clients choose. It covers [CORE DELIVERABLES] and gets you [MAIN BENEFIT]. You can always upgrade later.

**Bronze ($500)** is a starter option. It handles [BASIC NEED] and is good for testing the waters.

Based on what you told me about [THEIR SPECIFIC SITUATION], I'd recommend Silver. It gives you [SPECIFIC BENEFIT THEY MENTIONED] without the extras you don't need yet."

## Price framing techniques

### Break it down

$1,000 sounds like a lot. $33/day for a month sounds manageable.

"$1,000 for the project. Broken down, that's about $30/day for a month. Compare that to the 2 hours you'll save every single day going forward."

---

### Compare to what they already spend

"You're paying $500/month for [TOOL] and spending 10 hours using it. My system will cut that time in half. Over 6 months, you'll save 120 hours. That's worth way more than $1,000."

---

### Show the math

Never assume they'll do the calculation. Do it for them.

"Let's do quick math:
- Current: 10 hours/week on [TASK]
- After: 1 hour/week
- Savings: 9 hours/week
- Annual savings: 468 hours
- At $50/hour (conservative): $23,400/year in time saved
- Investment: $1,000
- ROI: 23x"

---

### Payback period

"This pays for itself in [X] weeks/months."

Calculate:
- Price / monthly value saved = payback months
- $1,000 / ($200 saved/month) = 5 months payback

Anything under 6 months payback is an easy yes.

## Handling price pressure

### "Can you do better on price?"

"I price based on the value delivered, not the hours spent. I could lower scope, but I wouldn't lower price for the same work.

What I can do is [ALTERNATIVE: payment plan, smaller scope, etc.]."

---

### "Your competitor is cheaper."

"There's always someone cheaper. The question is: what does cheaper actually cost you?

I've had clients come to me after cheaper options failed. They spent $300, wasted 6 weeks, then spent $1,000 with me. Total cost: $1,300 and 2 months lost.

I'm not the cheapest. I'm the one who gets it right."

---

### "I don't have the budget right now."

"I understand. Here are two options:

1. We start smaller. $[LOWER PRICE] for [REDUCED SCOPE]. Get value now, expand later.

2. We wait until budget frees up. When does your next quarter/budget cycle start? I'll follow up then."

---

## When to raise prices

Signs you should charge more:

- Closing more than 70% of proposals
- Clients never negotiate
- You're too busy to take new work
- Your results exceed expectations consistently
- Competitors with similar work charge more

---

### How to raise prices

**New clients:** Just update your pricing. They don't know what you charged before.

**Existing clients:**

"Hey [NAME], wanted to give you a heads up. Starting [DATE], my pricing is increasing to reflect the results I'm delivering and demand for my work.

For you, as an existing client, I'm locking in your current rate through [DATE]. After that, any new projects will be at the new rate.

Appreciate you and happy to discuss any upcoming projects before the change."

---

## Pricing confidence

### Mindset shift

You're not charging for hours. You're charging for:
- Years of learning
- Speed of delivery
- Reliability of results
- Risk you take off their plate
- Opportunity cost of them doing it themselves

---

### Practice saying your price

Out loud. Until it feels natural.

"The investment is $1,000."

Say it 10 times. Get comfortable with silence after.

---

### Never apologize for your price

Don't say:
- "It's a bit expensive, but..."
- "I know that's a lot..."
- "Sorry, I wish I could do it for less..."

Do say:
- "Here's why it's worth it..."
- "The investment is X, and here's what you get..."
- "Most clients see ROI within [TIMEFRAME]..."

---

## Discounting rules

Avoid discounts. They train clients to ask for them and devalue your work.

**If you must discount:**

1. Never discount more than 10-15%
2. Always get something in return (faster timeline, testimonial, referral, case study)
3. Frame it as one-time or situational
4. Never discount without being asked

**Better than discounting:**
- Add value instead of reducing price
- Offer payment plan
- Reduce scope to fit budget
- Offer retainer rate for ongoing work
