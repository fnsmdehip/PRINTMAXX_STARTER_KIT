# Objection Handling Guide

Common objections and how to respond. Never argue. Always acknowledge, clarify, then address.

---

## Framework: LAER

**L** - Listen fully (don't interrupt)
**A** - Acknowledge their concern
**E** - Explore the real issue
**R** - Respond with specifics

---

## Price objections

### "It's too expensive."

**Acknowledge:** "I hear you. Budget matters."

**Explore:** "Just so I understand, is it that the price is higher than you expected, or that you're not sure the ROI is there?"

**Respond (if ROI concern):**
"Let's do the math. You said you spend [X] hours/week on this. At your hourly value, that's $[AMOUNT]/month. This project pays for itself in [X] months, then it's pure savings."

**Respond (if budget constraint):**
"I get it. Here's what we could do: start with the Bronze package at $[LOWER PRICE] and expand later once you see results. Would that work better?"

---

### "I can find cheaper."

**Acknowledge:** "You probably can."

**Explore:** "What matters more to you, lowest cost or getting it done right the first time?"

**Respond:**
"I've had clients come to me after trying cheaper options. They spent $300, it didn't work, then spent another $500 with me. Ended up costing $800 and 6 weeks of delay.

I'm not the cheapest, but I deliver. And I stand behind it with a guarantee."

---

### "Can you do it for less?"

**Acknowledge:** "I appreciate you asking."

**Explore:** "Is price the only thing holding you back, or are there other concerns?"

**Respond (if price is truly the blocker):**
"I can't lower the price, but I can adjust scope. We could start with [SMALLER VERSION] for $[LOWER PRICE]. That way you get value now and can expand later. Would that work?"

**Alternative:**
"My pricing reflects the quality and support I provide. I could offer a payment plan: 50% now, 50% in 30 days. Would that help?"

---

## Timing objections

### "I need to think about it."

**Acknowledge:** "Totally fair."

**Explore:** "What specifically do you want to think through? Maybe I can help clarify right now."

**Common underlying concerns:**
- Price (address directly)
- Not sure it'll work (offer guarantee/case study)
- Need approval from someone (get them on a call)
- Not urgent enough (highlight cost of delay)

**Respond:**
"I find that 'think about it' usually means there's something specific on your mind. Is it the price, the approach, or something else?"

---

### "Now's not a good time."

**Acknowledge:** "I get it, timing matters."

**Explore:** "What's making now difficult? And when would be better?"

**Respond (if it's genuinely bad timing):**
"No problem. Let's schedule a follow-up for [DATE] when things calm down. I'll send a reminder. Sound good?"

**Respond (if it's avoidance):**
"I hear that a lot. Usually it means the problem isn't painful enough yet. But here's the thing: the problem doesn't get easier to fix. The sooner you start, the sooner you get those hours back.

What would need to change for this to become a priority?"

---

### "I'm too busy to implement this."

**Acknowledge:** "That's exactly why you need this."

**Explore:** "What if I told you this would give you 10+ hours back per week?"

**Respond:**
"The whole point is to reduce your workload. Yes, there's some upfront time in kickoff and review. Maybe 2-3 hours total from you. But after that? You get [X] hours back every single week.

What's 2 hours now versus 10 hours saved every week for the next year?"

---

## Trust objections

### "How do I know it'll work?"

**Acknowledge:** "Smart question."

**Respond:**
"Two things. First, I have a guarantee: if the system doesn't work as specified, I fix it for free. If I can't make it work, full refund.

Second, here's a case study: [NAME] had the same concern. They were spending [X] hours on [TASK]. After we built their system, they're down to [Y] hours. I can connect you with them if you want to hear it directly."

---

### "I've been burned before."

**Acknowledge:** "That's frustrating. And I respect your caution."

**Explore:** "What happened? What went wrong?"

**Respond:**
"Thanks for sharing that. Here's how I'm different: [SPECIFIC DIFFERENCE].

Plus, I work in phases. You see results before paying the final invoice. And I include [X] days of support to make sure everything works.

I can't promise perfection, but I can promise I'll make it right if something doesn't work."

---

### "Can I talk to a reference?"

**Acknowledge:** "Absolutely."

**Respond:**
"I'll connect you with [CLIENT NAME]. They had a similar project. I'll make an intro today and you two can chat directly.

Any specific questions you want them to answer?"

---

## Scope objections

### "Can you add X?"

**Acknowledge:** "Good thinking."

**Explore:** "Tell me more about why [X] is important."

**Respond (if reasonable):**
"I can add that. It would be an extra $[AMOUNT] and add [TIME] to the timeline. Want me to include it in the proposal?"

**Respond (if scope creep):**
"That's actually a separate project. Let's nail this first, and we can discuss [X] as a phase two. That way you're not paying for everything upfront and can see how the first part works."

---

### "I want everything, but my budget is limited."

**Acknowledge:** "I get it. You want the full solution."

**Respond:**
"Here's what I'd suggest: start with the core [DELIVERABLE] that solves your biggest pain. Get that running, see results, then add on from there.

Phase 1: $[PRICE] gets you [CORE VALUE]
Phase 2 (later): $[PRICE] adds [ADDITIONAL FEATURES]

That way you spread out the investment and validate before expanding. Sound reasonable?"

---

## Decision-maker objections

### "I need to talk to my partner/team."

**Acknowledge:** "Makes sense."

**Explore:** "What do you think they'll want to know?"

**Respond:**
"Here's what I'll do: I'll send a one-page summary they can review. It'll cover what we discussed, the ROI math, and what the investment looks like.

Better yet, could we do a quick 15-minute call with them? I can answer questions directly and it usually speeds things up."

---

### "My partner handles this."

**Acknowledge:** "Got it."

**Respond:**
"Happy to chat with them directly. Would they be available for a quick call this week?

In the meantime, I'll send you a summary you can forward. What's the best way to reach them?"

---

## Competition objections

### "I'm talking to other vendors."

**Acknowledge:** "Smart to shop around."

**Explore:** "What criteria are you using to decide?"

**Respond:**
"Good. You should compare. Here's what I'd suggest looking for:

1. Do they have specific experience with [YOUR TYPE OF PROJECT]?
2. What's included in their support and revision policy?
3. Do they guarantee results?

I can't speak for others, but here's what you get with me: [KEY DIFFERENTIATORS].

What matters most to you?"

---

### "Another vendor is cheaper."

**Acknowledge:** "There's always someone cheaper."

**Explore:** "What's the difference in what they're offering?"

**Respond:**
"Price is one factor, but here's what I'd consider:

- What's their revision policy? (I include [X] rounds)
- What happens if something breaks? (I offer [SUPPORT PERIOD])
- Do they guarantee results? (I do)

You might save $200 upfront and spend $500 fixing issues later. I've seen it happen.

Up to you, but I'd look at total cost, not just sticker price."

---

## Objection prevention

Best way to handle objections: prevent them.

**Price:** Show ROI math before they ask
**Trust:** Share case studies early
**Timing:** Create urgency (limited spots, price increase)
**Scope:** Clarify what's included and excluded upfront
**Decision-maker:** Ask who's involved early in discovery

---

## Phrases to avoid

Never say:
- "Trust me"
- "To be honest..."
- "I understand, but..."
- "You're wrong about that"
- "That's not a problem"

Instead say:
- "Here's what I can promise..."
- "Here's what I've seen..."
- "I hear you. And..."
- "I see it differently. Here's why..."
- "Let me address that..."

---

## When to walk away

Not every objection should be overcome. Walk away if:

- They're not willing to pay fair value
- They don't respect your time
- The project is outside your expertise
- Red flags suggest they'll be difficult to work with
- They keep adding scope without budget

**How to walk away gracefully:**

"I appreciate the conversation, but I don't think I'm the right fit for this project. The scope/budget/timeline doesn't align with how I work.

I can recommend [SOMEONE ELSE] who might be better suited. Best of luck with the project."
