## Page 1: Why most morning routines fail

You've seen the headlines:

"Billionaires wake up at 4am."
"The 2-hour morning routine that changed my life."
"Meditation, journaling, exercise, cold showers, gratitude, visualization..."

Here's the problem: Those routines take forever. And they assume you have no job, no family, and unlimited willpower at 6am.

**The 3 reasons morning routines fail:**

1. **Too long.** 90-minute routines sound great in blog posts. They collapse by Tuesday.

2. **Too rigid.** "Must wake at 5am" breaks the first time you have a bad night.

3. **Too complicated.** 7 steps with journaling prompts and specific meditation apps? You'll forget half of it.

**What works instead:**

A routine short enough to do even when you're tired. Simple enough to remember without an app. Flexible enough to survive real life.

5 minutes. 3 steps. That's it.

## Page 2: The 3-step morning sequence

Do these three things in this order. Every morning. Before you touch your phone.

**Step 1: Breathe (1 minute)**

Sit on the edge of your bed. Feet on floor. Eyes closed.

Take 5 deep breaths. In through nose (4 seconds). Out through mouth (6 seconds).

That's it. 1 minute. You're not meditating. You're just breathing.

Why it works: This activates your parasympathetic nervous system. You start the day calm instead of reactive.

**Step 2: Decide (2 minutes)**

Ask yourself one question: "What's the one thing that would make today successful?"

Not 5 things. Not your whole to-do list. One thing.

Write it down. Paper or phone notes, doesn't matter. Just make it concrete.

Why it works: Decision fatigue is real. Making your most important decision first means you won't bury it under 47 emails.

**Step 3: Move (2 minutes)**

Stand up. Do something physical. Options:

- 20 jumping jacks
- 10 squats
- Walk to your kitchen and back twice
- Stretch for 2 minutes
- 10 push-ups

Not a workout. Just movement. Get blood flowing.

Why it works: Exercise releases dopamine. Even 2 minutes changes your energy for the first hour.

**Total time: 5 minutes.**

## Page 3: The anchor habit technique

The routine works. Making it stick is the hard part.

**The anchor habit method:**

Attach your morning routine to something you already do. This is called "habit stacking."

Your anchor = something you do every morning without thinking

**Examples:**

- "After I turn off my alarm, I do my breathing."
- "After I put my feet on the floor, I do my routine."
- "After I use the bathroom, I do my 5 minutes."

**The rule:**

Same anchor. Same sequence. Every day.

Your brain stops deciding whether to do it. It just happens after the anchor.

**What about weekends?**

Same routine. Different time is fine. Just keep the anchor.

The goal is consistency, not perfection. Miss a day? Do it the next day. Don't restart from scratch.

## Page 4: 7 variations for different schedules

**Variation 1: The parent version**

Kids wake you up? Do Step 1 (breathing) while they eat breakfast. Step 2 and 3 while they get ready. Total: 5 minutes, distributed.

**Variation 2: The commuter version**

Breathe in your car before starting it (1 minute). Decide on your one thing during the drive. Move when you park (walk around the car once).

**Variation 3: The work-from-home version**

Do all 3 steps before walking to your desk. The "commute" from bedroom to desk is your anchor.

**Variation 4: The early riser version**

You wake up early anyway? Expand each step. 5 minutes breathing. 5 minutes journaling. 10 minutes exercise. Same structure, longer duration.

**Variation 5: The traveling version**

Hotel room? Do all 3 steps on the edge of the bed. Works anywhere.

**Variation 6: The bad night version**

Slept terribly? Do the minimum: 3 breaths, 1 decision, touch your toes. That counts.

**Variation 7: The late sleeper version**

Wake up at 10am? Same routine. Time doesn't matter. Sequence does.

## Page 5: What to expect

**Week 1:**

You'll forget once or twice. That's normal.

You might feel silly doing breathing exercises. Do it anyway.

The "one thing" question might feel hard. That's the point.

**Week 2:**

It starts feeling automatic.

You notice when you skip it because your morning feels different.

Your "one thing" actually gets done more often.

**Week 3:**

You stop thinking about whether to do it.

You start noticing how you feel compared to before.

Some people start waking up looking forward to it.

**Week 4 and beyond:**

It's just what you do.

The 5 minutes feel valuable, not like a chore.

You might start adding to it naturally.
