## Page 1: The focus problem

You're not lazy. You're distracted.

The average knowledge worker checks email 74 times per day. They check their phone 96 times. They get interrupted every 3 minutes.

After each interruption, it takes 23 minutes to fully refocus.

Do the math. Most people never reach full focus. They spend all day in shallow work: emails, messages, quick tasks, putting out fires.

Deep work is rare. And it's where all the valuable output comes from.

**What is deep work?**

Cal Newport's definition: "Professional activity performed in a state of distraction-free concentration that pushes your cognitive capabilities to their limit."

In plain English: Focused work on hard things, without interruption.

**Why it matters:**

- Deep work produces 5-10x more output than shallow work
- It's the only way to learn hard things quickly
- It creates work that can't be easily replicated
- It makes you more valuable, not just busier

**The goal of this protocol:**

Get you 2 hours of deep work per day. That's 40 hours per month of real output.

Most people would take months to produce what you can do in that time.

## Page 2: The 4-step deep work framework

**Step 1: Define the block**

Pick a 90-minute block. Put it on your calendar like a meeting.

Why 90 minutes? Research shows this matches your natural focus rhythm (ultradian cycles). Longer is possible but harder. Shorter isn't enough to get deep.

Best times for most people:
- First thing in the morning (before email)
- Right after lunch
- Late afternoon (second wind)

**Step 2: Prepare the environment**

Before your block starts:

- Close all tabs except what you need
- Put phone in another room (not just face-down)
- Close email and Slack
- Have water nearby
- Go to the bathroom
- Tell people you're unavailable (if needed)

The goal: Remove every possible reason to stop.

**Step 3: Work the block**

Start the timer. Work on one thing only.

If you get distracted, notice it and return. Don't judge yourself. Just come back.

If you think of something else you need to do, write it on paper and return to work.

If you hit a wall, don't stop. Stare at the problem. Boredom often precedes breakthrough.

**Step 4: End cleanly**

When the timer ends, stop.

Review what you accomplished. Write it down.

Reward yourself (coffee, walk, snack). This trains your brain to associate deep work with good feelings.

Do NOT check email or phone immediately. Take 5 minutes first.

## Page 3: Phone jail setup

Your phone is the #1 deep work killer. Not because you're weak. Because it's designed by thousands of engineers to capture your attention.

You can't win through willpower. You need physical separation.

**Phone jail options:**

1. **Another room.** Put your phone in a drawer in another room. The friction of getting up is usually enough.

2. **Kitchen timer box.** Lock phone in a box with a kitchen timer lock. Can't open until time ends.

3. **Car.** Leave phone in your car. Works if you work from home.

4. **Phone locker.** Buy a timed lock box. Put phone inside. Set timer.

5. **Give it to someone.** "Don't give this back for 90 minutes."

**Why not "do not disturb"?**

Because you know the phone is there. The temptation remains. Your brain spends energy resisting.

Physical separation removes the option. Your brain stops thinking about it.

**What about emergencies?**

In 90 minutes, almost nothing is a real emergency. If you have kids at school, give the school your work number or a partner's number.

Real emergencies are rare. The fear of missing them is constant. Don't let fear run your focus.

## Page 4: The 90-minute rhythm

Why 90 minutes beats Pomodoro for deep work:

**Pomodoro (25 min work, 5 min break):**
- Good for shallow tasks
- Too choppy for deep thinking
- Just when you're getting somewhere, you stop
- Context switching every 30 minutes

**90-minute blocks:**
- Matches your natural ultradian rhythm
- Enough time to get past the "startup" phase
- Allows real immersion in hard problems
- One context per block

**The daily rhythm:**

Most people can do 2 deep work blocks per day. That's 3 hours.

Some can do 3 blocks (4.5 hours). Beyond that, quality drops.

**A realistic schedule:**

- 8:00-9:30: Deep work block 1
- 9:30-10:00: Break, shallow work
- 10:00-12:00: Meetings, email, admin
- 12:00-1:00: Lunch
- 1:00-2:30: Deep work block 2
- 2:30-5:00: Collaboration, shallow tasks

You still do email. You still have meetings. But your best hours go to your best work.

## Page 5: Environment design checklist

Your environment shapes your focus more than your willpower.

**Physical space:**

- [ ] Dedicated deep work location (same place each time)
- [ ] Chair and desk that don't cause pain
- [ ] Good lighting (natural light if possible)
- [ ] Comfortable temperature
- [ ] Minimal visual clutter in your line of sight

**Digital environment:**

- [ ] Browser: Close all tabs except active work
- [ ] Desktop: Hide everything except current project
- [ ] Notifications: All off during deep work (not just silenced)
- [ ] Email: Closed completely (not minimized)
- [ ] Chat apps: Closed or status set to unavailable

**Audio environment:**

- [ ] Noise-canceling headphones if in noisy space
- [ ] No music with lyrics (instrumentals only)
- [ ] Brown noise or white noise if silence is too quiet
- [ ] Or complete silence if that works for you

**Physical needs:**

- [ ] Water within reach
- [ ] Bathroom visited before starting
- [ ] Light snack if session is before a meal
- [ ] Temperature comfortable (a bit cool is better than warm)

## Page 6: The focus recovery technique

You will get distracted. Everyone does.

The skill isn't never getting distracted. It's coming back quickly.

**The 4R technique:**

1. **Recognize.** Notice that you're distracted. Don't judge, just notice.

2. **Record.** Write down what distracted you on paper. This gets it out of your head.

3. **Return.** Go back to exactly where you were before. Physically point at the line or task.

4. **Restate.** Say out loud (or in your head): "I'm working on [specific task] for the next [time remaining]."

**Common distractions and fixes:**

"I need to check something online."
- Write it on paper. Check after block ends.

"Someone might need me."
- They can wait 90 minutes. They always could.

"I'm stuck on this problem."
- Stay in the block. Work on a related sub-task. Or stare at it.

"I'm bored."
- Good. Boredom means your brain is adjusting. Push through.

"I had a great idea for something else."
- Write it down. Return to current task.

**The distraction audit:**

After each block, review your paper notes. Look for patterns.

If the same distraction keeps appearing, solve it outside of deep work. Change your environment, process, or tools.

## Page 7: What to expect

**Day 1-3:**

This will feel hard. Your brain is used to constant stimulation.

You might only focus for 30 minutes before wanting to stop. That's normal.

Do the full 90 anyway. Even if you're just sitting there.

**Day 4-7:**

It starts getting easier.

You'll notice you can go longer without thinking about your phone.

You might feel restless during breaks because you're used to constant input.

**Week 2:**

You'll start seeing results.

Projects move faster. You'll ship things you've been putting off.

The 90 minutes will feel more valuable than 3 hours of interrupted work.

**Week 3+:**

It becomes normal.

You'll feel wrong starting work without protecting the block.

You'll guard your deep work time like you guard important meetings.
