## Page 1: Why your pelvic floor matters

Your pelvic floor is a hammock of muscles at the bottom of your pelvis.

It holds up your bladder, uterus, and bowel. It controls when you go to the bathroom. It supports your core and posture.

When it's weak or tight, things go wrong:
- Leaking when you laugh, cough, sneeze, or exercise
- Sudden urgency to pee
- Lower back pain
- Feeling "heavy" down there

**Why kegels alone don't work:**

Kegels only squeeze. A healthy pelvic floor needs to:
1. Relax fully
2. Contract when needed
3. Coordinate with your breath
4. Work with your core muscles

This 7-day reset teaches all four.

## Page 2: Before you start

**The bathroom test:**

1. Next time you pee, try to stop mid-stream
2. If you can stop it, your muscles work
3. If you can slow it but not stop, they're weak
4. If nothing happens, they're very weak or disconnected

Don't do this test often. It's just a baseline check.

**Quick anatomy:**

Feel your sit bones (the bones you sit on). Your pelvic floor stretches between them, front to back.

The muscles wrap around:
- Your urethra (pee hole)
- Your vagina
- Your anus

When you "kegel," you're lifting all three areas up and in.

## Page 3: Day 1-2 - Find your floor

**Day 1: The awareness exercise**

1. Lie on your back, knees bent, feet flat
2. Place one hand on your lower belly
3. Breathe in slowly. Let your belly rise.
4. As you breathe out, gently lift your pelvic floor (like stopping pee)
5. Feel a gentle tightening under your hand? That's it.
6. Repeat 10 times

Time: 3 minutes

**Day 2: The counting hold**

1. Same position as Day 1
2. Breathe out and lift your pelvic floor
3. Hold for 3 seconds while breathing normally
4. Release for 3 seconds
5. Repeat 10 times

Time: 4 minutes

**Common mistake:** Squeezing your butt, belly, or thighs. Only your pelvic floor should work.

## Page 4: Day 3-4 - Learn to relax

A tight pelvic floor leaks too. Relaxation is half the solution.

**Day 3: The full release**

1. Sit on a rolled towel placed lengthwise (under your perineum)
2. Breathe in deeply. Feel your pelvic floor press into the towel.
3. This is a full release. Hold for 5 seconds.
4. Breathe out. Let your floor naturally lift slightly.
5. Repeat 10 times

Time: 4 minutes

**Day 4: The contract-release cycle**

1. Lie on your back, knees bent
2. Breathe out, lift and hold pelvic floor for 5 seconds
3. Breathe in, fully release for 5 seconds
4. The release is just as important as the lift
5. Repeat 10 times

Time: 4 minutes

**Why this matters:** Many women with leaks actually have tight muscles that can't fully contract because they never fully relax.

## Page 5: Day 5-6 - Build strength

**Day 5: Quick flicks**

1. Lie on your back or sit upright
2. Contract your pelvic floor quickly and firmly
3. Release immediately
4. Do 10 quick flicks
5. Rest 10 seconds
6. Repeat 3 sets

Time: 3 minutes

**Day 6: The elevator**

1. Imagine your pelvic floor has 3 floors
2. Breathe out, lift to floor 1 (light squeeze)
3. Lift to floor 2 (medium squeeze)
4. Lift to floor 3 (full squeeze)
5. Hold at floor 3 for 3 seconds
6. Lower back down: 3, 2, 1, release
7. Repeat 5 times

Time: 4 minutes

**Why both:** Quick flicks train the fast-twitch muscles (for coughs and sneezes). Elevators train endurance (for holding).

## Page 6: Day 7 - The daily routine

This is what you'll do every day going forward.

**The 2-minute maintenance routine:**

1. 10 contract-and-release cycles (30 seconds)
2. 10 quick flicks (30 seconds)
3. 5 elevator lifts (60 seconds)

Do it once per day. Pick a trigger:
- While brushing teeth
- Waiting for coffee to brew
- First thing when you sit at your desk

That's it. 2 minutes. Every day. Results in 4-6 weeks.

## Page 7: What to expect

**Week 1-2:**
- Better awareness of your pelvic floor
- Might feel sore (like any new exercise)
- No visible changes yet

**Week 3-4:**
- Fewer accidents
- Better control when you need to go
- More confidence during exercise

**Week 5-6:**
- Significant reduction in leaks
- Can laugh, jump, sneeze without worrying
- Stronger core overall

**If you're not improving by week 6:**
- See a pelvic floor physical therapist
- You might have a tight floor that needs release work first
- There could be an underlying issue that needs assessment
