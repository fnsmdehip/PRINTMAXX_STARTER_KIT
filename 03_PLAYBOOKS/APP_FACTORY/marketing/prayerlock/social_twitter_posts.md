# PrayerLock Twitter/X posts

20 posts: mix of tips and engagement hooks.

---

## Tips posts (10)

### Post 1
My screen time dropped from 6 hours to 2.5 hours per day.

The change: I blocked social media until after morning prayer.

Not willpower. A literal block.

Took 3 days to stop fighting it.

### Post 2
The order of your morning matters more than you think.

Phone first = reactive mode all day
Prayer first = intentional mode all day

I tested this for 60 days. The difference is real.

### Post 3
Unpopular opinion: App limits don't work.

You set them. You ignore them. You feel guilty.

What works: making the apps completely unavailable until you do the important thing first.

### Post 4
3 things that changed when I started praying before scrolling:

1. Morning anxiety dropped
2. Actually remember what I read in my Bible
3. Social media posts don't bother me as much

Took 2 weeks to notice. Worth it.

### Post 5
Your phone is designed by engineers whose job is to keep you scrolling.

Your prayer life doesn't stand a chance against billion-dollar algorithms.

Unless you remove the option entirely.

### Post 6
I asked 50 Christians when they pray.

43 said "when I remember" or "at night before bed."

Only 7 had a consistent morning routine.

The 7 all had one thing in common: they removed phone access until after prayer.

### Post 7
Hot take: The enemy doesn't need to attack you directly.

He just needs to keep you distracted.

Your phone is the most effective distraction device ever created.

Guard your mornings.

### Post 8
Average person checks their phone 96 times per day.

Average time spent in prayer: 3 minutes.

The math isn't mathing.

Something has to change.

### Post 9
I built an accountability system that works:

1. Set which apps get blocked
2. Set prayer time required
3. Can't access apps until done
4. Streak tracks consistency

Simple but effective.

### Post 10
Week 1: Frustrated that I can't check my phone
Week 2: Getting used to it
Week 3: Starting to enjoy the quiet
Week 4: Can't imagine going back

The discomfort is temporary. The peace is lasting.
