# Pricing Survey Template (Van Westendorp)

Finding the right price using the Price Sensitivity Meter.

**When to use:** Pre-launch pricing, before major price changes
**Target responses:** 100+ per segment
**Best for:** Understanding price perception, not just what people say they'll pay

---

## The Van Westendorp method

Four questions that reveal acceptable price range:

### Question 1: Too cheap

> At what price would you consider [product] to be priced so low that you'd question the quality?

$___

**Why this matters:** Reveals floor price below which credibility suffers.

---

### Question 2: Bargain

> At what price would you consider [product] to be a bargain - a great buy for the money?

$___

**Why this matters:** Sweet spot for value perception.

---

### Question 3: Getting expensive

> At what price would you consider [product] starting to get expensive - you'd still consider it, but you'd have to think about it?

$___

**Why this matters:** Where price resistance begins.

---

### Question 4: Too expensive

> At what price would you consider [product] to be so expensive that you wouldn't consider buying it?

$___

**Why this matters:** Ceiling price, absolute maximum.

---

## Analysis

### Plot the curves

X-axis: Price
Y-axis: Cumulative percentage of respondents

1. **Too cheap:** Cumulative % who said this price or lower is too cheap
2. **Not a bargain:** Cumulative % who said this price or higher is NOT a bargain (100% minus bargain)
3. **Not expensive:** Cumulative % who said this price or lower is NOT expensive
4. **Too expensive:** Cumulative % who said this price or higher is too expensive

### Key intersections

| Intersection | What it means | How to find |
|--------------|---------------|-------------|
| **OPP** (Optimal Price Point) | Where "too cheap" crosses "too expensive" | Best balance of volume and value |
| **IDP** (Indifference Price) | Where "bargain" crosses "expensive" | Equal buyers think it's cheap vs expensive |
| **PMC** (Point of Marginal Cheapness) | Where "too cheap" crosses "not a bargain" | Below this = quality concerns |
| **PME** (Point of Marginal Expensiveness) | Where "not expensive" crosses "too expensive" | Above this = losing significant buyers |

### Acceptable price range

**Range = PMC to PME**

This is your pricing flexibility zone.

## Alternative pricing research methods

### Gabor-Granger method

For understanding demand at specific prices.

> Would you buy [product] at $X?
- Definitely would buy
- Probably would buy
- Might or might not buy
- Probably would not buy
- Definitely would not buy

Test 3-5 price points. Plot demand curve.

---

### Conjoint analysis (advanced)

Show product bundles at different prices:

> Which would you prefer?

**Option A:**
- Feature X included
- Feature Y NOT included
- $10/month

**Option B:**
- Feature X NOT included
- Feature Y included
- $8/month

**Option C:**
- Both features included
- $15/month

Statistical analysis reveals value of each feature and price sensitivity.

---

### Simple A/B pricing

Actually test prices:
- Show different prices to different segments
- Measure conversion rates
- Calculate revenue optimization

**Caution:** Can upset customers who discover price differences.

## Pricing strategy decisions

### Based on Van Westendorp results

| If your goal is... | Price at... |
|--------------------|-------------|
| Maximum market penetration | PMC (floor) |
| Revenue optimization | OPP to IDP |
| Premium positioning | Near PME |
| Testing price sensitivity | Multiple points in range |

---

### Tiered pricing discovery

Ask these for each tier:

**Basic tier:**
> For a version with [basic features], what would be too cheap / bargain / expensive / too expensive?

**Pro tier:**
> For a version with [pro features], what would be too cheap / bargain / expensive / too expensive?

Compare ranges to set tier prices and gaps.

---

## Common mistakes

1. **Asking only "what would you pay"** - People say what sounds reasonable, not what they'd actually pay
2. **Not enough responses** - Need 100+ for reliable curves
3. **Ignoring segments** - Power users have different price tolerance
4. **Testing in vacuum** - Always provide context (competitor prices, feature list)
5. **Taking results literally** - Van Westendorp gives range, not exact price
6. **Not validating** - Actual A/B test beats survey every time
