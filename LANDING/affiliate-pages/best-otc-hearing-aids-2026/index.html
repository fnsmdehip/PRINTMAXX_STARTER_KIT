Here is the context most hearing aid review sites do not give you: the hearing aid industry ran a clinic-capture playbook for 70 years. $5,000-$7,000 pairs, mandatory audiologist fittings, insurance that barely covers anything. The FDA's 2022 OTC rule broke that open. Brands can now sell hearing aids directly to adults with mild-to-moderate loss — no prescription, no clinic, no markup.

The result: Jabra (a professional headset company) entered the market. Bose licensed tech to Lexie. Sony launched two OTC aids. Eargo (founded by a French ENT surgeon) went direct-to-consumer. The options below are the best of what that competitive market produced as of early 2026.

Who OTC aids are for: Adults 18+ with perceived mild to moderate hearing loss. If you struggle to hear conversations in noisy restaurants, miss consonants on TV, or ask people to repeat themselves regularly — that is the profile. If you suspect severe or profound loss, see an audiologist first; OTC aids are not designed for that range.

What to Know Before You Buy an OTC Hearing Aid

The 2022 FDA OTC Ruling — What It Actually Changed

Before August 2022, you legally had to see a licensed audiologist to obtain a hearing aid in the US. The FDA's new OTC category removed that requirement for adults with mild to moderate hearing loss. Brands could start selling hearing aids directly on Amazon, their websites, and in retail stores.

This did not lower standards. OTC hearing aids still require FDA clearance and must meet technical specifications for maximum output levels, frequency response, and self-fitting accuracy. The difference is that you do the fitting yourself using a smartphone app, rather than paying an audiologist $1,500-$2,500 for the same process.

How to Know If OTC Is Right for You

OTC aids are FDA-cleared for adults with perceived mild to moderate hearing loss — meaning you believe you have this level of loss based on your experience. Mild: you miss some words in conversation. Moderate: you frequently ask people to repeat themselves, struggle with TV at normal volume, and miss high-pitched sounds like birds or doorbells.

Severe loss (you cannot hear loud speech in quiet environments) and profound loss (almost total deafness) require prescription aids fit by an audiologist. If you are unsure, the National Institute on Deafness and Other Communication Disorders offers a free online screening at https://www.nidcd.nih.gov/.

Important: Sudden hearing loss — hearing loss that develops within 72 hours — is a medical emergency. See a physician immediately. This is not something OTC hearing aids address; it requires urgent treatment to preserve function.

What "Self-Fitting" Actually Means

Modern OTC self-fitting is not guesswork. The better brands (Jabra, Eargo, Sony) run a short in-app hearing screener — typically 10-20 tones at different frequencies and volumes. You respond to what you can and cannot hear. The app then sets an initial amplification profile based on your responses, similar to how audiologists use audiograms to set prescription aids. You then fine-tune in real-world environments over the following weeks.

Medicare and HSA Coverage

Traditional Medicare does not cover hearing aids. Some Medicare Advantage plans include hearing aid benefits — call your plan or check your Evidence of Coverage under the "hearing benefits" section. HSA (health savings account) and FSA (flexible spending account) funds can typically be used for OTC hearing aids; verify with your account administrator. VA benefits sometimes cover hearing aids for veterans with service-connected hearing loss.

Frequently Asked Questions

Are OTC hearing aids as good as prescription hearing aids?

For mild to moderate hearing loss, yes — in many cases. The FDA's 2022 OTC regulation cleared devices that meet the same technical standards as prescription aids. The main difference is fitting precision: prescription aids involve audiologist customization with real-ear measurement (REM), which is genuinely valuable for severe loss. For moderate loss, self-fitting OTC aids like Jabra Enhance Plus and Eargo 7 perform comparably in real-world tests, and cost 60-80% less.

How much do OTC hearing aids cost vs prescription?

OTC hearing aids range from $200 to $1,500 per pair. Prescription hearing aids typically cost $4,000 to $7,000 per pair, with audiologist fitting fees on top. The savings are real for mild-moderate loss. For severe loss, the extra precision of a clinical fitting may justify the premium.

Does Medicare cover OTC hearing aids?

Traditional Medicare (Part A and B) does not cover hearing aids, OTC or prescription. Some Medicare Advantage plans include a hearing aid benefit — typically $500-$2,500 per ear every 2-3 years. Always check your specific plan's Evidence of Coverage document before buying. OTC aids are sometimes covered under HSA/FSA accounts.

What is the return policy on OTC hearing aids?

Most OTC brands offer 30-45 day returns: Jabra Enhance gives 100 days, MDHearing Aid gives 45 days, Eargo gives 45 days, Sony (via Amazon) gives 30 days, Lexie B2 gives 45 days. Buy during a period where you have time to test them thoroughly in varied environments — restaurants, TV, phone calls, outdoor conversations — before the window closes.
