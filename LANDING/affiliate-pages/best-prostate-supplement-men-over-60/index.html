Ingredient Evidence — What the Research Actually Says

Before buying any prostate supplement, know what's actually studied and at what doses.

Ingredient Dose Studied Evidence Level What the Research Shows
Beta-sitosterol 60–130mg/day Strong BJU meta-analysis (519 men): significant improvement in urinary scores and peak flow. Most consistent evidence in this category.
Pygeum africanum 100–200mg/day Moderate Cochrane review: modest improvement vs placebo. Studies were small and short. Used in Europe medicinally for decades.
Zinc 10–15mg/day Moderate Prostate tissue has high zinc concentration. Deficiency linked to DHT elevation. Zinc citrate or picolinate absorbs better than oxide.
Pumpkin seed oil 320–640mg/day Moderate Some RCT evidence for urinary symptoms. Contains phytosterols including beta-sitosterol. Effect appears modest but real.
Saw palmetto 320mg/day (studied) Weak NIH STEP trial (225 men): no significant improvement vs placebo at standard dose. Mixed evidence in smaller studies. Popular but less supported.
Lycopene 15–30mg/day Weak Epidemiological link to lower prostate cancer risk in tomato-rich diets. Direct supplementation evidence is weaker. Still a useful antioxidant.

⚠️ See a Doctor First

If you're having urinary symptoms — frequent urination at night, weak stream, difficulty starting or stopping — those symptoms can be from BPH, but they can also be from prostate cancer, bladder issues, or medication side effects. Get evaluated by a urologist before relying on supplements. PSA testing, DRE, and urine flow tests are the right starting point. Supplements are not a substitute for diagnosis.

Frequently Asked Questions

Answers based on clinical literature, not marketing copy.

ProstaGenix ranks highest for men over 60 because it contains a clinically meaningful dose of beta-sitosterol (600mg) — the ingredient most studied for prostate health. Most budget supplements use saw palmetto at sub-therapeutic doses, which has weaker clinical evidence at typical label amounts.
The evidence is mixed. A large NIH-funded trial (STEP study) found saw palmetto at 320mg/day produced no significant improvement over placebo for BPH symptoms. However, beta-sitosterol — a compound in saw palmetto and other plants — has stronger evidence at 60-130mg/day in the form of plant sterols. Many supplements use inadequate doses of both.
Look for: Beta-sitosterol (60-130mg minimum), Pygeum africanum extract (100-200mg), Zinc (10-15mg as zinc citrate or picolinate), and Pumpkin seed oil. Avoid proprietary blends that hide individual ingredient amounts — you can't tell if the dose is therapeutic. Any supplement that won't show you exact ingredient amounts is a red flag.
Most prostate supplements are generally well-tolerated, but always inform your doctor what you're taking. Saw palmetto may have mild blood-thinning properties. Zinc at high doses (over 40mg/day) can interfere with some antibiotics. Always clear new supplements with your physician, especially if you take prescription medications for blood pressure, cholesterol, or blood thinning.
Most clinical studies showing benefit used 6-12 week timeframes. If you're not noticing any change in urinary symptoms after 90 days of consistent use at proper dosage, the product likely isn't working for you. Don't be misled by "30-day trials" — real prostate health improvement takes longer. Budget for at least 90 days before judging a supplement.
Prescription medications like finasteride (Proscar) and dutasteride (Avodart) are 5-alpha reductase inhibitors with strong, consistent clinical evidence for BPH and prostate size reduction. Alpha blockers like tamsulosin (Flomax) rapidly relax the bladder neck and improve urine flow. Supplements work through different mechanisms and have less robust overall evidence. If your symptoms are severe, see a urologist before relying on supplements.
