IV vs. Oral vs. Subcutaneous NAD+

The delivery method debate is the biggest question in NAD+ therapy:

  • IV infusion: 100% bioavailability. 2-4 hour sessions. $250-750 each. Most studied clinically. The gold standard for delivery but impractical for regular use.
  • Subcutaneous injection: Self-administered at home. Good bioavailability. $200-400/month. Growing evidence base. Best balance of cost and effectiveness.
  • Oral NMN/NR: $40-60/month. Convenient. Lower bioavailability. NR (Tru Niagen) has the most human trial data of oral options. NMN is popular but less studied in humans.

What NAD+ Actually Does

NAD+ is a coenzyme in every living cell. It's required for cellular energy production (mitochondrial function), DNA repair (PARP activation), and gene regulation (sirtuin activation). Levels decline roughly 50% between age 40 and 60. The theory: restoring NAD+ levels can partially reverse age-related metabolic decline.

Frequently Asked Questions

Many report increased energy within 1-2 weeks of starting IV or subcutaneous NAD+. Oral NMN/NR effects tend to be more gradual over 4-8 weeks. Cognitive and sleep benefits may take 4-12 weeks to become apparent.
Depends on your goals and budget. The evidence for NAD+ precursors (NR, NMN) on biomarkers is promising but not conclusive for clinical outcomes. If energy and cognitive function are your primary concerns, a trial of oral NR at $40-50/month is low-risk. IV at $750/session requires more conviction.
NR (nicotinamide riboside) has more human clinical trial data. NMN (nicotinamide mononucleotide) is one step closer to NAD+ in the biosynthetic pathway. In practice, both raise NAD+ levels. NR has the regulatory advantage of being a recognized dietary ingredient. NMN's status varies by country.
IV NAD+ can cause flushing, nausea, and chest tightness during infusion (rate-dependent). Subcutaneous injection may cause local site reactions. Oral NR/NMN is generally well-tolerated. All forms can cause mild nausea initially.
