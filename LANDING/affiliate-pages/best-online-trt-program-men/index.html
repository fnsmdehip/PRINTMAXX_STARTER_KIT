Important: TRT is Not for Everyone

Testosterone replacement therapy requires a clinical diagnosis of hypogonadism (low T), confirmed through blood tests showing total testosterone below roughly 300 ng/dL on two separate morning draws. Symptoms alone are not sufficient. Fatigue, low libido, and mood changes can stem from sleep issues, stress, thyroid dysfunction, or a dozen other causes. A responsible TRT clinic will require bloodwork before prescribing anything. If a clinic offers testosterone without labs, that is a red flag, not a feature. TRT also suppresses natural production and fertility. Talk to a physician about the full picture before committing.

What You Need to Know Before Starting Online TRT

The Bloodwork Requirement Is Non-Negotiable

Every reputable TRT clinic requires bloodwork before prescribing testosterone. The clinical standard for diagnosing hypogonadism is two separate morning blood draws showing total testosterone below approximately 300 ng/dL (though reference ranges and clinical judgment vary). Some telehealth clinics accept one confirmed low reading with symptoms present, but the responsible ones will always require lab work. The minimum panel should include: total testosterone, free testosterone, SHBG, estradiol, LH, FSH, CBC, full metabolic panel, PSA, and a lipid panel. More thorough clinics add thyroid markers, prolactin, cortisol, DHEA-S, and insulin.

Injections vs. Gels vs. Pellets

Injectable testosterone cypionate is the standard for online TRT programs. It is the most cost-effective option, allows precise dose adjustments, and produces stable blood levels when administered at the right frequency (most protocols use twice-weekly subcutaneous injections now rather than the older once-weekly intramuscular approach). Topical gels avoid needles but cost more, absorb unpredictably between individuals, and carry a real transfer risk to partners and children through skin contact. Pellets are implanted every 3-6 months by a provider and offer convenience, but they do not allow easy dose adjustments and are the most expensive option per year.

The Fertility Question

Exogenous testosterone suppresses the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, which in turn shuts down natural testosterone production and sperm production. For most men, this means near-zero sperm counts while on TRT. This is typically reversible after stopping treatment, but recovery takes months and is not guaranteed, especially with prolonged use or in older men. If you want children in the future, this is a conversation you need to have with your provider before starting. Alternatives like clomiphene citrate or enclomiphene can raise testosterone without suppressing fertility, though they work through a different mechanism and may not be appropriate for all patients.

Ongoing Monitoring Matters

TRT is not a one-and-done prescription. It requires regular blood monitoring (typically every 3-6 months) to track testosterone levels, hematocrit (red blood cell concentration), estradiol, PSA, and liver function. Elevated hematocrit is one of the most common complications and may require therapeutic phlebotomy (essentially donating blood to bring levels down). Estrogen management is another ongoing consideration, as testosterone converts to estradiol through the aromatase enzyme. Any clinic that prescribes TRT without scheduling follow-up labs is not providing adequate care.

The Bottom Line

Online TRT has made testosterone replacement therapy accessible to men who would have previously faced months-long waits for endocrinology appointments, insurance denials, and provider gatekeeping. That is genuinely useful. But accessibility does not change the medical reality: TRT is a hormone therapy with real benefits, real side effects, and real long-term commitments. It requires bloodwork, monitoring, and honest conversations with a provider about whether it is the right intervention for your specific situation.

For most men getting started, Hims offers the best combination of brand medications, included labs, and a polished user experience at $179/month. If budget is the primary concern, Fountain TRT gets you in the door for $35 and keeps monthly costs under $150. Ro is the strongest choice for men with insurance who want help navigating coverage. And Marek Health is the clear pick for men who want the deepest clinical approach and are willing to pay premium prices for it.

Whatever clinic you choose, the fundamentals do not change. Get your bloodwork done. Take the time to understand your results. Ask your provider questions about side effects, fertility, and long-term monitoring. And do not rush the decision. The right TRT program is the one that treats your specific clinical situation with appropriate medical rigor, not the one with the best marketing.
