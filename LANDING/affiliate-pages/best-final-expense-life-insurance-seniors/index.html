Gerber Life offers guaranteed issue coverage — no health questions, no medical exam, cannot be declined. If you are between 50 and 80 and alive when you apply, you are approved. This is the right product for seniors with serious conditions that would cause denial or exclusions on simplified issue policies: recent cancer diagnosis, heart failure, kidney disease, oxygen dependency, nursing home residency.

The tradeoff is the 2-year graded death benefit: if the insured passes within the first 2 policy years, the beneficiary receives the premiums paid plus 10% interest — not the full face value. After 2 years, the full death benefit pays. This is standard for guaranteed issue products. It is not a scam; it is how insurers manage adverse selection.

Gerber Life (yes, the baby food company's insurance division) has strong brand recognition and an A AM Best rating. Premiums are higher than simplified issue for the same coverage, but for seniors who cannot qualify for anything else, it is the option that matters.

What You Need to Know Before Buying Final Expense Insurance

Simplified Issue vs Guaranteed Issue — Which Do You Need?

Simplified issue requires health questions but no exam. You can be declined, but if approved, premiums are lower and there is typically no waiting period. Most people with common controlled conditions (hypertension, diabetes, arthritis) qualify for simplified issue.

Guaranteed issue cannot decline you. No health questions. The tradeoffs: higher premiums, lower max coverage ($25,000 typically), and a 2-year graded benefit period. Only choose guaranteed issue if you have been declined for simplified issue, or if you have serious conditions that would cause denial.

What "Whole Life" Means for Final Expense Policies

Final expense policies are whole life insurance. This means three things: (1) premiums never increase after issue, regardless of age or health changes, (2) coverage never expires — the policy stays active as long as premiums are paid, (3) the policy builds cash value over time that you can borrow against or surrender. Compare this to term life, which expires after a set period and has no cash value.

Watch for this scam: Some direct mail marketing for "senior burial insurance" has deliberately low-ball headline rates that only apply to the smallest coverage amount and youngest age bracket. Always ask for the exact monthly premium for your specific age, coverage amount, and health class before making any decision. Reputable carriers will give you this in writing before you agree to anything.

Is Final Expense Insurance Worth It?

It depends on your alternative. If you have $15,000-$25,000 in savings earmarked specifically for final expenses, you may not need insurance. If that cash does not exist — or if you do not want to tie up savings that your spouse may need — final expense insurance makes the math work for most seniors.

A 65-year-old male paying $57/month for $10,000 in coverage will have paid $6,840 in premiums after 10 years. If he lives to 85, he will have paid $13,680 for $10,000 coverage — technically underwater on a pure ROI basis. But insurance is not an investment. It is a transfer of risk. The question is whether your family can absorb a $15,000 unplanned expense at an emotionally difficult time — not whether you will "come out ahead."

Frequently Asked Questions

What is final expense life insurance?

Final expense insurance is a small whole life policy — typically $5,000 to $25,000 in coverage — designed to cover burial costs, funeral expenses, and remaining medical bills. Premiums never increase, coverage never expires, and it builds cash value over time.

Can I get life insurance at 80?

Yes. Most final expense insurers accept applications up to age 80 or 85. Coverage amounts are smaller and premiums are higher at advanced ages, but coverage is available. Gerber Life accepts guaranteed issue applications through age 80. Mutual of Omaha accepts simplified issue through age 85.

How much final expense insurance do I need?

The average funeral and burial in the US costs $12,000-$20,000 including burial plot, casket, service, and headstone. Cremation costs $3,000-$8,000. Add anticipated remaining medical bills and any small debts you want covered. Most financial planners suggest $15,000-$25,000 as a reasonable final expense coverage target for full burial, $10,000 for cremation with a service.

Does final expense insurance cover cremation?

Yes. Final expense insurance pays a cash death benefit to your named beneficiary. They can use it for whatever the family decides — cremation, traditional burial, celebration of life, or any remaining expenses. The money is not restricted to a specific use.
