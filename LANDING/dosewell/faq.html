About GLP-1 medications

GLP-1 receptor agonists are a class of prescription medications that mimic a natural hormone called glucagon-like peptide-1. This hormone regulates appetite and blood sugar. These drugs were first developed to treat type 2 diabetes (brand names like Ozempic) and were later approved for weight management (Wegovy, Zepbound) after clinical trials showed significant weight loss. They work primarily by reducing hunger, slowing gastric emptying, and improving how your body handles blood sugar.

Clinical trials showed participants lost an average of 15% of body weight with semaglutide 2.4mg (STEP trials) and up to 22% with tirzepatide 15mg (SURMOUNT-1) over approximately 68-72 weeks, when combined with diet and exercise changes. These are averages across thousands of patients. Some lost more, some lost less. Individual results depend on starting weight, diet, activity level, dosage, genetics, and many other factors. Dosewell does not guarantee specific weight loss outcomes because they genuinely vary from person to person.

Brand-name medications (Ozempic, Wegovy, Mounjaro, Zepbound) are manufactured by specific pharmaceutical companies (Novo Nordisk, Eli Lilly) and are individually FDA-approved. They typically cost $1,000-1,500/month without insurance.

Compounded versions are prepared by FDA-registered 503B outsourcing facilities (compounding pharmacies) using the same active pharmaceutical ingredient. They are legal to produce during periods of drug shortage or when a provider determines a patient needs a specific dosage form. Compounded medications are not FDA-approved as finished drugs, though the active ingredients and the facility are regulated. This is how we offer the medication at a lower price point.

Common (reported by many patients, especially early on): Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, constipation, stomach pain, reduced appetite, headache, fatigue, and injection site reactions. These typically improve over the first 4-8 weeks as your body adjusts and as the dose is gradually increased.

Less common: Dizziness, bloating, gas, heartburn, hair thinning (temporary in some patients), gallstones.

Rare but serious: Pancreatitis (inflammation of the pancreas), gallbladder problems, kidney injury, allergic reactions. GLP-1 medications carry an FDA boxed warning about the risk of thyroid C-cell tumors, which were observed in rodent studies. It is unknown if this risk applies to humans, but these medications are contraindicated in patients with a personal or family history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2 syndrome.

Your prescribing physician will discuss all risks specific to your medical history during your consultation.

Research shows that most patients regain some or all of the weight lost after discontinuing GLP-1 medication. A study published in Diabetes, Obesity and Metabolism found that participants regained approximately two-thirds of their lost weight within one year of stopping semaglutide. This is not unique to GLP-1 medications. It reflects how the body's weight regulation works. This is why lifestyle changes (diet and exercise) alongside medication are important, and why some patients choose to remain on a maintenance dose long-term. Your doctor will discuss a sustainable long-term plan with you.

Yes. GLP-1 medications are administered as a subcutaneous (under the skin) injection once per week. Common injection sites are the abdomen, thigh, or upper arm. The needle is very thin and most patients describe it as nearly painless, similar to an insulin injection. Your medication kit includes detailed instructions, and your care team can walk you through the process over video or messaging if needed. If you have needle anxiety, discuss this with your physician.

Drug interactions exist with GLP-1 medications. They can affect the absorption of oral medications because they slow gastric emptying. This is particularly relevant for oral contraceptives, antibiotics, and other time-sensitive medications. You must disclose ALL current medications, supplements, and over-the-counter drugs during your health assessment. Your prescribing physician will evaluate potential interactions and may advise timing adjustments. Do not start GLP-1 medication without telling your doctor about everything you take.

Medical questions

The clinical trials that showed 15-22% weight loss included diet and exercise as part of the protocol. The medication alone produces results, but combining it with healthy eating and regular physical activity typically produces better outcomes and helps maintain weight loss if you eventually stop the medication. Your care team can provide general guidance on nutrition and exercise alongside your treatment.

This varies by patient. Some patients take GLP-1 medication for a defined period (6-12 months) to reach a target weight, then taper off while maintaining lifestyle changes. Others may benefit from longer-term or indefinite treatment. Research indicates that weight regain is common after stopping. Your physician will discuss the best approach for your specific situation, including a plan for if and when to discontinue.

GLP-1 medications were originally developed for type 2 diabetes, so yes, they may be appropriate. However, if you are already on diabetes medication (especially insulin or sulfonylureas), your doctor needs to coordinate the GLP-1 with your existing treatment to avoid dangerous drops in blood sugar. Disclose all diabetes medications during your health assessment. Your primary care doctor or endocrinologist should also be informed.

Cardiovascular safety has been studied for GLP-1 medications. The SELECT trial showed semaglutide reduced major cardiovascular events by 20% in patients with overweight/obesity and established cardiovascular disease. However, these medications may affect heart rate and blood pressure. Disclose all heart conditions and medications during your assessment. Your physician will evaluate whether GLP-1 is safe given your specific cardiac history.

No. GLP-1 medications are not recommended during pregnancy or for patients planning to become pregnant. Animal studies have shown potential harm to the fetus. You should discontinue GLP-1 medication at least 2 months before a planned pregnancy (some guidelines say longer). If you become pregnant while taking GLP-1 medication, stop the medication and contact your physician immediately.
