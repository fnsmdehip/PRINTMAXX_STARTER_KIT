How Rockefeller Money Killed Electrotherapy (And It's Coming Back)

2026-03-28 · PEMF Series, Part 2 of 2
In 1910, a single report funded by two of America's wealthiest foundations closed nearly half of all medical schools and buried electromagnetic medicine for a century. The FDA has been quietly vindicating it ever since.

The setup

If you read Part 1, you know that PEMF therapy has 17 RCTs in osteoarthritis, FDA-cleared bone healing devices, Medicare-reimbursed wound healing applications, and promising preclinical data in sepsis and neurological conditions. You also know that most physicians have never heard of it, or dismiss it as alternative medicine.

That gap between evidence and adoption didn't happen by accident. It has a specific origin, a specific date, and specific people who funded it. Understanding that history isn't conspiracy theorizing. It's understanding how medical institutions work, what incentives drive them, and why certain therapies get adopted while others with equal or better evidence don't.

The Flexner Report: what it actually did

In 1910, a schoolteacher named Abraham Flexner published a report that would reshape medical education in North America for the next century and counting. He wasn't a physician. He wasn't a scientist. He was an educator who had written a book about American colleges, which caught the attention of the Carnegie Foundation for the Advancement of Teaching.

The Carnegie Foundation hired Flexner to survey every medical school in the United States and Canada. He visited 155 schools over 18 months. His report documented what he found: overcrowded classrooms, inadequate laboratories, minimal clinical training, and diploma mills that graduated physicians with almost no hands-on experience.

On this point, Flexner was right. American medical education in 1910 was a mess. Many schools admitted anyone who could pay tuition and graduated them regardless of competence. Something needed to change.

What changed went far beyond quality control.

Who paid for it

The report was commissioned by the Carnegie Foundation. But the implementation, the actual closing and consolidation of schools, was driven by funding decisions from the Rockefeller Foundation's General Education Board.

John D. Rockefeller had a specific vision for medicine. He was the primary benefactor of the University of Chicago and a major funder of institutions that practiced what Flexner called "scientific medicine," meaning laboratory-based, pharmaceutical-oriented, German-model medical education. The Rockefeller Foundation directed funding toward schools that adopted this model and away from schools that didn't.

This wasn't a shadowy conspiracy. It was explicit philanthropic strategy, documented in foundation records. Rockefeller's advisors, particularly Frederick T. Gates (a Baptist minister turned business advisor, not a physician), believed that medicine should be built on chemistry and pharmacology. Modalities that didn't fit that framework, regardless of their clinical results, were classified as "sectarian" and defunded.

What got closed

In the decade following the Flexner Report, the number of medical schools in the United States dropped from 155 to 66. Nearly half were closed or merged. The casualties weren't random.

Schools that taught homeopathy, naturopathy, eclectic medicine, and electrotherapy were disproportionately targeted. Of the roughly 20 homeopathic medical colleges operating in 1910, only 2 survived, and only by converting to conventional curricula. Every eclectic medical school closed. Naturopathic schools were excluded entirely from the "legitimate" medical education framework.

Schools that served Black communities were devastated. Of the 7 Black medical schools operating in 1910, only 2 survived: Howard and Meharry. Flexner explicitly recommended closing the rest, arguing that Black physicians should be trained primarily in hygiene and sanitation rather than surgery or specialized medicine.

The schools that thrived were the ones aligned with the Rockefeller-Carnegie funding model: Johns Hopkins, Harvard, Columbia, the University of Chicago. These became the templates for all American medical education going forward.

What modalities died with the schools

When the schools closed, their curricula died with them. The specific modalities that were eliminated from mainstream medical education included:

  • Electrotherapy: The use of electrical currents and electromagnetic fields to treat pain, inflammation, and various conditions. This was a recognized medical specialty in the late 1800s with its own journals and professional societies.
  • Hydrotherapy: Therapeutic use of water (temperature, pressure, mineral content) for treatment of musculoskeletal and circulatory conditions.
  • Manual medicine: Osteopathic and chiropractic techniques for musculoskeletal treatment (osteopathy survived by adopting pharmaceutical curriculum; chiropractic was excluded entirely).
  • Botanical medicine: Systematic use of plant-derived compounds, which the eclectic schools had developed extensive pharmacopeias for.
  • Homeopathy: Highly diluted preparations, which remains controversial but had a large institutional presence in 1910.

The key point: these modalities weren't eliminated because they were tested and found to fail. They were eliminated because the schools that taught them lost funding, and the schools that survived didn't teach them. The modalities were guilty by institutional association, not by evidence.

Pacemakers alone have saved millions of lives. They work by delivering electrical impulses to cardiac tissue. That's electrotherapy. The word was just too tainted by 1910's institutional politics to use, so cardiology pretended it was something new.

The "not yet proven" trap

The Flexner Report made a specific logical error that persists in medical institutions today: it conflated "not yet proven" with "disproven."

In 1910, electrotherapy wasn't disproven. It was unproven by the standards Flexner considered legitimate, which meant German-model laboratory science. The clinical observations of electrotherapy practitioners weren't wrong; they just hadn't been validated through the specific institutional framework Flexner demanded.

But "unproven" and "disproven" lead to very different institutional responses. "Unproven" means "we should test this." "Disproven" means "we should stop doing this." Flexner treated them as the same thing, and the funding apparatus he helped create enforced that conflation for a century.

The result: modalities that were merely unproven in 1910 were treated as debunked, excluded from medical schools, excluded from research funding, and excluded from the cultural vocabulary of medicine. By the time the evidence caught up (bone stimulators in the 1970s, rTMS in the 2000s), the institutional bias was so entrenched that each vindication was treated as a surprising exception rather than a predictable correction.

Modern institutional groupthink

The Flexner Report was 116 years ago. You'd think the institutional apparatus would have self-corrected by now. It hasn't, and we have data showing why.

What the scoping reviews found

Scoping reviews examining decision-making in hospital settings have identified pervasive groupthink in clinical teams. The finding is striking: only four empirical studies in the research literature have directly examined groupthink dynamics in healthcare settings. Four. For a phenomenon that directly affects patient outcomes in every hospital on the planet.

The studies that do exist paint a consistent picture: clinical teams exhibit classic groupthink markers. Conformity pressure. Illusion of invulnerability ("our standard of care is correct"). Collective rationalization ("if PEMF worked, we'd already be using it"). Self-censorship among junior members who might suggest non-standard approaches. Stereotyping of out-groups ("that's alternative medicine").

The hierarchy problem

Medicine is one of the most hierarchical professions in existence. Attending physicians set the standard. Residents follow it. Nurses execute it. The hierarchy exists for good reasons, patient safety requires clear chains of command, but it has a side effect: novel therapies can only enter practice from the top.

A resident who reads the 17 PEMF osteoarthritis RCTs and suggests trying it for a patient will be told to follow the standard protocol. A nurse who mentions PEMF will be redirected to "evidence-based" options (meaning options that are already in the guidelines). The only person who can introduce PEMF is an attending physician willing to go off-protocol, accept the liability, and defend the decision to colleagues and administrators.

The number of attendings willing to do that for a therapy they've never been taught, that their hospital doesn't stock, that insurance won't reimburse, and that their colleagues will raise eyebrows at, is approximately zero. Not because they're closed-minded. Because the incentive structure makes it irrational.

The liability cage

If a physician prescribes an FDA-approved drug for an FDA-approved indication and the patient has a bad outcome, the physician is legally protected by the standard of care. They followed the guidelines. The drug was approved. The outcome was a known risk.

If a physician prescribes PEMF for sepsis (an indication with strong preclinical evidence but no FDA clearance, no guideline support, and no reimbursement), and the patient has a bad outcome, the physician is exposed. It doesn't matter that PEMF is noninvasive. It doesn't matter that the mouse data is compelling. What matters is that they deviated from the standard of care, and in the American malpractice system, deviation from standard is the primary metric of negligence.

This creates a cage where the standard of care can only change if physicians use non-standard treatments, but using non-standard treatments is a career risk. The cage doesn't require anyone to be corrupt or closed-minded. It just requires everyone to be rationally self-interested within the existing incentive structure.

The financial trap

Even if a physician wanted to champion PEMF, even if a hospital wanted to adopt it, even if patients demanded it, the financial structure of American medicine makes it nearly impossible.

No patent, no trial

You cannot patent an electromagnetic field. You can patent a specific device that generates one, but the device patent is narrow: it covers the hardware, not the therapy. Any manufacturer can build a PEMF device with slightly different specifications and avoid the patent entirely.

This is the opposite of pharmaceuticals. When a drug company patents a molecule, they have 20 years of exclusivity. That exclusivity justifies the $50-200M investment in Phase III clinical trials, because the company knows it will be the sole beneficiary of positive results.

With PEMF, any company that funds a large trial and proves PEMF works for a new indication has just funded a marketing campaign for all of its competitors. The rational business decision is to let someone else fund the trial. Since everyone makes the same calculation, nobody funds the trial.

The reimbursement death loop

The path from evidence to clinical adoption in American medicine runs through a specific sequence:

  1. Large RCTs demonstrate efficacy
  2. Professional societies update clinical guidelines
  3. FDA clears or approves the device for the new indication
  4. CMS (Medicare) assigns a reimbursement code
  5. Private insurers follow CMS
  6. Hospitals purchase the equipment
  7. Physicians prescribe it

Each step requires the previous step. No large RCTs means no guideline updates. No guideline updates means no FDA expansion. No FDA expansion means no reimbursement code. No reimbursement code means no hospital adoption. No hospital adoption means no prescribing.

And no prescribing means no real-world outcomes data, which means no pressure to fund RCTs. The loop is closed. Once a therapy falls out of this sequence (as PEMF did in 1910), getting back in requires breaking the loop at one of the steps, and every step has its own gatekeepers who need the other steps to go first.

PEMF managed to break through for bone healing and wound care. For everything else, it's trapped in the loop.

Drugs with worse evidence, better adoption

This is the part that should make you angry.

There are FDA-approved drugs in current clinical use whose evidence base is thinner than PEMF's. Some were approved on the basis of two small trials. Some had their original approval based on surrogate endpoints (lab markers that are supposed to predict clinical outcomes but don't always). Some have been on the market for decades with persistent questions about their risk-benefit profile.

The difference isn't evidence. It's economics. Those drugs have patent protection, which means a company had the financial incentive to fund the trials, lobby for guideline inclusion, train physicians on prescribing, build a sales force, and navigate the reimbursement system. PEMF has none of that infrastructure, not because the science is weaker, but because the business model doesn't support it.

When a physician tells you PEMF is "unproven," what they usually mean is: "It's not in my guidelines, my hospital doesn't have the equipment, insurance won't cover it, and I've never been trained on it." All of those are true statements. None of them are statements about the evidence.

The path forward

PEMF isn't going to get adopted through the normal pathway. The normal pathway is designed for patentable molecules with billion-dollar revenue potential. A non-patentable physical therapy modality needs a different route.

1. Standardize protocols first

The single most impactful thing the PEMF research community can do is agree on standardized treatment protocols. Frequency ranges, intensity parameters, waveform specifications, treatment durations, and outcome measures. Not because one protocol is best for everything, but because standardization enables meta-analysis, and meta-analysis is what changes guidelines.

The 17 osteoarthritis RCTs are powerful individually but weakened collectively by protocol heterogeneity. A consensus conference that produces a recommended protocol for osteoarthritis alone would be worth more than another 10 underpowered trials with unique parameters.

2. Pilot integrative trials in critical care

Sepsis kills 270,000 Americans per year. The preclinical PEMF data in sepsis models shows large effect sizes. A small, well-designed pilot trial of PEMF in septic shock patients, with appropriate safety monitoring, would be feasible, ethical (the intervention is noninvasive with near-zero risk profile), and potentially practice-changing.

Critical care is where the unmet need is largest and the risk-benefit calculation most favors trying something new. A patient in septic shock with 30% mortality on standard care has little to lose from a noninvasive electromagnetic field.

Combining PEMF with tailored nutritional support (the synergy hypothesis from Part 1) would test two ideas simultaneously and could produce data that neither intervention alone would generate.

3. Educate inside the system

PEMF isn't taught in medical schools. Physicians don't learn about it during residency. Continuing medical education rarely covers it. You can't prescribe what you don't know exists.

The fix: integrate PEMF science into physical medicine and rehabilitation curricula, where it has the most natural home. Secure CPT codes for PEMF applications beyond bone healing and wound care, so physicians can bill for it. Create clinical decision support tools that surface PEMF as an option when a patient meets the criteria.

None of this requires the medical establishment to admit it was wrong. It just requires updating the curriculum to include therapies that now have evidence. That's supposed to be what evidence-based medicine does.

4. Combat groupthink structurally

The scoping reviews identified the problem but offered no solution. Here's one: form multidisciplinary teams that include biomedical engineers, integrative medicine physicians, and critical care specialists. Engineers understand the physics. Integrative physicians know the clinical history. Critical care specialists have the patient populations where PEMF could make the biggest difference.

Single-discipline teams will never adopt PEMF because no single discipline owns it. It sits at the intersection of physics, engineering, and medicine. Only interdisciplinary teams can hold the full picture.

5. Build the economic model

PEMF can't be patented, but it can be bundled. A PEMF device combined with proprietary protocols, training programs, and outcomes tracking software is a defensible business. The device is generic; the clinical system around it isn't.

If someone builds a PEMF clinical system that demonstrates consistent outcomes in a specific indication, with real-world evidence from a network of clinics, that's the kind of data that moves insurance companies. Not because the science changed, but because the business case became clear.

Kill switches for the institutional critique

This article argues that PEMF's marginalization is primarily institutional, not scientific. Here's what would disprove that:

Kill switch 1: If a well-funded, protocol-standardized, multi-site RCT in a new PEMF indication (sepsis, neurodegeneration, or chronic pain beyond osteoarthritis) shows no benefit over sham, the problem is scientific, not institutional. The institutions were right to wait. This hasn't happened yet because the trials haven't been funded, which is itself evidence for the institutional argument.
Kill switch 2: If another non-patentable physical therapy modality (say, specific exercise protocols or dietary interventions) achieves rapid guideline adoption despite having no patent-protected revenue model, then PEMF's slow adoption is specific to PEMF, not a general feature of unpatentable therapies. This would undermine the financial trap argument.
Kill switch 3: If the Flexner Report's school closures show no correlation with the specific modalities that were suppressed (i.e., if schools that taught electrotherapy were closed at the same rate as schools that didn't), then the Flexner-as-cause argument collapses. The historical record shows disproportionate closure of "sectarian" schools, but a formal statistical analysis of closure rates by modality taught would be definitive.

The institutional critique isn't a conspiracy theory. It's a description of how incentive structures, liability frameworks, reimbursement systems, and educational curricula create path dependence. A therapy that falls off the path in 1910 stays off the path in 2026, not because anyone is actively suppressing it, but because no one has a financial incentive to put it back on.

The evidence is there. The mechanisms are understood. The FDA has cleared the anchor applications. What's missing is the institutional will to follow where the data leads, even when the data leads to a therapy that nobody can patent.
