Order Your At-Home Lab Kit

Day 1 — 5 minutes to complete

After you sign up, we ship a lab collection kit to your address within 1-2 business days. The kit includes everything you need for a finger-prick blood collection at home.

What We Test

Your initial panel is thorough. This isn't a single testosterone number. We need the full picture to prescribe safely.

Full Hormone Panel

  • Total Testosterone — Primary measure of T levels
  • Free Testosterone — The bioavailable portion (often more telling)
  • Estradiol (E2) — Estrogen level; too high causes side effects
  • SHBG — Sex Hormone Binding Globulin; affects free T
  • LH & FSH — Signals from pituitary; helps identify cause
  • Prolactin — Rules out pituitary issues

Health Markers

  • CBC — Red blood cells, hematocrit (TRT can raise these)
  • CMP — Liver and kidney function
  • Lipid Panel — Cholesterol and triglycerides
  • PSA — Prostate health baseline
  • Thyroid Panel — TSH, T3, T4 (thyroid mimics low T symptoms)
  • Hemoglobin A1c — Blood sugar / insulin resistance
2

Meet Your Physician

Day 3-5 — 20-30 minute video consultation

Once your lab results are in, we schedule a video consultation with a licensed physician who specializes in hormone therapy. This isn't a 5-minute rubber stamp. Expect a real conversation about:

  • Your lab results in plain language — what's normal, what's low, what matters
  • Your symptoms and health history
  • Whether TRT is appropriate for your specific situation
  • Treatment options: injection, topical gel, or whether a non-TRT approach makes more sense
  • Potential side effects and how we monitor for them
  • Fertility considerations if applicable

If your bloodwork is normal or TRT isn't appropriate, your doctor will explain why and discuss alternatives. About 30% of consultations don't result in a TRT prescription, and that's by design. We're not here to prescribe to everyone who asks.

What qualifies as low?

Most labs list "normal" total testosterone as 264-916 ng/dL. But a 35-year-old at 280 ng/dL with clear symptoms is a different case than a healthy 70-year-old at 350.

Your doctor evaluates the full picture: total T, free T, SHBG, symptoms, age, and health history. A single number doesn't tell the whole story.

Not a pill mill

We don't prescribe based on symptoms alone. Bloodwork is required. Clinical thresholds must be met. If lifestyle changes (sleep, diet, exercise, stress) are more appropriate, your doctor will say so directly.

3

Treatment & Ongoing Monitoring

Day 7-10 — Medication arrives, monitoring begins

If prescribed, your medication ships from a licensed U.S. pharmacy within 2-3 business days. Everything you need comes in the box:

  • Testosterone (cypionate injection or topical gel, per your doctor's recommendation)
  • Syringes and needles (if injection) in the correct gauge and length
  • Alcohol swabs and bandages
  • Sharps disposal container
  • Injection technique guide with video walkthrough

Monitoring Schedule

TRT isn't fire-and-forget. Your protocol gets adjusted based on real data:

  • 6 weeks: First follow-up labs. Check T levels, hematocrit, estradiol. Dose adjustment if needed.
  • 12 weeks: Second follow-up. By now your levels should be stabilized. Full panel recheck.
  • Quarterly: Ongoing monitoring every 3 months for the first year. Then every 6 months if stable.
  • Annual: Complete health review including lipids, PSA, metabolic panel, and full hormone panel.

Target range

Most protocols aim for total testosterone in the 600-900 ng/dL range. Your doctor sets a target based on your baseline, symptoms, and how you respond. The goal is where you feel best, not the highest possible number.

Injection is not as bad as you think

Most patients self-inject subcutaneously with a small insulin-type needle (27-29 gauge). It's a pinch, not a clinic-grade intramuscular shot. Your care team walks you through it over video if needed.

Understanding Your Bloodwork

What the numbers actually mean

Lab reports can be confusing. Here's how to read the key markers your doctor will discuss.

Total Testosterone

Reference: 264-916 ng/dL

The total amount of testosterone in your blood, both bound and unbound. This is the primary diagnostic number. Below 300 ng/dL with symptoms is generally considered clinically low.

Free Testosterone

Reference: 5-21 ng/dL

The testosterone actually available for your body to use. Only 2-3% of total T is "free." A man can have normal total T but low free T (often due to high SHBG), and still have symptoms.

Estradiol (E2)

Target on TRT: 20-40 pg/mL

Testosterone converts to estrogen via aromatase. Too high causes water retention, mood issues, and gynecomastia. Too low causes joint pain and low libido. Balance matters.

Hematocrit / CBC

Concern above: 54%

TRT stimulates red blood cell production. High hematocrit thickens blood and increases cardiovascular risk. This is the most common side effect that requires protocol adjustment.

SHBG

Reference: 10-57 nmol/L

Sex Hormone Binding Globulin binds testosterone and makes it unavailable. High SHBG means less free T even if total T looks normal. Low SHBG can cause T to clear faster.

PSA

Baseline required before TRT

Prostate-specific antigen. TRT doesn't cause prostate cancer, but it can accelerate existing conditions. Baseline and ongoing PSA monitoring is standard medical practice.

Common Scenarios

What happens if...

My levels are normal?

We don't prescribe. Your doctor may recommend lifestyle modifications (sleep, strength training, nutrition, stress management) that naturally support testosterone production. You still get the lab results and consultation.

I'm on other medications?

Your doctor reviews your full medication list during consultation. Some medications interact with TRT or affect hormone levels. Opioids, corticosteroids, and some antidepressants are common factors. Honest disclosure is critical.

I want to have kids?

TRT suppresses sperm production. If fertility is a concern, your doctor may prescribe HCG alongside testosterone, use clomiphene instead, or recommend banking sperm before starting. This is a real conversation, not a footnote.

I don't like needles?

Topical testosterone gel is an option. It's applied daily to shoulders or upper arms. Absorption varies more than injection, and it carries transfer risk (skin contact can expose others). Your doctor will discuss the tradeoffs.

I want to stop TRT?

You can stop, but it's not immediate. Your natural production is suppressed while on TRT and takes time to restart. Your doctor will taper your protocol and may prescribe medications to support recovery (clomiphene, HCG).

I've used TRT before?

Bring your previous bloodwork and protocol details. If you're switching from another clinic, we run fresh labs and your physician reviews your history. No need to start from scratch if records are available.
