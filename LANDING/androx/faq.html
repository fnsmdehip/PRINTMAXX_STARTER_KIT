The Basics

What is testosterone replacement therapy (TRT)?

TRT is a medically supervised treatment that restores testosterone levels in men diagnosed with clinically low testosterone (hypogonadism). It uses FDA-regulated testosterone, delivered via injection or topical gel, to bring levels back to a normal physiological range.

It is not a supplement. It's a prescription medication that requires bloodwork, physician evaluation, and ongoing monitoring.

How do I know if I have low testosterone?

Bloodwork is the only way to confirm. Common symptoms include persistent fatigue despite adequate sleep, decreased libido, difficulty building or maintaining muscle, brain fog, irritability, increased body fat, and depression-like mood changes.

These symptoms can also be caused by thyroid dysfunction, sleep apnea, depression, chronic stress, vitamin deficiencies, or other conditions. That's why bloodwork tests multiple markers, not just testosterone.

What bloodwork is required?

Our initial panel tests: Total Testosterone, Free Testosterone, Estradiol (E2), SHBG, LH, FSH, Prolactin, CBC (including hematocrit), CMP (liver/kidney), Lipid Panel, PSA, Thyroid Panel (TSH/T3/T4), and Hemoglobin A1c.

This is more thorough than what most clinics order. We need the full picture to prescribe safely and identify if low T symptoms might be caused by something else entirely.

What testosterone levels are considered "low"?

Most labs define "normal" total testosterone as 264-916 ng/dL. Clinical guidelines generally consider below 300 ng/dL as hypogonadal. But context matters: a 30-year-old at 310 ng/dL with symptoms is a different clinical picture than a 65-year-old at the same level.

Your doctor evaluates total T, free T, SHBG levels, your age, symptoms, and overall health. A single number doesn't determine treatment.

Is TRT the same as anabolic steroids?

TRT uses the same base compound (testosterone) but at replacement doses that bring levels to a normal physiological range, typically 600-900 ng/dL. Steroid abuse for performance enhancement uses supraphysiological doses, often 3-10 times higher, sometimes combined with other compounds.

The intent is different (health restoration vs. performance enhancement), the dosing is different, and the risk profile is different. TRT under medical supervision with regular bloodwork is a standard medical treatment prescribed by endocrinologists and urologists.

Treatment Details

Injection or gel: which is better?

Injection (Testosterone Cypionate): Most common choice. Injected 1-2x per week subcutaneously with a small needle. Provides steady levels, precise dosing, and no transfer risk. Most patients choose this.

Topical Gel: Applied daily to shoulders or upper arms. No needles. But absorption varies between individuals, and there's a real risk of transferring testosterone to partners or children through skin contact. Requires covering the application area with clothing.

Your doctor will recommend one based on your lifestyle, preference, and clinical factors. Both are included in your plan at the same price.

Do I have to inject myself?

Yes, TRT injections are self-administered at home. But it's much simpler than most people expect. Subcutaneous injections use a small insulin-type needle (27-29 gauge) into the belly fat or thigh. It takes about 60 seconds.

Your care team provides video instruction and walks you through your first injection over a live call if needed. Most patients report it becomes completely routine within 2-3 weeks.

How long until I feel results?

Timeline varies by individual, but general patterns:

  • 1-2 weeks: Improved energy, sleep quality for some
  • 2-4 weeks: Mood improvements, mental clarity
  • 3-6 weeks: Libido changes, sexual function
  • 8-12 weeks: Noticeable body composition changes (with training)
  • 3-6 months: Significant muscle gain, fat reduction (with diet and exercise)
  • 6-12 months: Full effects stabilized

TRT is not instant. It also doesn't replace training and nutrition. Men who combine TRT with strength training and reasonable eating habits see the most meaningful changes.

What dose will I be prescribed?

Starting doses are typically 100-200mg of testosterone cypionate per week, split into 2 injections for more stable levels. Your exact dose depends on your baseline levels, SHBG, body weight, and clinical response.

At your 6-week follow-up labs, your doctor adjusts the dose based on where your levels land and how you feel. The goal is the lowest effective dose that resolves symptoms and puts you in a healthy range.

Side Effects & Risks

What are the side effects of TRT?

Potential side effects include:

  • High hematocrit: Most common. TRT increases red blood cell production. Monitored via CBC. May require dose reduction or therapeutic blood donation.
  • Acne: Usually mild, back/shoulders. Manageable with skincare.
  • Hair thinning: In men genetically predisposed to male pattern baldness, TRT can accelerate it. Not reversible.
  • Testicular atrophy: External testosterone signals your body to reduce natural production. Testicles may shrink. HCG co-therapy can prevent or reduce this.
  • Mood changes: Most men report improved mood. Some experience irritability, especially if estrogen gets too high.
  • Sleep apnea: TRT can worsen existing sleep apnea. Get screened if you snore heavily.
  • Fertility suppression: See fertility section below.

Your doctor monitors for all of these through regular bloodwork and check-ins. Most side effects are manageable with dose adjustments or ancillary medications.

Will TRT affect my fertility?

Yes. Exogenous testosterone suppresses your body's natural testosterone and sperm production (via the HPT axis). Sperm count typically drops significantly, and some men become temporarily azoospermic (zero sperm count) while on TRT.

Options if fertility matters:

  • HCG co-therapy: Taking HCG alongside TRT helps maintain testicular function and sperm production. Not 100% effective but significantly helps.
  • Clomiphene instead of TRT: Stimulates your body's own testosterone production without suppressing fertility. Lower T levels achieved vs. TRT.
  • Sperm banking: Freeze sperm before starting TRT as insurance.
  • Planned breaks: Discontinuing TRT before trying to conceive (recovery takes months).

If you're planning children in the next 1-3 years, this should be the first thing you discuss with your doctor. Don't treat it as an afterthought.

Is TRT safe long-term?

TRT has been prescribed for decades. Current research shows it's generally safe when prescribed at physiological doses with proper monitoring. The Endocrine Society and American Urological Association both publish clinical guidelines for its use.

Long-term risks are primarily cardiovascular (related to hematocrit elevation) and prostate-related (PSA monitoring). Recent large studies (TRAVERSE trial, 2023) found no increased cardiovascular risk in men on TRT vs. placebo.

The key is ongoing monitoring: regular bloodwork catches issues before they become problems. Unsupervised TRT without monitoring is where serious risks emerge.

Can TRT cause prostate cancer?

Current evidence does not show that TRT causes prostate cancer. This was a long-held concern based on older studies, but modern research has largely refuted it. The Endocrine Society guidelines state that TRT is not contraindicated in men without known prostate cancer.

However, TRT can accelerate the growth of existing prostate cancer. That's why PSA testing is required before starting and during treatment. If you have a history of prostate cancer, TRT may not be appropriate for you.

Eligibility

Who qualifies for TRT?

You may qualify if you're an adult male with:

  • Clinically low testosterone confirmed by bloodwork (generally below 300 ng/dL total T, though context matters)
  • Symptoms consistent with hypogonadism
  • No contraindications (active prostate cancer, untreated severe sleep apnea, very high hematocrit, desire for near-term pregnancy without a mitigation plan)

Not everyone qualifies. If your levels are normal, if your symptoms are better explained by another condition, or if you have contraindications, your doctor will not prescribe TRT.

What age do I need to be?

You must be 18 or older. Low testosterone is most common in men over 30, with prevalence increasing with age. However, younger men can have clinically low levels due to genetic factors, injury, or other medical conditions.

I'm already on TRT from another clinic. Can I switch?

Yes. Bring your previous bloodwork and current protocol details. We'll run fresh labs, and your physician will review your history. If your current protocol is working well, they may continue it. If there's room for improvement, they'll discuss options.

There's no lapse in treatment if you plan ahead. Start the process with Androx before your current supply runs out.

What if I've tried TRT before and stopped?

We need to understand why you stopped and what happened after. Fresh bloodwork will show your current levels and whether your natural production recovered. Your doctor will factor in your prior experience when creating a protocol.

Is testosterone a controlled substance?

Yes. Testosterone is classified as a Schedule III controlled substance by the DEA. It requires a prescription from a licensed physician and is dispensed by licensed pharmacies. Possession without a prescription is illegal. Androx operates fully within federal and state regulations for prescribing controlled substances via telehealth.

Can I travel with my testosterone?

Domestically, yes. Keep your medication in its original pharmacy packaging with your name and prescription label. For air travel, carry it in your carry-on (TSA allows syringes with labeled medication).

International travel is more complex. Testosterone regulations vary by country. Some countries require import permits, others prohibit it entirely. Check destination country regulations before traveling. Your care team can provide a letter from your prescribing physician if needed.

Will TRT show up on a drug test?

Standard workplace drug tests (5-panel, 10-panel) do not test for testosterone. However, sports organizations (NCAA, WADA, UFC, MLB, etc.) do test for exogenous testosterone via the T/E ratio or IRMS carbon isotope testing. If you compete in any regulated sport, TRT will likely result in a positive test and disqualification.
