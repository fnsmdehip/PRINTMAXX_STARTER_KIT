## Post 1 - Blue Collar AI (LinkedIn format)

A piping contractor in Ohio automated his quoting system with Claude Code.

Before: 40+ hours/week on manual pipe spec lookups and material takeoffs.
After: 3 hours.

No developer. No technical cofounder. No pitch deck.

Blue-collar operators are adopting AI faster than most tech founders because they have real, expensive problems and zero attachment to the old workflow.

The most valuable AI implementations aren't happening in Silicon Valley. They're happening in warehouses, job sites, and repair shops.

If you're building AI tools, stop selling to other tech companies. Start selling to the people who actually need efficiency gains.

## Post 2 - App Portfolio Economics

A Turkish mobile studio makes $4M/yr with 3 developers and 200+ live apps.

Their system:
- Build each app in 2 days
- Validate with real data for 14 days
- Monetize with ads + $2.99/week subscriptions
- Kill anything under $100 MRR at day 60
- Flip losers on Acquire.com for 12-24x monthly revenue

Most developers treat each app like a startup. These studios treat apps like inventory.

20 apps averaging $150/mo each = $36K/yr. Add exit revenue from flipping the losers and the total hits $40-50K.

Speed and portfolio thinking beat perfectionism every time.

## Post 3 - Finding Underserved Niches

I found a niche where thousands of people actively buy courses on saving money and getting out of debt.

Active Discord communities. Paid newsletters. $29-79 ebooks selling consistently.

Number of apps serving this audience: almost zero.

The gap between "people willing to pay for solutions" and "developers building solutions" is still one of the most reliable signals for profitable niches.

The pattern to check:
1. Find communities with paid products (courses, ebooks, coaching)
2. Check the App Store and Play Store for apps in that niche
3. If the ratio of paying customers to apps is high, build there

Frugal living. Meal prep budgeting. Envelope method tracking. The unsexy stuff has the best margins.

## Post 4 - AI Agent Reality Check

Someone set up an AI agent to run their household logistics. Morning briefings, budget summaries, task routing.

It worked great for a week.

Then the agent's memories vanished. Settings broke. The system that was supposed to manage them became a system they had to manage.

This is the actual state of production AI agents in March 2026:
- They demo well
- They break quietly
- The failure mode is always the same: state management

If you're building on agent infrastructure, your moat isn't features. It's reliability. The team that solves persistent memory and graceful degradation will own the market.

## Post 5 - API-First Email Opportunity

Every email marketing tool on the market was built for marketers clicking buttons in a UI.

But the market shifted. AI agents need email as an output. n8n workflows need email as a node. Custom automations need email as a function call.

The APIs that exist today:
- Mailchimp: bloated, complex, overbuilt for simple sends
- ConvertKit: limited endpoints, incomplete webhook support
- Beehiiv: barely functional programmatic access

An API-first email platform where the interface is optional and the API is the product. Dev-first documentation. Webhook-native architecture.

Infrastructure plays aren't glamorous but the plumbing always wins. Twilio proved this with SMS. Stripe proved this with payments. Email is next.
