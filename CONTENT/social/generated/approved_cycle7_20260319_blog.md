# Follow the silicon: how to read chip earnings and find your next $10K/mo product

Most indie builders are looking at Product Hunt for product ideas. The smarter ones are reading semiconductor earnings reports.

## The signal nobody is reading

Micron reported $23.9 billion in quarterly revenue this week. In 2022, same quarter, they reported $3.8 billion. That's 6.3x growth in 3 years.

Next quarter guidance: $33.5 billion. Above analyst expectations.

NVIDIA did $35 billion last quarter. AMD inference chips are sold out through Q3.

This is not speculative. These are real companies shipping real silicon to real customers who are deploying real AI infrastructure. The demand for compute is not slowing down. It's accelerating.

## What this means for solo builders

Every dollar spent on AI hardware needs software on top of it to generate ROI. Companies don't buy GPUs to admire them. They buy GPUs to run models, process data, and automate workflows.

The software layer above hardware is where the opportunity lives for indie builders. You don't need $100M to compete with NVIDIA. You need to build the $200/mo tool that helps a team of 10 manage their AI spend.

## The opportunity stack

Here's how the money flows and where solo builders fit:

**Layer 1: Hardware** (NVIDIA, AMD, Micron)
- Capital required: billions
- Competition: extreme
- Solo builder opportunity: zero

**Layer 2: Infrastructure** (AWS, Azure, GCP, data centers)
- Capital required: hundreds of millions
- Competition: high
- Solo builder opportunity: low (maybe niche hosting)

**Layer 3: Platform tools** (monitoring, management, optimization)
- Capital required: $0-50K
- Competition: moderate but growing
- Solo builder opportunity: high

**Layer 4: Application** (end-user tools solving specific problems)
- Capital required: $0-5K
- Competition: fragmented
- Solo builder opportunity: highest

The pattern: the further from hardware you build, the less capital you need and the less competition you face.

## 5 products the silicon demand is creating right now

**1. AI cost tracking dashboards**

Every company deploying AI models wants to know: which team spent how much on inference last month? This is the "cloud cost management" category (Datadog, CloudHealth) replicated for AI-specific spend. A solo dev built one and has 40 paying customers at $500/mo in 3 months.

**2. Prompt management and versioning**

Enterprise teams managing 50+ prompts across multiple models need version control, A/B testing, and cost-per-prompt tracking. This category didn't exist 2 years ago. Now it's a real pain point.

**3. AI agent monitoring**

As companies deploy autonomous agents (customer support, data processing, code review), they need observability: what did the agent do, did it succeed, how much did it cost, what went wrong. This is the "Datadog for AI agents" play.

**4. GPU utilization optimization**

Most companies are running GPUs at 30-60% utilization. A tool that helps them schedule workloads to maximize utilization saves thousands per month. The ROI pitch writes itself.

**5. Model evaluation and benchmarking**

Teams testing 3-5 different models for the same task need standardized evaluation. Quality, speed, cost, accuracy comparisons across models. Internal tooling that most companies build from scratch every time.

## How to validate before building

1. Read the earnings call transcripts (free on Seeking Alpha)
2. Look for phrases like "customer demand," "deployment acceleration," "capacity constraints"
3. Search LinkedIn for job titles that didn't exist 2 years ago (AI Platform Engineer, LLM Operations, Prompt Engineer)
4. Cold email 10 people with those job titles and ask what's painful about their daily workflow
5. Build a prototype that solves the most common pain point
6. Charge 1% of what they're spending on the infrastructure your tool optimizes

## The bottom line

$33.5 billion in Micron revenue next quarter means the software layer above it is a multi-billion dollar opportunity. You don't need $1 billion of it. You need $10K/mo.

Stop scrolling Product Hunt for ideas. Start reading earnings reports. The companies spending billions on hardware are telling you exactly what software they need.

Follow the silicon.
