There's a 10-question quiz that's been shared more than almost any other psychological assessment online.

It takes 3 minutes. The results predict your risk of heart disease, depression, substance abuse, and early death more accurately than most clinical tests your doctor runs.

It's called the ACE questionnaire. Adverse Childhood Experiences.

Dr. Vincent Felitti ran the study in 1998 with 17,000 Kaiser Permanente patients. The findings were brutal:

- Every 1-point increase in ACE score = 46% higher depression risk
- Every 1-point = 51% higher liver disease risk
- ACE score of 6+ = 1,220% higher likelihood of suicide attempt

The dose-response relationship is linear. Every point makes it worse. No floor, no plateau.

This is the most replicated, most cited finding in adverse childhood experiences research. It's been confirmed in dozens of countries across multiple demographic groups.

**And there's no app teaching the science behind it.**

I spent two hours looking. Wellness apps treat trauma like a vibe ("healing journal" prompts, gratitude lists, breathwork timers). Therapy apps are scheduling tools. Meditation apps pretend the nervous system doesn't have a past.

Nobody built a product that says: here is what your ACE score actually means neurologically. Here is what happened in your brain during development. Here is what the research says about recovery.

The mechanism is cortisol. High ACE scores mean your HPA axis (the stress response system) was chronically activated during a critical developmental window. Your amygdala learned to fire earlier. Your hippocampus learned to tag everything as dangerous. Your prefrontal cortex — the part that does rational thinking and impulse control — developed under constant cortisol suppression.

This is not a mindset problem. It's not a motivation problem. It's neurological architecture shaped by environment.

The communities that know this exist in the millions: r/CPTSD (500K), r/raisedbynarcissists (1M), r/PTSD (1.1M), attachment theory communities spanning 5.4M people across platforms.

All of them share the ACE quiz. Many of them don't know what the score means beyond "high is bad."

That's the gap. That's the product.
