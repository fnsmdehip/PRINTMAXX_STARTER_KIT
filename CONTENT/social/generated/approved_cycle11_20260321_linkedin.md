I've been scraping Reddit's SaaS, IndieHackers, and Entrepreneur communities daily for 6 weeks. 1,648 new entries just today.

The pattern is impossible to ignore: every founder is describing the same 5 problems.

1. "I launched and nothing happened." Most people post 3 times, run ads for 9 days, write 2 blog posts, and quit. Then say marketing doesn't work.

2. "How do I get paying users?" This question was posted 4 separate times today with 100+ combined comments. The advice is always the same: be useful first, sell second. Nobody wants to hear it.

3. "I wasted months building with AI tools." One founder spent $3,400 and 9 weeks. AI made it so fast to build that they skipped validating whether anyone wanted it. Speed without direction is expensive.

4. "Cold email is dead." It's not. But cold email alone is. One agency added LinkedIn to their sequences and reply rates doubled from 3-4% to ~7%. Multichannel is survival, not strategy.

5. "I built 4 products in 18 months. Zero customers." The builder trap. Building ships dopamine. Selling ships revenue. The gap between those feelings is where most projects die.

The uncomfortable ratio across all entries: people asking "how do I get users" outnumber people sharing "here's what worked" by about 15:1.

Building was never the hard part.

## LinkedIn Post 2 - The $16 Email Stack

One of today's most interesting Reddit posts: a founder replaced their $95/month email infrastructure with a $16/month self-hosted setup.

The old stack: SendGrid ($20) + Mailchimp ($45) + Zapier ($30) + 5 hours monthly maintenance.

The real cost of most SaaS stacks isn't the subscription. It's the 4 other subscriptions you need to make the first one work.

Every tool you add is a tax on your attention. Most stacks are 70% glue code connecting overpriced APIs to each other.

If you're spending more on infrastructure than you're earning in revenue, the infrastructure is the problem.
