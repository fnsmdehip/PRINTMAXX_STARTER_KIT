## 1/6

france's nuclear aircraft carrier was tracked in real-time by journalists using fitness app data.

sailors logged workouts. the GPS data leaked the carrier's exact position.

a $4B warship compromised by a free app.

here's why this matters for every founder, not just the military:

## 2/6

your data footprint is bigger than you think.

every app with location permissions: tracking you.
every fitness tracker: logging your patterns.
every photo with EXIF data: broadcasting your coordinates.
every Wi-Fi network your phone auto-joins: recording your presence.

the aircraft carrier crew didn't think they were sharing military intelligence. they thought they were logging their 5K time.

## 3/6

for founders and solopreneurs, the risks are different but real:

- your morning run reveals your home address
- your coworking check-ins reveal your schedule
- your travel patterns reveal your client meetings
- your app usage data is sold to data brokers who sell to your competitors

you don't need to be hiding a warship. you just need to understand that your routine is a data product someone is buying.

## 4/6

the fix isn't paranoia. it's awareness.

1. audit app permissions quarterly (takes 10 minutes)
2. disable location sharing on fitness apps or set to "only while using"
3. strip EXIF data from photos before posting (most phones have this setting)
4. use a VPN on public wifi
5. check haveibeenpwned.com every 90 days

basic OPSEC that 95% of tech founders skip.

## 5/6

the bigger pattern: every platform that collects data will eventually leak it.

strava leaked military base locations in 2018. same pattern, 8 years later, still happening.

the companies that understand data as liability (not just asset) will outlast the ones that don't.

if you're building an app: collect the minimum data needed. your users' trust is worth more than your analytics dashboard.

## 6/6

546 HN points because people are still shocked when this happens.

it'll happen again. with a different app. a different military. a different dataset.

the question isn't whether your data is exposed. it's whether you've checked.

the aircraft carrier learned the hard way. you don't have to.
