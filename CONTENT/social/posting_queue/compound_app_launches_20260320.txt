## TWITTER THREAD — DeskBreak (Behavioral Design)

### 1/5

built a tool that forces you to take breaks from your desk.

not reminders. not notifications. your screen locks.

the insight: friction belongs on the WRONG behavior, not the right one.

every break app reminds you to stand up. you dismiss the notification and keep working.

deskbreak makes it impossible to keep working.

### 2/5

how it works:

- set your work/break intervals (25/5, 50/10, whatever)
- when break time hits, your screen goes to a break screen
- you can't dismiss it. you can't minimize it. you can't "5 more minutes" it.
- when break is over, you're back to work.

pomodoro with enforcement.

### 3/5

why this works when reminders don't:

reminders rely on willpower. you see "take a break" and think "after this one thing."

4 hours later you haven't moved.

deskbreak removes the decision. you don't choose to take a break. the break happens.

same principle as prayerlock (locks phone during prayer). friction on the wrong behavior.

### 4/5

the pattern:

- prayerlock: friction on phone use during prayer
- deskbreak: friction on desk work during breaks
- soberstreak: friction on breaking your sober streak
- fitness streaks: friction on skipping workouts

every habit app gets this backwards. they add friction to STARTING the good habit. that's wrong.

add friction to CONTINUING the bad habit. the good habit becomes the path of least resistance.

### 5/5

deskbreak is free. works in any browser. no install. no account.

deskbreak.surge.sh

your back, your eyes, and your posture will thank you.

if you work from home and "forget" to take breaks, you don't have a discipline problem. you have a friction problem.

## LINKEDIN POST — "The behavioral design pattern nobody uses"

Every habit app gets friction backwards.

They add friction to STARTING the good habit. "Log your workout." "Track your calories." "Check in daily."

The result: you stop using the app because the overhead makes the habit harder.

The pattern that works: add friction to the BAD behavior.

PrayerLock doesn't remind you to pray. It locks your phone during prayer. The friction is on phone use, not on prayer.

DeskBreak doesn't remind you to take breaks. It locks your screen. The friction is on sitting, not on standing up.

SoberStreak doesn't motivate you with stories. It shows one number. Miss a day and it resets. The friction is on breaking the streak.

In UX design terms: make the right behavior the path of least resistance by making the wrong behavior harder.

I built 6 apps using this pattern this month. Each one does exactly one thing. No social features. No premium tiers. No accounts.

Because the product IS the friction. Not the features around it.
