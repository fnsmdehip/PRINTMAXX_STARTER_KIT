## POST 1 — The Educational Product Death Spiral

Anthropic just launched 13 free courses teaching Claude Code. With official certificates.

CC for Everyone teaches non-coders interactively. Free. Sahil Lavingia (400K audience) published 9 Claude Code skills on GitHub. Free. Multiple comprehensive guides from community developers. All free.

I have 9 Claude Code guides written and formatted as PDFs, ready to sell on Gumroad.

The honest assessment: the educational angle is dead for paid products.

When the company that built the tool gives away better education than you can sell, the game changes.

But here's what I've learned in the process:

The distinction isn't education vs. nothing. It's education vs. operation.

Educational: "Learn how to use Claude Code." Free everywhere. Official certificates available.

Operational: "Install this config file and 33 autonomous agents start running your business." Zero free alternatives. Nobody gives away running systems.

Courses teach you to use the tool. Operational products ARE the tool.

A CLAUDE.md config + hook definitions + agent architecture that, when dropped into a project, immediately starts scraping leads, generating content, analyzing competitors, and managing a product portfolio.

That's not a course. That's infrastructure.

The survivable position in any market where the platform owner commoditizes education: sell the implementation, not the knowledge.

Three questions for anyone selling digital products in spaces where free education is exploding:

1. Can your customer replicate your product's value from a free course?
2. If yes, you're selling convenience, not capability. Price accordingly.
3. If no, you have a moat. What makes it irreplaceable?

## POST 2 — Quant Thinking for App Portfolios

I manage my iOS app portfolio like a hedge fund manages positions.

Every app gets scored weekly on 7 dimensions: revenue potential, speed to first dollar, automation potential, downside risk, competition density, synergy with other apps, and capital efficiency.

Kill rules are non-negotiable:
- Below $100 MRR after 60 days: kill it
- Below 500 users after 90 days: kill it
- 20%+ sustained growth at $500+: double down

Most indie developers work on whatever feels exciting. They pour months into apps with no traction because they're emotionally attached to the code.

Portfolio theory says: 1 app has ~30% chance of hitting $1K MRR. 10 independent apps with 30% each = 97% probability at least one hits.

Diversification isn't a strategy. It's math.

The automation angle: I built a Python script that pulls revenue data, scores every app, and generates kill/scale/boost recommendations automatically. Runs weekly via cron. The system manages itself.

Question for the builders: how do you decide which project to kill vs. double down on? Is it data-driven or gut feel?
