### Script 7: "Non-Muslims: here's what Ramadan actually is"

**Duration:** 55 seconds
**Format:** Talking head, warm, educational, inviting

"if you're not muslim and you've always wondered what ramadan is about, here's the honest version."
TEXT OVERLAY: "ramadan explained honestly"

BODY (3-50s):
"ramadan is the 9th month of the islamic calendar. for 30 days, muslims fast from dawn to sunset. no food. no water. nothing.

but here's the part most people miss: it's not about the hunger.

fasting is supposed to do a few things. it teaches empathy. when your stomach is empty, you understand what the poor feel every day. that's why charity spikes during ramadan. it's not abstract anymore.

it builds discipline. saying no to your most basic desires - food, water, comfort - for 15 hours a day rewires something in you. if you can say no to water, you can say no to anything.

it creates space. when you're not eating, not snacking, not planning meals, not scrolling during lunch... you have all this open time. that time gets filled with prayer, quran reading, reflection, and community.

the evenings are the best part. families gather for iftar - the meal that breaks the fast. dates and water first (that's tradition). then food. then taraweeh prayer at the mosque, where the entire quran gets recited over 30 nights.

the last 10 nights are the holiest. muslims believe one of those nights - laylatul qadr - is worth more than 1,000 months of worship.

and then eid. the celebration at the end. new clothes, big meals, gifts for kids, visiting family.

that's ramadan. it's hard. it's beautiful. it changes you a little each year if you let it."

### Newsletter 1: "Why I Pray Before Every Sprint"

**Subject line:** i started praying before every work session. here's what happened after 30 days.
**Preview text:** the productivity hack that's 1,400 years old

---

i didn't plan this as an experiment. it started because i was anxious.

there was a project at work that i'd been putting off for weeks. the codebase was a mess, the deadline was close, and every time i opened the file i'd find something else to do instead. check email. refill water. reorganize my desk. anything to avoid the uncomfortable thing.

one morning, before opening the laptop, i just... prayed. not a formal salah with wudu and a mat. just closed my eyes at my desk and said: "God, help me focus. help me not be afraid of this. help me do good work today."

90 seconds. then i opened the laptop and started working.

i got more done in that morning session than i had in the previous 3 days combined.

could have been coincidence. could have been the specific day. so i tried it again the next morning. and the next. and i kept going for 30 days.

here's what i noticed.

**the startup cost disappeared.**

the hardest part of deep work isn't the work itself. it's the transition. the mental shift from scattered-attention-mode to focused-creation-mode. that transition usually takes me 15-20 minutes of warming up. checking things. clearing mental debris.

prayer compressed that transition to 90 seconds. close eyes. breathe. hand the session to God. open eyes. start. the debris was already cleared.

**my relationship with difficulty changed.**

before the experiment, hard tasks felt like threats. my body would tense. my brain would find escape routes.

after integrating prayer, hard tasks felt like... assignments. something was given to me. i'm equipped to handle it. if i can't, that's okay too. the outcome isn't mine to control. the effort is.

that mental reframe - from "i have to solve this or i fail" to "i'm going to work on this and the rest is not mine" - eliminated about 70% of my work anxiety.

**the quality improved.**

this is the one that surprised me. when you start a work session from stillness instead of urgency, the decisions are different. you think before typing. you design before building. you ask "is this the right approach?" instead of "what's the fastest approach?"

my code reviews came back cleaner. my bug count dropped. my pull requests were smaller and more focused. the prayer didn't give me supernatural coding ability. it gave me 90 seconds of patience, and patience made everything downstream better.

**the days i skipped were louder.**

by week 3, the contrast was sharp. prayer days: calm entry, steady focus, patience in meetings, clear thinking. skip days: inbox-checking, meeting-dreading, context-switching, shallow output.

i stopped skipping.

**this is not a productivity hack.**

i want to be clear about that. i didn't start praying to be more productive. i started because i was afraid and needed help. the productivity was a side effect.

every spiritual tradition has a version of this. centering prayer in christianity. tafakkur in islam. hitbodedut in judaism. meditation in buddhism. the sitting-in-silence-before-action thing is a human universal. it works because it's how we're wired.

if the word "prayer" doesn't work for you, call it what you want. stillness. centering. intention-setting. 90 seconds before you start, acknowledging that you're not in control of everything and asking for help from whatever you believe is bigger than you.

try it for one week. monday through friday. 90 seconds before your first work session.

i think you'll feel the difference by wednesday.

selah.

### Newsletter 2: "Ramadan Preparation Guide for Working Muslims"

**Subject line:** ramadan starts feb 28. here's the prep guide i wish i had my first year.
**Preview text:** sleep, food, work, spirit. in that order.

---

this is my 4th year fasting while working a full-time job. the first year was chaos. by year 3, i had a system. here's everything i learned, in the order that matters.

**1. fix your sleep first. everything else depends on this.**

suhoor is around 4:15am. if you're currently sleeping at midnight, you need to shift. start now - 2 weeks before ramadan - by moving your bedtime 15 minutes earlier each night.

the math: to function on a suhoor schedule, you need to be asleep by 10pm. that gives you 6 hours before the 4am alarm. it's not luxurious sleep, but it's enough if you protect it.

what kills ramadan sleep: staying up late after taraweeh (the prayers end around 10:30pm in most mosques - go straight to bed), scrolling your phone in bed, and eating too much at iftar (your body is digesting when it should be sleeping).

power nap window: 1-2pm. set an alarm for 20 minutes. this single nap rescues the afternoon.

**2. simplify your food. this is not a cooking competition.**

the biggest ramadan trap is elaborate iftars. families spend hours cooking, eat too much, feel sluggish for taraweeh, sleep poorly, and drag through the next day.

my suhoor rotation (prep in 5 minutes or less):
- overnight oats with dates, chia seeds, peanut butter
- eggs and toast with avocado
- yogurt bowl with banana and granola
- leftover soup from the freezer

my iftar rotation (15 minutes or less):
- dates and water first (always)
- pre-made soup (lentil, chicken, or tomato - batch cook before ramadan, freeze in portions)
- simple protein + rice or bread
- salad if i have the energy

cook 3 soups the weekend before ramadan. freeze in individual containers. that's 10+ iftars handled with zero weeknight cooking.

**3. restructure your workday around your energy.**

your energy curve during ramadan is different from normal days:

- 7-10am: peak focus. deep work here. code, write, build, think. this is your golden window.
- 10am-12pm: moderate energy. meetings, collaboration, lighter tasks.
- 12-3pm: the dip. admin tasks, emails, organizing. don't fight it.
- 3-5pm: low point. coast. do the minimum. take a walk if you can.
- 30 min before maghrib: stop working. make wudu. sit with your dua list. the final minutes before iftar are among the most spiritually valuable of the day. don't waste them on slack.

**tell your team you're fasting.** a simple message: "i'm observing ramadan this month, fasting from dawn to sunset. my energy may be lower in the afternoons. mornings are my best hours." every team i've told has been respectful.

**4. set your spiritual goals before day 1.**

don't figure out your ramadan goals on day 1 of ramadan. figure them out now.

my framework:
- quran goal: full khatm (20 pages/day) or half (10 pages/day) or custom
- prayer goal: all 5 on time, every day, no exceptions
- dua list: written, specific, reviewed daily
- charity goal: daily sadaqah, even $1
- community: taraweeh at the masjid X nights per week
- personal: one internal change i'm seeking (this year: patience)

write them down. tell someone. track them daily.

tools: ramadan-tracker.surge.sh for fasting timer, prayer times, and quran tracking. free, works offline. a physical journal for duas and reflections. i deliberately use paper for spiritual writing - the slowness is the point.

**5. the last 10 nights deserve their own plan.**

the last 10 nights of ramadan contain laylatul qadr - a single night worth more than 1,000 months of worship. it falls on one of the odd nights (21st, 23rd, 25th, 27th, or 29th).

take time off work if you can. even 2-3 days. plan your ibadah for these nights in advance: longer prayers, more quran, deeper duas, sadaqah each odd night.

don't arrive at the last 10 nights exhausted and improvising. plan now.

**the real advice: give yourself grace.**

your ramadan won't be perfect. you'll oversleep for suhoor. you'll be cranky at 3pm. you'll miss a prayer. you'll eat too much at iftar and feel terrible during taraweeh.

that's fine. the point of ramadan is not perfection. the point is showing up, day after day, imperfect and sincere, for 30 days. that consistency changes you in ways that a perfect single day never could.

you've got 15 days to prepare. start with sleep. the rest will follow.

ramadan mubarak.

### Newsletter 3: "The Selah Principle: What Ancient Hebrew Poetry Teaches About Modern Productivity"

**Subject line:** the 3,000-year-old productivity technique hiding in the psalms
**Preview text:** selah appears 71 times. it's not decoration.

---

the book of psalms contains a word that translators don't know what to do with.

it shows up 71 times. sometimes mid-sentence. sometimes at the end of a stanza. it interrupts the flow of the poem without explanation.

the word is "selah."

nobody is 100% certain what it means. the most common scholarly interpretation is that it's a musical or liturgical direction. something like: "pause here." or "let this resonate." or "interlude."

the psalm writer is mid-thought, pouring out grief or praise or anger, and then: selah. stop. breathe. let the last line settle before moving to the next one.

i've been thinking about this word for months. not as a theological concept. as a productivity principle.

**the problem with modern work is that nothing settles.**

we finish a meeting and immediately open slack. we ship a feature and immediately check metrics. we read an insight and immediately screenshot it for later (and never return to it).

nothing sits. nothing marinates. nothing completes its arc before the next thing starts.

we're not processing. we're accumulating.

**selah is the opposite of that.**

selah says: you just read something important. don't move on yet. let it finish landing.

in music, a rest is not silence. it's a note you don't play that gives the notes around it their shape. remove the rests and the music becomes noise.

our days have become noise. not because we're doing too much, but because we've removed all the rests.

**what selah looks like in practice.**

i've been experimenting with inserting deliberate pauses - selah moments - into my workday. here's what i've tried:

after completing a task, i sit for 60 seconds before starting the next one. no phone. no switching tabs. just 60 seconds of processing what i just did. what worked. what didn't. what i'd do differently.

the result: i catch errors i would have carried forward. i notice improvements i would have missed. i enter the next task with clarity instead of momentum.

between meetings, i take 2 minutes of silence. not "2 minutes to check email." 2 minutes of nothing. eyes closed. letting the previous conversation finish reverberating.

the result: i remember more from meetings. my follow-up actions are clearer. i don't carry the emotional residue of one conversation into the next.

after reading something meaningful - a passage, an article, a message from someone - i close the book or close the tab and sit with what i just read.

the result: i actually absorb things instead of consuming them. the difference between reading and absorbing is the pause after.

before prayer, i sit for 30 seconds. not praying yet. just transitioning. letting the work noise dissipate so i can enter the prayer present.

the result: my prayers feel like conversations instead of checklists.

**the science supports this, but the ancients didn't need the science.**

neuroscience calls it "consolidation." the brain needs idle time to move information from working memory to long-term memory. without pauses, learning degrades. creativity drops. insight requires incubation, and incubation requires doing nothing.

but the psalm writer didn't know about consolidation. they knew about the human heart. and the human heart needs pauses to feel things fully, process experiences completely, and hear the still small voice that only speaks in silence.

**the modern case for selah.**

i am not suggesting you become a monk. i'm not even suggesting you pray (though i think you should).

i'm suggesting that the simple act of pausing - deliberately, consistently, multiple times a day - will change the quality of your work, your relationships, and your inner life.

not because pausing is productive (though it is). because pausing is human. and we've been running from our humanity by filling every gap with noise.

the psalm writer knew. 71 times they wrote it into the text. 71 interruptions that say: this matters too much to rush past.

selah.
