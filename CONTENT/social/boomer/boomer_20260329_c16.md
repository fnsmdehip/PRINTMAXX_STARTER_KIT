```
Something worth thinking about if you've been spending money on health supplements:

Most of what they sell was free 30 years ago.

1. Walking 30 minutes a day (free)
2. Getting 7-8 hours of actual sleep, even if that means turning off the TV earlier
3. Cutting alcohol to 2-3 drinks a week instead of daily

Before adding anything to your supplement stack, try those three for 60 days and see what changes.
```

## YOUTUBE LONGFORM SCRIPT — "5 Ways to Know If a Website Is Trustworthy Before You Buy Anything"

**Format:** Educational talking-head or voiceover style | 8-12 minutes
**Target:** Males 55-70 who shop online but are cautious about scams and unreliable vendors
**Tone:** Calm, expert, like a knowledgeable friend explaining something at the kitchen table

---

**[INTRO — 0:00-1:00]**

If you've been shopping online for any amount of time, you've probably run into this situation: you find a product that looks exactly right, the price seems fair, but something about the website just doesn't feel quite right.

Maybe you can't put your finger on what it is. You go ahead and order. And then you're waiting. And waiting. And either the product shows up but it's nothing like the picture, or it doesn't show up at all.

Today I want to give you five specific things to check before you buy anything from a website you haven't bought from before. These aren't complicated. You can do all of them in about two minutes. And they'll save you a lot of frustration.

I've been using these checks for about three years now and I can't remember the last time an online purchase disappointed me.

Let's get into it.

---

**[POINT 1 — 1:00-2:30]**

**Check #1: Does the website show real people?**

The first thing I look for is whether there are real photographs of real people connected to this business.

Stock photos — those professional-looking images with perfect-looking models — are the first warning sign. They're cheap to license and they tell you nothing about the people actually running the business.

What I want to see: photos of actual employees, a real person's name attached to a "about us" page, or genuine customer photos that look like they were taken on a regular phone.

It sounds simple, but I've found it's one of the most reliable signals. A business that's been operating for years and has real customers will have real photos somewhere on their site. A business that launched last month with no history and no real people visible is higher risk.

---

**[POINT 2 — 2:30-4:00]**

**Check #2: How long has the website existed?**

This one takes about 10 seconds.

Go to a site called "Whois" — just search for "whois lookup" in your browser and use any of the free tools that come up.

Type in the website address. It'll tell you when the domain was registered — meaning, when the website was first created.

If the website has been around for less than a year, be more cautious. Not all new businesses are bad. But websites that are specifically set up to take your money and disappear often get created quickly, run for a few months, then vanish.

A website that's been operating for 5, 8, 10 years has some track record. That matters.

---

**[POINT 3 — 4:00-5:30]**

**Check #3: Can you find real reviews somewhere outside their own website?**

Every website has glowing testimonials. That doesn't mean anything — you can write your own testimonials.

What I look for is reviews on platforms the business doesn't control.

Three places worth checking:
- Google reviews (search the business name + "reviews")
- Better Business Bureau (bbb.org)
- Trustpilot

If you can't find the business on any of these, that's worth noting. If you find reviews and most of them are negative, that tells you a lot.

One thing to watch for: a lot of five-star reviews posted on the same day or within the same week. That's often a purchased review burst. Real businesses accumulate reviews over time, not all at once.

---

**[POINT 4 — 5:30-7:00]**

**Check #4: Is there a real return policy and real contact information?**

This one catches a lot of scam sites.

Find the contact page. Is there a physical address? A phone number that actually works? An email address that looks like a real business email rather than a Gmail account?

Then find the return policy. Is it specific? Does it say how many days you have to return something, who pays for shipping, and how refunds are processed?

Vague return policies — "returns accepted on a case-by-case basis" or no return policy at all — are major warning signs.

Legitimate businesses want you to be able to contact them and return products if something goes wrong. They're not hiding from you.

---

**[POINT 5 — 7:00-8:30]**

**Check #5: Does the price make sense?**

I've learned to be suspicious of prices that seem too good.

If a product normally sells for $150 at major retailers and this website is selling it for $45, that gap needs an explanation.

Sometimes there is one — closeout stock, direct from manufacturer, a sale. But if the site doesn't explain why the price is so much lower, the most likely explanations are: it's a counterfeit, it's a bait-and-switch, or the product doesn't actually exist and they'll take your money and disappear.

Compare the price against Amazon, Walmart, or other retailers you already trust. If the discount is more than 40-50% with no explanation, be careful.

---

**[OUTRO — 8:30-9:30]**

So those are the five checks:

One — do they show real people?
Two — how old is the website?
Three — can you find real outside reviews?
Four — is there real contact information and a clear return policy?
Five — does the price make sense?

None of these take more than a few minutes. Taken together, they'll filter out most of the unreliable vendors you'd otherwise run into.

If a website passes all five, I'd feel comfortable buying from them. If it fails two or more, I'd look elsewhere.

I hope this was useful. If you have questions or want me to look at a specific website, leave a comment below and I'll take a look. And if you know someone who shops online and might appreciate this, feel free to share it.

Thanks for watching.
