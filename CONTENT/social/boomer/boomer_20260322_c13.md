## YOUTUBE LONGFORM SCRIPT — "Why Local Business Websites Lose Customers (And How to Fix It in a Weekend)"

**Target length:** 8-12 minutes
**Tone:** Calm authority, practical, educational, no zoomer energy
**Hook style:** Problem-first, relatable scenario

---

**[INTRO — 0:00 to 0:45]**

If you've been running a local business for more than five years, you probably built your website back when it was enough to just have one.

A phone number. Your address. Maybe a photo of the storefront or the team.

That was fine in 2012. It's not fine now. And I don't say that to be harsh — I say it because the number one reason potential customers don't call is that they looked at the website, made a decision in about three seconds, and moved on.

Today I want to walk you through what those three seconds actually look at, and what you can do about it without hiring a web agency or spending a fortune.

---

**[SECTION 1 — The Three-Second Test — 0:45 to 2:30]**

When someone lands on your website from a Google search, they're making one decision: does this look like a place I can trust?

They're not reading. They're scanning. Here's what they actually see in those first three seconds:

First — does the page load quickly? If it takes more than three seconds on a phone, 53% of people leave. That's a Google stat, not mine.

Second — does it look like someone still runs this business? An outdated design, a copyright date from five years ago, photos that look like they were taken with a flip phone — these are signals. Not necessarily fair signals, but they're what people use.

Third — can they find the phone number and what you do in five seconds or less? Most visitors aren't going to scroll. They're going to look at what's above the fold — what's visible without scrolling — and make the call from there.

---

**[SECTION 2 — What a Website Audit Actually Shows — 2:30 to 4:30]**

There are free tools that score your website like a home inspector scores a house. I'll link one below. You type in your URL and it gives you a report.

The things it typically measures: page load speed, mobile compatibility, whether your contact information is easy to find, whether your site is secure (the little padlock in the browser bar), and whether Google can actually find it in search results.

Most local business websites I've seen scored on these tools come back with three or four fixable issues that, once addressed, meaningfully change how the site performs.

The most common ones:

Images that are too large and slow everything down. This is a five-minute fix with a free tool.

A phone number that isn't clickable on mobile. When someone on their phone wants to call you, they should be able to tap the number directly. If they have to copy and paste it, many won't.

No Google Business Profile linked. This is completely free and dramatically affects whether you show up in local search results.

---

**[SECTION 3 — What to Actually Do — 4:30 to 7:00]**

Here's a practical sequence that doesn't require hiring anyone:

Step one. Run the audit. Use Google PageSpeed Insights — it's free, it's from Google, and it's what Google actually uses. Type in your URL and run it.

Step two. Write down the top three issues it identifies. Don't try to fix everything at once.

Step three. For image issues — which is usually the biggest one — there's a free tool called Squoosh, also from Google, where you can compress images without losing visible quality. This alone can cut load times in half.

Step four. Make sure your phone number and address are in plain text on the page — not just an image — so Google can read them and so someone on a phone can tap to call.

Step five. If you haven't claimed your Google Business Profile, do that this week. It's free. It shows your hours, photos, reviews, and phone number directly in Google search results before someone even clicks through to your site.

---

**[SECTION 4 — When to Hire Someone — 7:00 to 9:00]**

If after the audit you're looking at a site that's more than seven years old and would need significant rebuilding, it's worth getting a quote from a local web developer.

The range for a simple local business site — five to eight pages, mobile-optimized, clear contact information, Google integrated — is typically $800 to $2,500 depending on where you are.

That's a one-time cost. And the return on it, if it converts even one or two additional customers per month who would have otherwise bounced, pays for itself quickly.

The thing to ask any developer: will this work correctly on mobile, will it load in under three seconds, and will you set up my Google Business Profile? If they can't answer yes to all three, keep looking.

---

**[OUTRO — 9:00 to end]**

Your website isn't just a business card anymore. It's doing work while you're not — answering questions, building or eroding trust, and deciding whether someone picks up the phone.

The good news is that most of the issues are fixable. The audit takes ten minutes. Several of the fixes take an afternoon.

If you found this useful, subscribe — I cover practical tools and strategies for running a business without overcomplicating things.

And if you ran the audit on your site and found something you're not sure how to fix, drop it in the comments. Happy to help think through it.
