## FACEBOOK POST 1 — Health Vertical
**Topic:** Blood pressure, stress, and the health habits that actually move the needle at 60+

---

Something most doctors don't explain clearly enough:

Chronic stress — the low-grade, every-day kind — is harder on your cardiovascular system than most dietary choices.

I've been reading through the research on cortisol and blood pressure in men over 55. What stood out was a finding from the Framingham Heart Study: men who reported consistently high work-related stress had a 40% higher 10-year risk of cardiovascular event compared to men with similar cholesterol and blood pressure readings but lower stress scores.

The cholesterol number gets all the attention. The stress number rarely comes up in a 15-minute annual checkup.

Three things the research consistently supports for men in our age range:

1. **Walking 30 minutes a day** reduces cortisol levels more effectively than most supplements marketed for "stress relief." No special equipment. No gym membership. The research on this goes back to the 1970s and has been confirmed repeatedly.

2. **Sleep quality over quantity.** REM sleep is when your brain clears cortisol from the previous day. Men over 55 often get adequate hours but poor REM quality. The practical fix is consistency — same bedtime, cooler room, no screens for 60 minutes before sleep. Basic, but the data on this is solid.

3. **Social connection is not soft advice.** The Harvard Study of Adult Development — 85 years of longitudinal data — found that relationship quality predicted physical health in late life better than cholesterol, blood pressure, or exercise levels combined. Not the quantity of relationships. The quality.

None of this is complicated. Most of it gets buried under supplement advertising and fitness trends aimed at younger demographics.

Worth knowing what the actual research says.

## FACEBOOK POST 2 — Golf Vertical
**Topic:** The one swing adjustment most golfers over 60 skip (and why it costs them 15+ yards)

---

I've been playing golf for 30 years. In the last five, the biggest improvement to my ball striking didn't come from a new driver or a lesson on my backswing.

It came from fixing my setup.

Specifically: hip rotation at address.

Most golfers over 60 lose significant distance not from loss of strength, but from reduced hip mobility that quietly creeps in over the years. Your body compensates by over-rotating the shoulders and under-rotating the hips — which produces a steeper swing path, more spin, and shorter, higher shots.

The fix is straightforward but takes deliberate practice:

**The adjustment:**
At address, feel like you're sitting slightly back on your heels rather than balanced on the balls of your feet. This naturally opens up space for your hips to clear through impact. Most instructors call this "sitting into your base."

From there, focus the first move of your downswing on your left hip rotating toward the target (right hip for left-handers) rather than on your hands or the club. Let the arms follow the hips.

It feels slow. It isn't. Slow hip turn with a connected follow-through produces more club speed through the impact zone than a fast, arms-only swing.

I tested this on my home course over six rounds. Average drive went from 215 yards to 228 yards. More importantly, the ball flight got lower and the misses became more predictable.

---

If you're playing in the 60s and above and have noticed distance dropping off in the last few years — try this for one range session before you spend money on a new shaft. The physics works before the equipment upgrade.
