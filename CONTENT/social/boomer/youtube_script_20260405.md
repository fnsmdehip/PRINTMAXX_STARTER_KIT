### [SECTION 1 — Heart Rate Variability, 0:45–2:30]

**[Screen or graphic: simple heart rhythm illustration]**

The first one is heart rate variability. Most people haven't heard of it.

Your heart doesn't beat at perfectly even intervals. Even if you have a resting heart rate of 60 beats per minute, there's natural variation between each beat. Sometimes it's 0.9 seconds, sometimes 1.1 seconds.

That variation — heart rate variability, or HRV — turns out to be one of the strongest predictors of cardiovascular health and nervous system function we've found.

High HRV in men over 60 is associated with lower risk of cardiac events, better recovery from physical stress, and better cognitive performance. Low HRV is an early warning signal — often showing up months or years before a clinical problem becomes detectable.

The good news: this is now trackable for free with a smartwatch. If you have an Apple Watch Series 6 or newer, it measures HRV during sleep automatically. Same with newer Garmin models.

A healthy HRV for a man in his 60s is generally between 25 and 65 milliseconds, depending on your fitness baseline. The number matters less than the trend. You want yours stable or improving over time.

The practical levers that raise HRV consistently in men our age: regular walking, consistent sleep schedule, reduced alcohol (alcohol suppresses HRV measurably the night you drink), and lower chronic stress loads.

### [SECTION 2 — Grip Strength, 2:30–4:15]

**[On camera]**

This one surprises most men the first time they hear it.

Grip strength is one of the most reliable predictors of all-cause mortality in men over 60. More reliable than BMI. More predictive than standard fitness tests in several studies.

A 2015 study published in The Lancet tracked 140,000 adults across 17 countries and found that a decrease in grip strength of 5 kilograms was associated with a 17% increase in cardiovascular mortality.

Why? Grip strength is a proxy for overall muscle quality. It reflects sarcopenia — the age-related loss of muscle mass — which accelerates after 60 and is associated with falls, metabolic dysfunction, and reduced immune response.

You can test yourself right now. Get a handgrip dynamometer — they cost about $20 on Amazon. Squeeze three times with your dominant hand and take the average. For men in their 60s, a score above 35 kilograms puts you in a healthy range. Below 30 is a signal to address.

The fix is resistance training. Not marathon running, not 45-minute cardio sessions. Lifting weights — or bodyweight movements that create resistance — done two to three times per week. Your muscles respond to resistance training at any age. The research on this is very clear.

### [SECTION 3 — Resting Heart Rate Trend, 4:15–5:30]

**[Screen or graphic: downward trend line]**

This one you probably already know, but the nuance matters.

A resting heart rate below 70 beats per minute is generally considered healthy for men in our age range. Elite athletes can be in the 40s. Most healthy sedentary adults are in the low 70s.

The important part isn't the number on any given day. It's the trend over months.

If your resting heart rate has been consistently 65 for the past two years and it's now 72 — even if 72 is technically "normal" — that upward shift is a signal worth paying attention to. It can indicate dehydration, oncoming illness, elevated stress, or cardiovascular changes.

Track this weekly, not daily. Daily variation is normal and noisy. Monthly trend is meaningful.

### [SECTION 4 — Sleep Depth, Not Just Hours, 5:30–7:00]

**[On camera]**

Most men over 60 have been told they need 7 to 9 hours of sleep. That's true. But the research suggests that *quality* of sleep matters more than quantity — particularly REM sleep and slow-wave sleep.

Slow-wave sleep is when your body does most of its physical repair. Human growth hormone is released almost exclusively during this stage. Testosterone is partially regulated by it. Your immune system does cleanup work during slow-wave sleep.

REM sleep is when your brain processes cortisol from the previous day. Poor REM quality is associated with elevated cortisol, impaired memory consolidation, and higher cardiovascular risk over time.

Men over 60 naturally get less slow-wave sleep than younger men. This is normal. But several things reliably make it worse: alcohol (even moderate drinking reduces slow-wave sleep significantly), inconsistent sleep timing, and sleeping in a room that's too warm.

The practical target: consistent bedtime within a 30-minute window, room temperature below 68 degrees Fahrenheit, no alcohol within three hours of sleep, and no screens for 45 minutes before bed.

Not exciting advice. But the research behind each of those recommendations is solid and replicated.

### [SECTION 5 — The One Blood Test Most Men Don't Get, 7:00–8:30]

**[On camera, leaning forward slightly — signal this matters]**

If you're over 60 and your doctor hasn't ordered an Lp(a) test — lipoprotein a — it's worth asking for.

LDL cholesterol gets almost all the attention. But Lp(a) is a separate lipoprotein that's almost entirely genetically determined and is a significant independent risk factor for cardiovascular disease in men over 60.

About 20 percent of the population has elevated Lp(a). Most of them don't know it. It doesn't respond to diet or statin therapy the way LDL does. It requires its own conversation about risk management.

The test costs about $30 out of pocket if your insurance doesn't cover it. It's a one-time test for most people because the number is largely stable throughout your life.

If you're doing everything "right" — diet, exercise, weight, blood pressure — but still have a family history of heart disease, Lp(a) is worth knowing about.
