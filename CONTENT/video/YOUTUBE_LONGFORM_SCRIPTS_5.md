## SCRIPT 002
**Title: "The cold email system that made me $73,000 last year (full breakdown)"**
**Target length: 10-12 minutes**

---

[HOOK - 0:00-0:30]

Last year I sent two thousand eight hundred and forty-seven cold emails.

Revenue directly attributed to those emails: seventy-three thousand dollars.

That's twenty-five dollars and sixty cents per email sent.

In this video I'm going to break down the exact system. The subject lines. The body copy. The follow-up sequences. The tools.

If you've tried cold email and it didn't work, something in here is probably why.

[THE PROBLEM WITH MOST COLD EMAIL - 0:30-2:00]

The reason most cold email doesn't work is simple.

People write about themselves.

"Hi, I'm John. I help companies with X. I've been doing this for Y years. We've worked with clients like Z. Would love to chat."

Nobody cares about John.

John's potential client is thinking about their own problems. Not John.

The fix is completely reorienting the email.

Start with what you know about them.

Not fake flattery. Specific knowledge.

"Saw you just posted a job for a head of sales" is specific.

"I love your company" is not.

The first sentence of every cold email I send proves I spent time on their business.

[THE FRAMEWORK - 2:00-4:30]

Here's the five-sentence framework that generates a twenty-three percent reply rate.

Sentence one: "Saw you're doing [specific thing from their LinkedIn/website/recent announcement]."

Sentence two: "I helped [similar company] add [dollar amount or metric] in [timeframe] by [specific method]."

Sentence three: "Same approach works for [their company/situation]."

Sentence four: "Worth twenty minutes?"

Sign off.

That's the whole email.

Why each sentence matters.

Sentence one proves research. It also makes them feel seen. People respond to feeling understood.

Sentence two delivers proof without a case study they won't read. The result is in the email itself.

Sentence three makes the connection explicit. You're not saying "hire me." You're saying "this already worked for someone like you."

Sentence four is the lowest friction ask possible. Not "can we schedule a one hour discovery call" — that feels like homework. Twenty minutes feels easy.

[THE SUBJECT LINE - 4:30-5:30]

Subject line: their first name, comma, "quick question."

That's it.

I tested twenty-two subject lines. This one beats everything else on open rate.

Why? It's personal. It implies brevity. It triggers curiosity without being clickbaity.

The subject lines that failed:
- Anything with emojis: filtered out or looks spammy
- "Partnership opportunity" — screams spam
- Clever puns — people don't open what they don't understand
- Long descriptive subjects — truncated on mobile

First name, comma, quick question. Don't overthink it.

[THE RESEARCH PROCESS - 5:30-7:00]

The research for each prospect takes four minutes.

Four minutes is the number. I timed it.

In four minutes I can find:
- Something they posted on LinkedIn in the last thirty days
- A recent product launch or company announcement
- A job posting that reveals a pain point they're trying to solve
- A podcast episode they appeared on

Any of these becomes the "[specific thing]" in sentence one.

The job posting angle is underused.

If a company is hiring a head of sales, they're experiencing a sales problem. Maybe I can help with lead generation while they hire. That's a very specific, timely reason to reach out.

The job posting tells you exactly what they're struggling with. It's the most honest signal a company sends.

[THE TOOLS - 7:00-8:30]

The full tech stack:

Apollo dot io: forty-nine dollars a month. Finds email addresses for anyone on LinkedIn. Also has a CRM built in. I build my lead lists here.

Instantly dot ai: thirty-seven dollars a month. Sends the sequences. Tracks opens, clicks, replies. Handles follow-up timing automatically.

Clay dot com: one hundred forty-nine dollars a month. This is where I do the enrichment. It pulls data from LinkedIn, company websites, job postings, and populates it into my lead sheet. Cuts my research time from twenty minutes per lead to four minutes.

Total: two hundred thirty-five dollars a month.

At my average deal size of twelve thousand dollars and a three percent call to close rate, I need to book one call per month to break even on the stack.

I book three to five calls per month.

ROI is obvious.

[THE FOLLOW-UP - 8:30-9:30]

The follow-up sequence that doesn't make people angry:

Day one: original email.

Day four: one sentence reply to my own email. Something like "Wanted to make sure this didn't get buried. Happy to share the case study from [similar company] if that's helpful."

Day ten: final touch. "Not sure if the timing is right — feel free to reach back out if anything changes. Here's that case study anyway." Attach a PDF or link.

That's it. Three touches. Done.

More than three and you're crossing into spam behavior. The relationship is worth protecting.

[THE RESULTS - 9:30-10:30]

My numbers from last year:

Total emails sent: two thousand eight hundred forty-seven.
Open rate: sixty-eight percent (personalized subject lines do work).
Reply rate: twenty-three percent (industry average is two to five).
Positive reply rate: eleven percent.
Calls booked: one hundred nine.
Deals closed: forty-one.
Average deal value: seventeen hundred and eighty dollars.
Total revenue: seventy-three thousand and eighty dollars.

The numbers I track most: reply rate and call to close rate. Everything else is vanity.

Reply rate tells me if my email is compelling.
Call to close tells me if my offer is strong enough.

[WRAP UP - 10:30-11:30]

If you're starting cold email today: write twenty emails this week. Real research. Four minutes per prospect. The framework I showed you.

Track your open rate and reply rate.

If opens are below fifty percent: fix your subject line and sending domain reputation.

If opens are high but replies are low: fix the email body.

If replies are positive but calls don't close: fix your offer or your sales conversation.

The system tells you where to fix. Trust the data.
