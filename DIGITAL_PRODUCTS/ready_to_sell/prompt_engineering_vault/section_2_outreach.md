# Section 2: Outreach — 40 Production Prompts

## Cold Email Prompts (41-55)

### Prompt 41: Cold Email — First Touch
```
Write a cold email to [PROSPECT TYPE — e.g., "ecommerce store owners doing $1M-$5M/year"].

I sell: [YOUR SERVICE/PRODUCT]
Key result I deliver: [SPECIFIC OUTCOME WITH NUMBERS]
Proof: [CASE STUDY, METRIC, OR SOCIAL PROOF]

Rules:
- Subject line under 6 words
- Email body under 85 words (mobile-friendly)
- First sentence references something specific about THEM (not about me)
- One clear CTA — a question, not a demand
- No "I hope this email finds you well"
- No "I'd love to hop on a quick call"
- No attachments, no links in the first email
```

### Prompt 42: Cold Email — Follow-Up #1 (3 Days Later)
```
Write follow-up email #1 for someone who didn't reply to this cold email:

[PASTE YOUR FIRST EMAIL]

Rules:
- Reply to the same thread (not a new email)
- Under 50 words
- Add one new piece of value (insight, stat, or relevant example)
- Don't reference that they didn't reply
- Don't say "just following up" or "bumping this"
- End with a yes/no question (easy to answer)
```

### Prompt 43: Cold Email — Follow-Up #2 (7 Days Later)
```
Write follow-up email #2. They haven't responded to two emails.

Original offer: [WHAT YOU SELL]
Their industry: [INDUSTRY]

This email should:
- Share a genuinely useful insight about their industry (not generic advice)
- NOT mention your product at all
- Position you as someone who understands their world
- End with: "Thought this might be useful. Happy to share more if relevant."
- Under 60 words
```

### Prompt 44: Cold Email — Breakup Email (14 Days Later)
```
Write a breakup email — the final follow-up.

Rules:
- Under 40 words
- Acknowledge you've reached out a few times
- Make it easy to say no: "Totally fine if the timing isn't right"
- Leave the door open without being desperate
- Include a subtle loss aversion element
- No guilt-tripping
```

### Prompt 45: Cold Email — Social Proof Version
```
Write a cold email that leads with social proof instead of a pitch.

My results:
- Client 1: [NAME/INDUSTRY] — [RESULT]
- Client 2: [NAME/INDUSTRY] — [RESULT]
- Client 3: [NAME/INDUSTRY] — [RESULT]

Prospect's industry: [INDUSTRY]
What I'm offering: [SERVICE]

Structure: "[PROSPECT INDUSTRY] companies like [SIMILAR COMPANY] are getting [RESULT]. Here's how: [ONE SENTENCE]. Worth exploring for [PROSPECT COMPANY]?"
```

### Prompt 46: Cold Email — Personalized at Scale
```
I need to send cold emails to [NUMBER] [PROSPECT TYPE].

Here's what I know about each prospect:
- Company name
- Their website URL
- Their role/title
- One recent post or article they published

Write a cold email TEMPLATE with personalization fields that I can fill in for each prospect. Mark each personalization point with {{FIELD_NAME}}.

The personalization should feel researched, not templated. Each email should look like I spent 10 minutes on it, even though I spent 30 seconds.

Template should be under 80 words with personalization filled in.
```

### Prompt 47: Cold Email — Referral Request
```
Write an email asking a current client to refer me to someone in their network.

My relationship with this client: [BRIEF DESCRIPTION]
Result I achieved for them: [SPECIFIC OUTCOME]
Who I want to be referred to: [IDEAL PROSPECT DESCRIPTION]

Rules:
- Make it easy (suggest exact language they can forward)
- Don't be vague — tell them exactly who to think of
- Offer to write the intro email for them
- Under 100 words
- Don't offer a referral bonus unless I specify one
```

### Prompt 48: Cold Email — Event Follow-Up
```
Write a follow-up email to someone I met at [EVENT NAME].

Context of our conversation: [WHAT YOU TALKED ABOUT — 1-2 sentences]
What I want from them: [SPECIFIC ASK — meeting, intro, feedback, etc.]

Rules:
- Send within 24 hours
- Reference the specific conversation (not generic "great meeting you")
- Include the ONE thing you discussed that connects to your ask
- Propose a specific next step with a date/time
- Under 75 words
```

### Prompt 49: Cold Email — Free Audit / Loom Video
```
Write a cold email offering a free [AUDIT TYPE] to [PROSPECT TYPE].

What I'll audit: [SPECIFIC THING — their website, ad campaigns, email sequences, etc.]
How I'll deliver it: [LOOM VIDEO / PDF REPORT / GOOGLE DOC]
Time it takes me: [HOW LONG]
What they'll learn: [2-3 SPECIFIC INSIGHTS THEY'LL GET]

The email should:
- Lead with what's wrong (a specific observation about THEIR business)
- Offer the audit as a no-strings gift
- NOT mention my paid service at all in this email
- Under 80 words
```

### Prompt 50: Cold Email Subject Lines (Batch)
```
Generate 20 cold email subject lines for [SERVICE] targeting [PROSPECT TYPE].

Split into 4 categories:
1. Question-based (5 lines)
2. Number/result-based (5 lines)
3. Curiosity-based (5 lines)
4. Direct/straightforward (5 lines)

Rules:
- All under 6 words
- No spam trigger words (free, guarantee, act now, limited time)
- No ALL CAPS
- No exclamation marks
- Each should pass the "would I open this?" test
- Include predicted open rate ranking (best to worst within each category)
```

### Prompt 51: Cold Email for Agency Services
```
Write a cold email pitching [SERVICE TYPE] services to [BUSINESS TYPE].

I charge: [PRICING MODEL]
My turnaround: [TIMEFRAME]
My differentiator: [WHAT MAKES ME DIFFERENT FROM THE 500 OTHER PEOPLE OFFERING THIS]

The email must not sound like every other agency pitch. Avoid:
- "We help businesses like yours..."
- "Our team of experts..."
- "We've worked with brands like..."

Instead: Start with their specific problem, state the cost of not fixing it, offer to fix it with a specific scope and timeline. Under 90 words.
```

### Prompt 52: Warm Email — Re-Engage Lost Deal
```
Write an email to re-engage a prospect who went cold [TIMEFRAME] ago.

What happened: [BRIEF CONTEXT — they ghosted, said "not now," budget issue, etc.]
What's changed since then: [NEW RESULT, FEATURE, CASE STUDY, OR MARKET SHIFT]

Rules:
- Don't reference the ghosting negatively
- Lead with what's new (give them a reason to re-engage)
- Make it feel like a fresh conversation, not a guilt trip
- One clear, low-commitment CTA
- Under 70 words
```

### Prompt 53: Partnership Outreach Email
```
Write an email proposing a partnership with [COMPANY/PERSON].

My business: [WHAT I DO]
Their business: [WHAT THEY DO]
Partnership idea: [SPECIFIC PROPOSAL — co-promotion, bundle, affiliate, content collab]
What's in it for them: [SPECIFIC BENEFIT]
What's in it for me: [BE HONEST]

Under 120 words. Lead with the benefit to THEM. Don't oversell. Propose a 15-minute call to explore, not a commitment.
```

### Prompt 54: Cold Email — CEO/Founder Targeting
```
Write a cold email to the CEO/founder of a [COMPANY SIZE] [INDUSTRY] company.

Key insight: CEOs don't read long emails. They skim.

Rules:
- 3 sentences maximum
- Sentence 1: One specific observation about their business
- Sentence 2: The result I can deliver, with a number
- Sentence 3: "Worth a 15-minute call this week?"
- Subject line: Their company name only (highest open rate for exec-level)
```

### Prompt 55: Cold Email A/B Test Framework
```
I'm A/B testing cold emails for [SERVICE] targeting [PROSPECT TYPE].

Write two versions of the same email, changing ONE variable:

Test 1: Tone (Version A: professional / Version B: casual)
Test 2: CTA (Version A: ask for a call / Version B: ask a question)
Test 3: Opening (Version A: compliment / Version B: observation about their business)

For each test, write both versions and predict which will win based on [INDUSTRY] norms. Under 80 words per version.
```

## LinkedIn Prompts (56-65)

### Prompt 56: LinkedIn Connection Request
```
Write 5 LinkedIn connection request messages for [PROSPECT TYPE].

Context: I want to connect because [REASON — genuine, not salesy].

Rules:
- Under 300 characters each (LinkedIn limit)
- No pitch in the connection request
- Reference something specific (their content, company, mutual connection)
- Each should use a different approach: compliment, question, shared interest, value offer, mutual connection
```

### Prompt 57: LinkedIn Post — Thought Leadership
```
Write a LinkedIn post about [TOPIC] that positions me as an expert.

My experience: [RELEVANT BACKGROUND]
Key insight: [THE ONE THING I WANT PEOPLE TO REMEMBER]

Structure:
- Hook line (bold, under 15 words — this shows above the "see more" fold)
- 2-3 short paragraphs (2-3 sentences each)
- Bullet list of 3-5 tactical points
- Closing question to drive comments

Rules:
- No "I'm humbled to announce"
- No corporate jargon
- Use line breaks aggressively (LinkedIn's algorithm rewards dwell time)
- Under 200 words total
```

### Prompt 58: LinkedIn DM — After Connection Accepted
```
Write a LinkedIn DM to send after [PROSPECT TYPE] accepts my connection request.

Rules:
- Thank them briefly (one sentence)
- Provide immediate value (share a relevant resource, insight, or tool)
- Do NOT pitch anything
- Do NOT ask for a call
- End with a genuine question about their work
- Under 60 words
- This is message 1 of 3. The pitch comes in message 3, not here.
```

### Prompt 59: LinkedIn DM — Follow-Up (Value Add)
```
Write LinkedIn DM #2 (sent 5-7 days after message 1).

Context from message 1: [WHAT YOU DISCUSSED]
Their industry: [INDUSTRY]
Something useful I can share: [ARTICLE, TOOL, INSIGHT, CASE STUDY]

This message should:
- Reference our previous exchange naturally
- Share the value-add without expectation
- Build genuine rapport
- Still NOT pitch anything
- Under 50 words
```

### Prompt 60: LinkedIn DM — Soft Pitch
```
Write LinkedIn DM #3 — the soft pitch.

Previous context: [SUMMARY OF MESSAGES 1 AND 2]
What I sell: [SERVICE/PRODUCT]
Why it's relevant to them: [SPECIFIC CONNECTION]

This message should:
- Transition naturally from the relationship built in messages 1-2
- Frame the pitch as "thought of you when..." not "I'd like to sell you..."
- Offer something specific (audit, strategy call, sample)
- Give them an easy out: "If it's not relevant, no worries at all"
- Under 70 words
```

### Prompt 61: LinkedIn Post — Personal Story
```
Write a LinkedIn post sharing a professional lesson I learned.

The story: [WHAT HAPPENED — 2-3 sentences of context]
The lesson: [WHAT I LEARNED]
The takeaway for the reader: [HOW THEY CAN APPLY THIS]

Structure: Start with the failure or challenge (hook), walk through what happened (middle), land the lesson (end). Use short paragraphs. End with "What's a lesson you learned the hard way?" to drive comments.

Under 180 words. No "5 years ago I was broke, now I drive a Lambo" energy.
```

### Prompt 62: LinkedIn Comment Strategy
```
I want to comment on posts from [TARGET ACCOUNTS — industry leaders, potential clients, etc.].

Give me 5 comment templates that add value without being generic:

1. "Agree + add data point" template
2. "Respectful disagreement + alternative perspective" template
3. "Personal experience that supports their point" template
4. "Question that advances the conversation" template
5. "Resource recommendation that's relevant" template

Each template should be 2-3 sentences. Never say "Great post!" or "So true!" as the opening.
```

### Prompt 63: LinkedIn Article Outline
```
Create an outline for a LinkedIn article about [TOPIC].

Target audience: [WHO]
Goal: [ESTABLISH EXPERTISE / GENERATE LEADS / BUILD BRAND]
Length: 800-1200 words

Include:
- A headline that works on LinkedIn (different from blog SEO — optimize for clicks, not keywords)
- 4-6 sections with H2 headers
- Key points for each section
- A CTA at the end that directs readers to [DESIRED ACTION]
```

### Prompt 64: LinkedIn Recommendation Request
```
Write a message asking [PERSON — client, colleague, manager] for a LinkedIn recommendation.

Our relationship: [HOW YOU KNOW THEM]
What I'd like them to highlight: [SPECIFIC SKILLS OR RESULTS]
Why now: [REASON — updating profile, job search, building credibility]

Rules:
- Make it easy for them (suggest 2-3 specific points they could mention)
- Offer to write a draft they can edit
- Under 80 words
- Not awkward
```

```
Rewrite my LinkedIn sections for maximum visibility and conversion.

```

## DM / Other Outreach Prompts (66-80)

### Prompt 66: Twitter DM — Cold
```
Write a Twitter DM to [PROSPECT TYPE] who I've never interacted with.

What I want: [SPECIFIC ASK — feedback, collaboration, interview, etc.]
Why them specifically: [WHAT MAKES THEM THE RIGHT PERSON]
What's in it for them: [BENEFIT]

Rules:
- Under 280 characters (first message should be SHORT)
- No "Hey, big fan of your work" openings
- Lead with what you can give them, not what you want
- One clear question, easy to answer with yes/no
```

### Prompt 67: Twitter DM — Warm (After Engagement)
```
I've been engaging with [PERSON]'s tweets for [TIMEFRAME]. We've had [NUMBER] public interactions. Now I want to DM them.

Our interactions: [BRIEF SUMMARY]
What I want to propose: [COLLABORATION, INTRO, QUESTION, etc.]

Write a DM that:
- References a specific interaction we had
- Transitions naturally to the ask
- Doesn't feel like all the engagement was just a sales funnel
- Under 200 characters for the opening message
```

### Prompt 68: Instagram DM — Collaboration
```
Write an Instagram DM pitching a collaboration to [CREATOR TYPE] with [FOLLOWER COUNT].

My account: [SIZE AND NICHE]
Collab idea: [SPECIFIC PROPOSAL]
What they get: [BENEFIT — exposure, content, product, money]

Rules:
- Under 150 words
- Compliment must be specific (reference a specific post or reel)
- Proposal must be concrete (not "let's collab sometime")
- Include timeline and next step
```

### Prompt 69: Community DM — Value First
```
Write a DM to send to someone in a [SLACK/DISCORD/COMMUNITY] group.

The DM should:
- Reference their specific post
- Offer concrete help (not "let me know if I can help")
- Share a specific resource, answer, or offer to hop on a call
- NOT mention my product or service
- Under 80 words
```

### Prompt 71: Influencer Gifting Email

```
What I'm offering: [FREE PRODUCT + any additional perks]
```

### Prompt 72: Testimonial Request Email
```
Write an email asking a happy customer for a testimonial.

Customer name: [NAME]
What they bought: [PRODUCT/SERVICE]
Result they got: [SPECIFIC OUTCOME]

Include:
- 5 specific questions for them to answer (makes it easy — they don't have to write from scratch)
- Permission request to use their name and company
- Offer to draft something based on their answers that they can approve
- Under 100 words

Questions should extract: problem before, solution experience, specific result, would they recommend, one-line quote.
```

### Prompt 73: Speaker Application
```
Write a speaker application for [CONFERENCE/EVENT NAME].

Event topic: [THEME]
My talk title: [PROPOSED TITLE]
Talk description: [3-5 SENTENCES]
My background: [RELEVANT EXPERIENCE]
Previous talks: [LIST OR "FIRST TIME" — both are fine]

Format the application to match typical CFP (Call for Proposals) requirements:
- Title (under 75 characters)
- Abstract (200-300 words)
- Target audience level (beginner/intermediate/advanced)
- 3-5 learning outcomes
- Speaker bio (100 words, third person)
```

### Prompt 74: Joint Venture Email
```
Write an email proposing a joint venture with [BUSINESS/PERSON].

JV proposal: [SPECIFIC — webinar, bundle, cross-promotion, co-created product]

- Propose a specific, time-bound project (not an open-ended partnership)
```


### Prompt 76: Press/Media Pitch
```
Write a media pitch email for [PUBLICATION/JOURNALIST].

Story angle: [THE NEWS HOOK — why should they care NOW?]
My role in the story: [EXPERT, FOUNDER, DATA SOURCE]
Key stat or data point: [THE NUMBER THAT MAKES THIS NEWSWORTHY]

- Under 100 words
- Lead with the story, not with me
- Include 1 key data point
- Offer to provide: quote, data, interview, exclusive

```

### Prompt 77: Beta User Recruitment
```
Write a message recruiting beta testers for [PRODUCT].

Where I'm posting: [REDDIT / TWITTER / DISCORD / INDIE HACKERS]
What the product does: [ONE SENTENCE]
What beta testers get: [FREE ACCESS, LIFETIME DEAL, INPUT ON FEATURES]
What I need from them: [FEEDBACK FREQUENCY, BUG REPORTS, etc.]
How many I need: [NUMBER]

Adapt the message to the platform's culture. Reddit hates self-promotion — lead with value. Twitter is casual. Indie Hackers wants the backstory.
```

### Prompt 78: Apology/Recovery Email
```
Write an email apologizing for [ISSUE — late delivery, bug, mistake, missed deadline].

What happened: [FACTUAL DESCRIPTION]
Impact on them: [HOW IT AFFECTED THE CUSTOMER]
What I've done to fix it: [SPECIFIC ACTIONS TAKEN]
What I'm offering as compensation: [CREDIT, REFUND, FREE MONTH, etc.]

Rules:
- Own it completely. No "we apologize for any inconvenience" — say what went wrong.
- Don't over-explain or make excuses
- Lead with the fix, not the apology
- Under 100 words
- End with a specific next step
```

### Prompt 79: Upsell Email (Existing Customer)
```
Write an email to existing customers offering [UPSELL PRODUCT/SERVICE].

What they already bought: [CURRENT PRODUCT]
What I'm offering next: [UPSELL]
Price: [PRICE]
Why now: [REASON — new feature, limited offer, natural next step]
Discount for existing customers: [IF ANY]

Rules:
- Reference the value they've already gotten (be specific)
- Frame the upsell as a natural evolution, not a separate purchase
- Include one customer result from someone who upgraded
- One CTA, no pressure
- Under 120 words
```

### Prompt 80: Reactivation Email (Churned Customer)
```
Write an email to a customer who cancelled [TIMEFRAME] ago.

What they were paying: [PRICE]
Why they likely cancelled: [REASON IF KNOWN, or "unknown"]
What's changed since they left: [NEW FEATURES, IMPROVEMENTS, RESULTS]
Win-back offer: [DISCOUNT, FREE MONTH, etc.]

Rules:
- Don't be desperate
- Lead with what's new (give them a reason to come back)
- Acknowledge they left without asking why (unless I want feedback)
- Make reactivating effortless (one-click link)
- Under 90 words
```
