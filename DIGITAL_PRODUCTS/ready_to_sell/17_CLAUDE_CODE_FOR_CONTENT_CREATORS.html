The 3-Tweet Rule

Every build session, research sprint, product launch, or client meeting generates 3 tweets and 1 thread minimum. Non-negotiable. This is Rule 9 in the operating system.

The rule in plain language: You do not finish a work session without 3 drafted tweets in the queue. Not ideas. Not "I should post about this." Drafted, filtered, queued.

The Exact Prompt Template

You just [DESCRIBE WHAT YOU DID]. Write 3 tweets about this for a solopreneur audience.
Rules: No AI vocabulary. No em dashes. No "game-changing." No "dive into."
Each tweet: specific, consequence-first, no more than 3 sentences.
One tweet per line. No preamble.
Why consequence-first? Twitter readers decide in 0.3 seconds whether to keep reading. The consequence of what you learned or built is more interesting than the explanation of it. Start with the result, then explain how.

Stage 2: Draft Generation

The engagement bait converter prompt. Takes any raw finding and returns 3+ formatted posts:

Raw finding: [PASTE THE FINDING OR OBSERVATION]

Convert this into 3 Twitter posts for a [NICHE] audience.
Rules:
- No AI vocabulary (no "delve," "dive into," "seamlessly," "leverage," "game-changing")
- No em dashes
- Consequence first, explanation second
- Specific numbers over vague claims
- Max 3 sentences per tweet
- No hashtags
- No preamble, just the tweets

Format: one tweet per line, blank line between each.
The engagement bait classifier: Before generating content from any finding, run it through this 5-point test. Specific tool or platform named? Verifiable numbers? Executable today with info given? Plausible at stated scale? Real method or recycled wisdom? If 3+ are yes: full content piece. If 1-2: extract the specific kernel first, then write from that.

Stage 3: Voice Editing

This is the only stage that requires you. It takes 5 minutes per batch of 10 drafts. You are looking for:

  • AI tells (em dashes, banned words, passive voice) that the anti-slop filter missed
  • Claims without numbers you can add
  • Hedging you can remove ("it might be worth considering" becomes "do this")
  • The first word of the tweet. If it's "I," rewrite the opening.
  • Anything that sounds like it was written by a tool, not a person with skin in the game

See the Anti-Slop Filter section for the full checklist.

AI-generated content has fingerprints. Readers who use AI themselves spot them in 2 seconds. The anti-slop filter removes those fingerprints before anything goes live.

The No Em Dash Rule

Em dashes (like this one) are the single most reliable AI signal in 2026. Claude and GPT use them at roughly 3-4x the rate of human writers. Any reader who has used AI in the last 12 months will clock them immediately. The rule is simple: never use em dashes in content. Use a period. Or a comma. Or end the sentence and start a new one.

Sentence Structures That Kill Engagement

Passive voice
Before: "Content was generated by the system and distributed across platforms." After: "The system generates and distributes content automatically." Active is shorter, stronger, and sounds like a human wrote it.
FIX: ACTIVE VOICE
Hedged claims
Before: "It might be worth considering whether this approach could potentially improve your results." After: "This will improve your reply rate. Run it for 14 days and measure." Hedging signals either the writer doesn't believe what they're saying, or they're an AI trying to be safe.
FIX: COMMIT
Jargon stacking
Before: "Leverage this comprehensive framework to seamlessly optimize your holistic content strategy." This sentence means nothing. After: "Post 3 times per day. Reply to 10 large accounts. Do this for 90 days." Numbers and actions beat noun phrases every time.
FIX: SPECIFICS

The 5-Question Voice Check

Run every draft through these before queuing:

  1. Would a real person say this out loud without cringing?
  2. Is there a specific number I could add to make this more concrete?
  3. Is there hedging I can remove (might, could, potentially, perhaps)?
  4. Does it lead with the consequence, not the explanation?
  5. Is there any word I would never use in a text message to a friend?

If any question surfaces a problem, fix it before moving on. The voice check takes 60 seconds per tweet when you are fast at it.

What It Punishes

External links in the tweet body
Twitter's algorithm suppresses tweets with outbound links. The standard workaround: post the link in a reply to your own tweet. Or post link-free content and put the URL in the first comment immediately after posting.

The Reply Strategy

10 quality replies per day to large accounts in your niche grows your account faster than 3 original tweets. This is not opinion. It is how the algorithm works. Your reply is shown to the large account's followers. If the reply is good, they check your profile.

The compound effect: 10 replies/day for 90 days = 900 high-visibility touchpoints in your niche. Each one is a tiny ad that costs $0 and targets exactly the audience you want. The accounts you reply to are already doing the targeting work for you.

What a good reply looks like:

  • Adds information the original post didn't include
  • Disagrees with a specific point (politely) using data
  • Shares a directly relevant result from your own experience
  • Asks a specific question that invites the original poster to engage back

What a bad reply looks like: "Great post!" "This." "Agreed." "Love this perspective." These replies hurt you because they signal low-value account.

The Controversy Play

The highest-engagement format: a claim most people disagree with, backed by specific numbers, with an invitation for pushback. The algorithm rewards replies. Controversy generates replies. The math is simple.

Use carefully: The controversy must be genuine. You must actually believe it and have data for it. Manufactured controversy where you don't believe the take damages trust faster than it builds reach. The goal is "provocative but defensible," not "wrong for clicks."

The Local API Poster

Posts directly via Twitter API v2. No third-party tool. No monthly cost. Runs on cron every hour, checks the queue, posts anything due:

# crontab entry (runs every hour)
0 * * * * /usr/bin/python3 /path/to/posting_queue_runner.py >> /path/to/logs/poster.log 2>&1
# posting_queue_runner.py (full version)
import csv, os, sys
from datetime import datetime
import tweepy

QUEUE_PATH = 'CONTENT/social/posting_queue/queue.csv'
LOG_PATH   = 'CONTENT/social/posting_queue/post_log.txt'

def get_client():
    return tweepy.Client(
        consumer_key=os.environ['TWITTER_API_KEY'],
        consumer_secret=os.environ['TWITTER_API_SECRET'],
        access_token=os.environ['TWITTER_ACCESS_TOKEN'],
        access_token_secret=os.environ['TWITTER_ACCESS_SECRET']
    )

def run():
    with open(QUEUE_PATH, 'r') as f:
        rows = list(csv.DictReader(f))

    now = datetime.now()
    posted = False

    for row in rows:
        if row['status'] != 'PENDING':
            continue
        if row['platform'] != 'twitter':
            continue
        scheduled = datetime.fromisoformat(row['post_time'])
        if scheduled > now:
            continue

        try:
            client = get_client()
            client.create_tweet(text=row['content'])
            row['status'] = 'POSTED'
            with open(LOG_PATH, 'a') as log:
                log.write(f"{now.isoformat()} POSTED: {row['content'][:60]}\n")
            posted = True
        except Exception as e:
            row['status'] = 'FAILED'
            with open(LOG_PATH, 'a') as log:
                log.write(f"{now.isoformat()} FAILED: {e}\n")
        break  # one post per cron run

    if posted or any(r['status'] in ('POSTED','FAILED') for r in rows):
        with open(QUEUE_PATH, 'w', newline='') as f:
            writer = csv.DictWriter(f, fieldnames=rows[0].keys())
            writer.writeheader()
            writer.writerows(rows)

if __name__ == '__main__':
    run()
