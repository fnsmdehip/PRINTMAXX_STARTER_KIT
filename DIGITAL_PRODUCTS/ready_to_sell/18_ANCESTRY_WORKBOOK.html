Introduction Before You

How To Use This Workbook

"Every family is a story waiting to be told. This workbook is the vessel that holds it — from the stories your grandparents almost forgot to tell, to the ancient lands your DNA remembers."

This workbook moves in five phases. You don't have to complete them in order, and you won't finish it in one sitting. That's by design. The best family histories are built over years, one conversation at a time.

The Five Phases

1
The Living Map
Your 5-generation family tree — names, dates, places. Start here. This is the skeleton everything else hangs on.
2
Ancestor Profiles
Four dedicated pages for key ancestors — their lives, occupations, stories, and the world they lived in.
3
The Interview Guide
40 questions to ask elderly relatives while you still can. The most time-sensitive section in this book.
4
Heritage Discovery
Map your DNA ethnicities, document regional heritage, and understand what your ancestral lands were like.
5
Story & Legacy
Writing prompts, a family timeline, and space to write the narrative that will outlast you.

A Note on Sources

The most valuable information in this workbook won't come from the internet — it will come from conversations. Birth certificates, old letters, and photographs unlock what databases can't. Use the Research Log (p. 18) to track every source you find.

Phase 3 — The Interview Guide Before You

Questions to Ask Before It's Too Late

"The stories that die with the last person who knew them are not lost through tragedy — they are lost through silence. These questions end the silence."

Set aside two hours with the oldest relatives you can reach. Record the conversation on your phone — you can transcribe later. The goal isn't perfect answers, it's capturing their voice, their hesitations, their laughter.

Before You Begin

  • Phone charged and set to record audio
  • Quiet room, no interruptions
  • Old photos on the table as memory triggers
  • Water and snacks (long conversations need fuel)
  • This workbook open and ready
  • Permission granted to share their stories

Tip: Start with easy, non-threatening questions (childhood, food, school). Build trust before asking about hard topics (wars, losses, family conflicts). The best stories often come after 45 minutes, once they've settled in.

Phase 3 — Interview Questions: Early Life Before You

Childhood & Early Life

"Where were you born, and what do you remember about that place?"
"Describe the house you grew up in. What did it smell like? What sounds do you remember?"
"What did your family eat? Was there a dish that meant home to you?"
"What did your parents do for work? What do you remember about their daily lives?"
"What was the hardest thing about growing up in your family?"
"What was the funniest or strangest thing that ever happened to your family?"
— 7 —
Phase 3 — Interview Questions: Heritage & History Before You

Heritage, History & Migration

"Where did our family originally come from? What country or region? How far back do you know?"
"Do you know why your ancestors left their home country? What were they running from or moving toward?"
"Were any of our relatives affected by major historical events — wars, famines, immigration quotas, political upheaval?"
"Are there any family stories about the 'old country' — traditions, foods, or customs that got passed down?"
"What languages did our ancestors speak? Are there any words or phrases you remember from them?"
"Is there a family secret or a story nobody talks about? (You don't have to answer — but if you're willing...)"
— 8 —
Phase 3 — Interview Questions: Legacy Before You

Values, Wisdom & Legacy

"What is the most important thing your parents or grandparents taught you?"
"What do you most want the younger generations of our family to know or remember?"
"What have you changed your mind about over your lifetime?"
"Is there a decision you made that changed the entire course of your life?"
"If you could send a letter back to your 20-year-old self, what would you say?"
"Is there a family heirloom, object, or photograph that holds a story? Tell me about it."

Research Tools & Checklist Before You

Genealogy Research Checklist

Work through these sources systematically. Each one unlocks a different layer of your family's history.

Documents to Locate

  • Birth certificates (your parents, grandparents)
  • Marriage certificates
  • Death certificates
  • Immigration / naturalization papers
  • Passport records
  • Census records (1900, 1910, 1920, 1930)
  • Military service records
  • Land deeds / property records
  • Church baptism / marriage records
  • Old family photographs (labeled)
  • Letters / diaries / journals
  • School yearbooks
  • Newspaper clippings
  • Social Security applications (SSDI)
  • Wills and estate records

Online Resources

  • Ancestry.com — censuses, vitals, immigration
  • FamilySearch.org — free, global records
  • FindAGrave.com — gravestone photos
  • Fold3.com — military records
  • Newspapers.com — historic newspapers
  • MyHeritage.com — European records
  • Ellis Island Foundation records
  • National Archives (archives.gov)
  • Google Books — old local histories
  • Local library microfilm collections
  • Country-specific archives (Ireland: irishgenealogy.ie)
  • DNA match communication (AncestryDNA)
  • GEDmatch.com — cross-platform DNA
  • Reddit r/genealogy — community help
  • Regional genealogical societies

What Comes Next Before You

Your Next Steps

This workbook is the start, not the end. Here's how to go deeper — and how to turn everything you've gathered into a story your family will keep forever.

Immediate Actions (This Week)

  • Call the oldest living relative and set an interview date
  • Search your family's surname on FamilySearch.org (it's free)
  • Scan or photograph any physical documents you have at home
  • Ask a parent or sibling what they remember about grandparents
  • Order a DNA kit if you haven't yet (AncestryDNA runs sales often)

Longer-Term Research

  • Visit your ancestral region (even virtually via Google Street View)
  • Contact a local genealogical society in your ancestral country
  • Hire a professional genealogist for brick walls (2-3 hours is often enough)
  • Reach out to DNA matches who share more than 50cM
  • Order original documents from the National Archives
