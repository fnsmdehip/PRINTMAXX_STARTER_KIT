THE PROBLEM

Your Platform Dashboard Is Lying to You

Instantly shows "healthy." Smartlead shows "warming up." Your emails land in spam. Here's what none of the dashboards actually check.

Cold email infrastructure has exactly one job: get your message into the inbox. Not the promotions tab. Not spam. The inbox.

Most operators fail this before they write a single word. They set up domains, connect them to Instantly or Smartlead, wait 14 days for warmup, and launch campaigns that quietly land in spam. The platform dashboard shows green. The reply rate shows zero.

SECTION 1 — DNS AUTHENTICATION (Points 1-4)

The Foundation Nobody Reads Twice

DNS authentication is the baseline. If these fail, nothing else matters. Yet 40%+ of operators get these wrong at setup and never recheck them after domain transfers, DNS provider changes, or hosting migrations.

1. SPF Record — Exists and Has No Syntax Error
Your SPF record tells receiving servers which IPs are allowed to send on behalf of your domain. A missing or broken SPF is an instant spam signal. Common mistake: having two SPF records (Gmail creates one automatically when you set up Google Workspace — then you add another for your cold email tool. Two SPF records = neither works).
Check: mxtoolbox.com/spf → enter your domain → confirm exactly ONE SPF record exists with no "permerror."
Fix: Merge all sending sources into one SPF record. Format: v=spf1 include:_spf.google.com include:sendgrid.net ~all
2. DKIM Record — Aligned and Valid
DKIM signs your emails with a cryptographic signature. Without it, your emails fail authentication at Gmail, Outlook, and Yahoo. After connecting your domain to a new ESP (email service provider), DKIM takes 24-72h to propagate — most operators check it at hour 2 and assume it's broken when it's just slow.
Check: mxtoolbox.com/dkim → enter your domain + selector (usually "google" or your ESP's name) → confirm "DKIM Record Found."
Fix: If missing, re-add the DKIM TXT record from your ESP settings. If still failing after 72h, the record has a typo — copy-paste directly from ESP, don't retype.
3. DMARC Record — Present with Quarantine Policy
DMARC tells receiving servers what to do when an email fails SPF or DKIM. Without DMARC, Gmail treats your domain as less trustworthy. "None" policy is better than nothing. "Quarantine" is the sweet spot — shows you care without being so strict that legitimate mail bounces.
Check: mxtoolbox.com/dmarc → confirm record exists.
Minimum acceptable: v=DMARC1; p=quarantine; rua=mailto:dmarc@yourdomain.com
Add monitoring email — the rua address gets weekly reports on who's sending email from your domain (catches spoofing).
4. BIMI Record — Optional but +15% Trust Signal
BIMI (Brand Indicators for Message Identification) displays your logo next to your email in Gmail's inbox. It requires a Verified Mark Certificate at the enterprise level, but the standard version works without that and still sends trust signals to spam filters. Most cold emailers don't implement this. It's a 20-minute setup that most of your competition skips.
Skip if: You don't have a brand logo or this is a throwaway sending domain.
Add if: You're sending from a domain you want associated with a real brand. DNS TXT record at default._bimi.yourdomain.com.

SECTION 2 — DOMAIN REPUTATION (Points 5-8)

What Your Warmup Score Doesn't Show

Warmup tools track engagement metrics. They don't track your domain's actual reputation with Google, Microsoft, and Cisco Talos — the three databases that matter most for inbox placement.

5. Google Postmaster Tools — Domain Reputation Check
Google Postmaster Tools is the only free, direct-from-Google signal about how Gmail perceives your domain. It shows domain reputation (Bad/Low/Medium/High) and spam rate. Most cold emailers never set this up. They find out their domain is "Bad" after sending 1,000 emails into a void.
Setup (10 min, one-time): postmaster.google.com → add domain → verify via DNS TXT record → wait 24-48h for data to appear.
Required status: "Medium" or "High" before launching campaign. "Low" = proceed with caution. "Bad" = pause all sending immediately, 30-day recovery minimum.
6. Microsoft SNDS — Outlook Sender Reputation
40%+ of business email inboxes run on Microsoft Outlook/Exchange. If you're not checking Microsoft SNDS (Smart Network Data Services), you're flying blind on nearly half your potential recipients. Outlook uses different spam signals than Gmail — a domain that passes Google's filters can still fail Outlook's.
Check: sendersupport.olc.protection.outlook.com/snds → register your IP range.
Green status required before sending to Outlook domains. Yellow = monitor. Red = do not send, open Microsoft's mitigation form immediately.
7. Cisco Talos — IP and Domain Reputation
Cisco Talos powers spam filters for 85% of enterprise mail servers. Your domain's reputation here determines whether your email reaches anyone behind a corporate firewall. Warmup tools don't track Talos. You can be fully warmed up in Instantly and blacklisted in Talos simultaneously.
Check: talosintelligence.com/reputation_center → enter your sending domain AND your ESP's sending IP (find in email headers).
Required: "Neutral" or "Good." "Poor" = stop all campaigns, investigate bounce and complaint sources.
8. Blacklist Check — 50+ Lists in 5 Minutes
Blacklists are maintained by hundreds of organizations. Your domain or sending IP might be on one or more of them without any warning from your ESP. Being on even one major blacklist (Spamhaus, SORBS, Barracuda) can reduce inbox placement by 30-60%.
Check (free): mxtoolbox.com/blacklists → run on your sending domain AND your ESP's IP. Check monthly.
If blacklisted: Each blacklist has its own removal request process. Spamhaus removal requires addressing the root cause (bounces, spam complaints) before they'll delist you. Do not spam the removal request form — one submission per 72h maximum.

SECTION 3 — LIST QUALITY (Points 9-11)

The Variable Your ESP Never Audits

Your infrastructure can be perfect and your campaigns can still fail if your list is dirty. Sending to invalid addresses, spam traps, or role-based accounts generates bounce signals and complaint rates that permanently damage domain reputation.

9. Bounce Rate — Must Be Under 2%
Hard bounce rate above 2% is a platform-level spam signal. Instantly and Smartlead will throttle or suspend accounts that exceed this threshold. Most operators don't check bounce rates per-domain — they see the aggregate across all domains and miss that one domain is dragging the average down while others are clean.
Check per-domain, not in aggregate. If any single domain exceeds 2% bounces, pause it immediately. Investigate: were those contacts verified before import?
Verify lists before import with ZeroBounce ($0.008/email) or NeverBounce ($0.003/email). Non-negotiable for purchased or scraped lists.
10. Spam Trap Test — Run Before Every New List
Spam traps are email addresses maintained by blacklist organizations. They never sign up for anything. If they receive your email, it means you scraped or purchased a dirty list. One spam trap hit can get your entire sending domain blacklisted within 48h. There is no appeal process at most blacklists — you just rebuild from a new domain.
Check: Use ZeroBounce's spam trap detection ($0.008/email — same price as bounce verification, run both simultaneously).
Rule of thumb: Any list older than 6 months needs re-verification. Email addresses go invalid at ~22% per year. A list from 12 months ago has potentially 22% invalid addresses.
11. Role-Based Address Filter — Remove Before Sending
Role-based addresses (info@, admin@, support@, sales@, marketing@, team@, hello@) are monitored by multiple people, rarely result in replies, and frequently click "spam" because no one person owns the inbox. They inflate bounce rates and complaint rates without contributing to reply rates.
Filter these before import: info@, admin@, support@, sales@, marketing@, contact@, hello@, team@, noreply@, careers@, jobs@, hr@, billing@, accounts@.
Simple regex filter in your list processing step catches 95% of these.

SECTION 4 — SENDING BEHAVIOR (Points 12-14)

How the Platforms Still Get It Wrong

Even with perfect infrastructure and clean lists, sending behavior patterns trigger spam filters. These are the three that catch experienced operators.

12. Daily Send Volume — Ramp Doesn't End at Day 14
The "14-day warmup" narrative is a simplification that gets people burned. Warmup tools build engagement scores by sending emails between warming accounts. Real inbox placement builds over 60-90 days with real recipients. Day 14 warmup completion means you can start sending — it doesn't mean you can send 200 emails/day from a 14-day-old domain.
Ramp schedule per domain: Days 1-14: warmup only (tool handles this). Days 15-30: max 20 real emails/day. Days 31-45: max 40/day. Days 46-60: max 60/day. Days 61-90: max 100/day. After 90 days: up to 150/day.
Most operators blow past these limits because warmup tool says "complete" and they immediately send 100/day. Domain reputation degrades within 2 weeks.
13. Email Copy — AI-Detection Signal Check
Gmail and Outlook have been training AI-detection models on cold email copy since 2024. Over-polished, grammatically perfect copy now triggers spam signals that poorly-written human copy doesn't. This is counterintuitive but confirmed: casual, slightly imperfect copy outperforms corporate-style copy on deliverability in 2026.
Check your copy for: Em dashes (—), passive voice chains, "I wanted to reach out," "I hope this finds you well," three-sentence value propositions, bullet lists in first emails. These are AI writing markers that spam filters now detect.
Fix: Write like a human who's slightly rushed. Short sentences. One typo per 200 words is fine. No em dashes.
14. Reply-to Address — Matches Sending Domain
When the reply-to address differs from the sending domain, spam filters flag it as a phishing signal. This happens most often when operators set up a sending domain (outreach.acme.com) but have replies go to their primary domain (acme.com). The mismatch is a spam signal regardless of both domains' individual reputations.
Check in your ESP: Confirm reply-to is either identical to or a subdomain of your sending domain.
If you need replies at a different address: Set up email forwarding from the sending domain to your primary inbox — keeps the reply-to consistent while you still receive replies where you want them.
