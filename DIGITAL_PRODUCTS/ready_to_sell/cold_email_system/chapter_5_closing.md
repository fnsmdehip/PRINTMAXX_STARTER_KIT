# Chapter 5: Reply Handling, Call Booking, and Closing

## What You'll Have After This Chapter

A complete system for handling every type of reply, booking discovery calls, running those calls, and sending proposals that close. This is where cold emails become revenue.

## Reply Classification

Every reply falls into one of six categories. Each requires a different response:

### Category 1: Positive Interest

"Sure, send me more info."
"Sounds interesting. Let's chat."
"What does this look like for a company our size?"

**Response (within 2 hours):**

```
{{FIRST_NAME}},

Great to hear from you. Here's a quick overview:

[2-3 BULLET POINTS answering their specific question]

The best way to see if this is a fit is a quick call — usually
takes 15-20 minutes.

Here are a few times that work this week:
- [DAY, TIME RANGE]
- [DAY, TIME RANGE]
- [DAY, TIME RANGE]

Or grab a time that works for you: [CALENDLY/CAL.COM LINK]

{{YOUR_FIRST_NAME}}
```

**Key rules:**
- Respond within 2 hours. Response time is your biggest conversion lever.
- Always suggest 3 specific times AND a scheduling link.
- Don't send a PDF deck or long proposal. They asked for "more info" — give them enough to say yes to a call, not enough to make a decision without talking to you.

### Category 2: Soft Interest

"Can you tell me more about pricing?"
"What results do you typically get?"
"How is this different from [competitor/alternative]?"

**Response:**

```
{{FIRST_NAME}},

Good question. Here's the quick version:

[DIRECT ANSWER TO THEIR QUESTION — 2-3 sentences, not a novel]

For a more detailed breakdown specific to {{COMPANY}}, it's
usually easier to walk through it live. I can share some examples
from [SIMILAR INDUSTRY/COMPANY] on a quick call.

Would [DAY] or [DAY] work for 15 minutes?

{{YOUR_FIRST_NAME}}
```

**Key rule:** Answer their question directly. Don't dodge pricing or hide behind "it depends." If they ask about pricing, give them a range: "For a company like {{COMPANY}}, typical projects run $3K-$8K depending on scope." Then move to the call.

### Category 3: Not the Right Person

"I'm not the person for this. Try reaching out to [NAME]."
"That's handled by our [DEPARTMENT]."

**Response:**

```
{{FIRST_NAME}},

Appreciate you pointing me in the right direction. Would you
mind making a quick intro to [SUGGESTED PERSON]? Even a one-line
email would be huge.

If it's easier, I can draft the intro email and you just
forward it.

Thanks again.

{{YOUR_FIRST_NAME}}
```

Then follow up with the suggested person:

```
Subject: [REFERRING PERSON] suggested I reach out

Hi [NEW CONTACT],

[REFERRING PERSON] on your team pointed me your way — [he/she]
thought you'd be the right person to chat with about
[SPECIFIC TOPIC].

[ONE SENTENCE about what you do and a result].

Worth a quick conversation?

{{YOUR_FIRST_NAME}}
```

**Key rule:** The warm referral email has a 3-5x higher response rate than the original cold email. Don't waste this intro — be concise and specific.

### Category 4: "Not Right Now"

"Thanks for reaching out, but we're not looking at this right now."
"Maybe in Q3."
"Not a priority currently."

**Response:**

```
{{FIRST_NAME}},

Totally understand. I'll reach back out in [THEIR TIMEFRAME — or
Q3 if they said Q3, or 3 months if they were vague].

In the meantime, if anything changes or [TRIGGER EVENT — "you
start planning next quarter's budget," "the team decides to
revisit this"], feel free to reach out.

{{YOUR_FIRST_NAME}}
```

**Key rule:** Set a calendar reminder for the date they mentioned. When you follow up 3 months later, reference this conversation: "We talked in March and you mentioned Q3 might be a better time. Is that still the case?"

"Not now" replies close at 8-15% when you follow up at the right time.

### Category 5: Objections

"We already have someone for this."
"Too expensive."
"We tried this before and it didn't work."

**Responses by objection:**

**"We have someone for this"**
```
{{FIRST_NAME}},

No problem. Out of curiosity — how's that going? Most companies
I talk to that already have [SERVICE TYPE] tell me one of two things:
they're thrilled with the results, or they feel like they're
overpaying for what they're getting.

If you ever want a second opinion on the work, I'm happy to do a
quick review — no pitch, just honest feedback.

{{YOUR_FIRST_NAME}}
```

**"Too expensive"**
```
{{FIRST_NAME}},

I hear you. Can I ask — too expensive compared to what you're
spending now, or compared to what you expected?

Just trying to understand your budget range. Sometimes there's
a smaller-scope version that makes more sense as a starting point.

{{YOUR_FIRST_NAME}}
```

**"Tried this before, didn't work"**
```
{{FIRST_NAME}},

That's fair. Do you mind sharing what went wrong? I ask because
when [SERVICE TYPE] fails, it's usually one of 3 things:

1. [COMMON FAILURE REASON 1]
2. [COMMON FAILURE REASON 2]
3. [COMMON FAILURE REASON 3]

If your experience was different, I'm curious what happened.
Not trying to sell you — genuinely want to understand so I
can avoid those pitfalls with my own clients.

{{YOUR_FIRST_NAME}}
```

### Category 6: Hard No / Unsubscribe

"Not interested, please don't contact me again."
"Remove me from your list."

**Response:** Remove them immediately. Don't reply. Don't try to convince them. Mark them as "Do Not Contact" in your spreadsheet. Continuing to email someone who asked you to stop is both unethical and, depending on jurisdiction, illegal (CAN-SPAM, GDPR).

## Booking the Discovery Call

### Setting Up Free Scheduling

**Cal.com** (free tier): Open-source Calendly alternative. Unlimited booking pages, calendar integration, automatic timezone detection.

1. Create an account at `cal.com`
2. Connect your Google Calendar
3. Create a "Discovery Call" event type:
   - Duration: 20 minutes (not 30 — tighter meetings = less resistance to booking)
   - Buffer: 10 minutes before and after
   - Available hours: 9 AM - 4 PM your timezone, Monday-Thursday
   - Minimum notice: 4 hours (prevents same-day bookings you're not prepared for)
4. Add a pre-call question: "What's the one thing you'd want to solve on this call?"

Include the Cal.com link in every positive reply. The fewer steps between "yes" and booked call, the better.

### Pre-Call Preparation (10 minutes per call)

Before every discovery call:

1. **Re-read the email thread.** Know exactly what they responded to and what interested them.
2. **Visit their website.** Note 2-3 specific things you'd improve. Not generic — specific.
3. **Check their LinkedIn.** Read their recent posts. Know their background.
4. **Prepare your case study.** The one most similar to their situation. Have numbers ready.
5. **Write down your opening question.** Not "tell me about your business." Something specific: "I saw you launched [X] last month — how's that going?"

## Running the Discovery Call

The discovery call has one goal: determine if there's a fit and agree on next steps. You're not pitching on this call. You're diagnosing.

### Call Structure (20 minutes)

**Minutes 1-2: Open**

"Thanks for hopping on, {{FIRST_NAME}}. Before I dive in — you mentioned [REFERENCE THEIR PRE-CALL ANSWER OR EMAIL]. Can you expand on that?"

Let them talk. This isn't your monologue.

**Minutes 3-10: Diagnosis**

Ask these questions in order. Each one builds on the previous:

1. "What's the biggest challenge you're facing with [AREA]?"
2. "How are you handling it right now?"
3. "What's the impact of not solving this?" (Get them to quantify — "We're losing X customers a month" or "It takes us Y hours per week")
4. "What have you tried before? What worked and what didn't?"
5. "If we could fix this, what would success look like for you?"

**Minutes 10-15: Position**

"Based on what you've told me, here's what I'd recommend..."

Share your approach — not a full proposal, but the high-level strategy. Reference your case study: "We did something similar for [COMPANY] and they went from [BEFORE] to [AFTER] in [TIMEFRAME]."

**Minutes 15-18: Scope and Budget**

"For a project like this, typical investment is [RANGE]. Does that align with what you had in mind?"

If they flinch at the price, ask: "What budget were you thinking?" Don't negotiate on the call. Adjust scope, not price.

**Minutes 18-20: Next Steps**

"Here's what I suggest as next steps: I'll send over a proposal by [DATE — within 48 hours]. It'll outline exactly what we'd do, the timeline, and the investment. Sound good?"

Get a verbal commitment to review the proposal by a specific date: "Can you take a look by Friday and let me know where you land?"

## The Proposal Template

Send the proposal within 48 hours of the call. Delays kill deals. Use this structure:

```

Prepared by: {{YOUR_NAME}}, {{YOUR_COMPANY}}
Date: {{DATE}}

---

THE CHALLENGE

[2-3 sentences summarizing what they told you on the call. Use their
exact words. When they see their own language reflected back, they
feel understood.]

THE APPROACH

Phase 1: [WHAT YOU'LL DO FIRST] (Week 1-2)
- [Deliverable]
- [Deliverable]
- [Deliverable]

Phase 2: [WHAT YOU'LL DO NEXT] (Week 3-4)
- [Deliverable]
- [Deliverable]

Phase 3: [FINAL PHASE] (Week 5-6)
- [Deliverable]
- [Deliverable]

EXPECTED OUTCOMES

Based on similar projects (e.g., {{SIMILAR_COMPANY}}):
- [SPECIFIC METRIC]: [EXPECTED IMPROVEMENT]
- [SPECIFIC METRIC]: [EXPECTED IMPROVEMENT]
- [SPECIFIC METRIC]: [EXPECTED IMPROVEMENT]

INVESTMENT

{{YOUR_NAME}}
{{YOUR_EMAIL}}
{{YOUR_PHONE}}
```

### Why Two Options

Always present two options (not three — three creates decision paralysis). The two-option structure:

- **Option A** is your recommended scope (and the one you want them to buy)
- **Option B** is a smaller scope at a lower price

Most people choose Option A because they don't want to feel like they're getting the "cheap" version. But Option B prevents losing the deal entirely over price.

Never present Option B as "basic" or "starter." Frame it as: "This focuses on [CORE DELIVERABLE] first, and we can expand to Phase 2 once we see results."

## After the Proposal

### Follow-Up Timing

- **Day 2** (if no response): "Hey {{FIRST_NAME}}, just checking that the proposal came through. Any questions I can answer?"
- **Day 5**: "Wanted to see where you landed on the proposal. Happy to hop on a quick call to walk through anything."
- **Day 10**: "Following up one more time. If the timing isn't right, I totally understand — just let me know either way so I can plan my schedule."
- **Day 14**: "The proposal I sent over expires today. If you'd like to move forward, let me know and I'll extend it. If not, no worries — happy to revisit in the future."

### Closing Techniques That Aren't Sleazy

**The Assumptive Close:** After they say they like the proposal, say: "Great. I'll send the contract over today. Does a [DATE] start date work for you?"

**The Timeline Close:** "I have availability starting [DATE]. After that, my next opening is [LATER DATE]. If you'd like to start sooner, let me know by [DATE]."

**The Value Reframe:** If they hesitate on price: "Just to put this in context — this project pays for itself once [SPECIFIC OUTCOME] happens. Based on {{SIMILAR_COMPANY}}'s results, that usually happens within [TIMEFRAME]."

## The Closed-Won System

After they sign:

1. **Send a welcome email** within 1 hour. Outline what happens next, who they'll work with, and when the first deliverable is due.
2. **Collect payment** immediately. Invoice same day via Stripe, Wave (free), or PayPal invoice.
3. **Set up the project** in your project management tool (Notion free tier, Trello free, Linear free).
4. **Deliver early** on the first milestone. Under-promise, over-deliver on the first deliverable. This sets the tone for the entire engagement.


Cost: $30/year in domains. $0 in tools. Your time to write and send: ~10 hours per 500 emails with the systems from Chapters 1-4.

The system works. The variable is whether you sit down and send the emails. Everything else is just execution of what's in these five chapters.
